IRGC declares Hormuz formally closed to all vessels; US second-wave strikes on Iran complete; ceasefire framework in acute collapse. Confidence: High.
Threat level
5 / 5 · Crisis
Iran's IRGC issued a formal closure declaration for the Strait of Hormuz on 11 June — closed to all vessels including oil tankers and commercial ships — following the US second-wave strikes on Iranian military surveillance, communications, and air defence sites completed late 10 June (CENTCOM confirmed). The IRGC struck two vessels attempting transit and fired on US military facilities in Kuwait, Bahrain and Jordan; US forces intercepted the Iranian missiles with no confirmed damage to US facilities. CENTCOM counter-stated that the Strait remains open and that commercial ships continue to transit, but AIS data and operator reports indicate near-zero commercial movement. The 60-day MOU framework — already fragile since the Bandar Abbas/Qeshm/Sirik/Jask strikes on Day 103 — has no confirmed path to signing: Iran's FM described US strikes as a "clear violation of sovereignty" and the Houthi campaign in the Red Sea / Gulf of Aden continues, eliminating both chokepoints as viable commercial routes.

Six categories

01

Maritime Security

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • IRGC declared Hormuz formally closed to all vessels including oil tankers and commercial ships on 11 June, citing "insecurity in the region" following US strikes — IRGC Telegram cited by CNN/ANI (Tier 1/2). IRGC struck two vessels attempting to transit.
  • CENTCOM denied closure on 10 Jun and stated commercial ships continue to transit; CENTCOM also confirmed completing self-defence strikes on Iranian surveillance, comms, and air defence sites targeting ships in regional waters (ABC News/CENTCOM Tier 1).
  • Houthis: Norderney (2 missiles, both struck) and MSC Tavvishi (1 struck, 1 intercepted) hit in Gulf of Aden 9 Jun; partner forces downed 1 Houthi UAS 10 Jun (Wilson Center/CENTCOM Tier 1/2). Israel-linked ship ban operative since 8 Jun.

Why it matters: Iran's formal IRGC closure declaration — explicitly including oil tankers — is a structural escalation above the de-facto closure that has been in place since Day 28. The IRGC striking two transiting vessels provides kinetic enforcement. CENTCOM's counter-statement creates an ambiguous operational environment where a single commercial captain cannot safely decide to transit.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only routing is now the only defensible option for all categories of cargo regardless of flag or nationality. War-risk premiums (already 8× pre-crisis) will rise further on the IRGC formal declaration. P&I club coverage review expected within 48h. No reopening before IRGC declaration is rescinded AND CENTCOM/Iran jointly confirm safe-transit conditions.

Sources: IRGC Telegram via CNN (Tier 2); ANI/Tribune India (Tier 3); CENTCOM/ABC News (Tier 1); Wilson Center Houthi timeline (Tier 2); UKMTO advisory (Tier 1)
02

Military Operations

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • US second-wave strikes completed 10 Jun (CENTCOM confirmed via ABC News): precision munitions on Iranian military surveillance capabilities, communication systems, and air defence sites; USS Michael Murphy launched Tomahawk cruise missiles. Trump huddled with NSC ahead of strikes.
  • Iran retaliated: ballistic missiles and drones fired at US military facilities in Bahrain (Fifth Fleet), Kuwait, and Jordan. US intercepted Iranian missiles overnight with no confirmed damage to US facilities (US official via ABC News 10 Jun 23:27 EDT). IRGC claims it struck Strait-related targets.
  • IDF continues Lebanon operations: 3,600+ killed in Lebanon per Lebanese MoH (MS NOW). IRGC clashes with US naval forces in Hormuz area reported (Al Jazeera/Iranian media). Iran declared it targeted a US military vessel in Gulf of Oman for "Hormuz rules violation" — US denied.

Why it matters: This is the second US strike wave in 48h — Trump warned "very strong, very powerful" response and Hegseth announced strikes on "key facilities" in advance. The direct US–Iran military exchange is now a structural feature, not a one-off. IRGC escalation doctrine — validated by the Jun 3 Kuwait airport strike, Jun 6 Qeshm response, Jun 9–10 exchanges — shows a willingness to widen targeting to civilian/commercial nodes.

Supply-chain implication: Each US–Iran direct exchange activates a 48–72h IRGC counter-retaliation window per observed doctrine. The formal Hormuz closure declaration is the current counter-move; the next step in the escalation ladder is a GCC energy terminal strike. Probability floor for IRGC GCC energy terminal outage now 0.55+ for 72h.

Sources: CENTCOM/ABC News (Tier 1); MS NOW liveblog (Tier 3); Britannica latest (Tier 2); Wikipedia 2026 Iran war (Tier 3)

Diplomatic Track

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • MOU framework in acute jeopardy: Iran FM Araghchi publicly called US strikes a "clear violation of sovereignty"; Trump accused Iran of "stalling negotiations" and said Iran would "have to pay the price" (Trading Economics/Reuters); CSIS (Tier 2) assessed diplomacy "at an impasse" as of 10 Jun.
  • A BBC YouTube report (unverified by CENTCOM or Iran FM at 06:00 UTC) cited US-Iran negotiators reaching a Hormuz reopening agreement — placed on watchlist as UNCONFIRMED pending ≥1 Tier 1–2 corroboration. CNN (Tier 2) noted Trump said talks continue at "rapid pace" even as Iran suspended negotiations.
  • Counter-signal: Trump stated on 1 June that agreement reachable "over the next week" and called five regional countries "very concerned" and supportive of the negotiating framework (CNN Tier 2). Pakistan mediation channel nominally intact but silent since Jun 10 exchange.

Why it matters: The 60-day MOU was already "tentative" (Vance) without Trump sign-off; the second US strike wave while negotiations were "tentatively agreed" repeats the Day 103 pattern. Iran's sovereignty framing — validated by IRGC formal closure declaration — signals that any MOU now requires Iran to retreat from a public red line. CSIS nuclear impasse (20-year vs 5-year enrichment freeze, HEU disposition) remains unresolved.

Supply-chain implication: MOU probability capped at ≤8% for 14-day horizon. Diplomatic off-ramp window for Hormuz reopening effectively closed until Iran rescinds formal closure declaration. Plan for Hormuz closed through at least July 2026.

Sources: CSIS (Tier 2); CNN (Tier 2); Trading Economics (Tier 2); Critical Threats/AEI (Tier 2); BBC YouTube (Tier 4 — unconfirmed)

Infrastructure & Energy

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • Brent crude: $89.61–$94.43 range on 11 Jun (Investing.com Tier 2); did NOT break $100 despite US second-wave strikes — market partially pricing in SPR releases (58 mb per CNN) and OPEC+ +188 kb/d July quota. WTI $91.55 (+1.69% on day). 52-week range $58.72–$126.41. US crude inventories -7.2 mb 7th consecutive week (EIA).
  • SW Iran petrochemical outage ongoing (≥3 days): Mahshahr (Karoon hub) + Asaluyeh (South Pars) struck 8 Jun by IDF; NPC damage confirmed; Bushehr deputy governor confirmed production units damaged; widespread power disruptions (Argus Media Tier 2).
  • GCC energy infrastructure: IISS (Tier 2) confirmed sustained hits on Ras Tanura and Yanbu; QatarEnergy, KPC, Bapco all in prior FM. No new confirmed GCC energy terminal strike on 11 Jun at 06:00 UTC — but IRGC formal Hormuz closure declaration elevates tail risk significantly.

Why it matters: Brent's failure to break $100 on second US strike wave reflects market expectations that diplomatic channel is not fully dead — SPR releases and OPEC+ supply serving as damper. But the IRGC formal closure declaration and 7th consecutive week of US inventory draws indicate structural tightening. A GCC energy terminal hit would be the price shock catalyst the market is currently discounting.

Supply-chain implication: Lock Q3–Q4 petrochemical feedstock (naphtha, ethane) forwards immediately; SW Iran petrochemical outage now threatens Q3 fertiliser and plastics supply chains. IEA June OMR (12 Jun) will update October rebalancing call — watch for demand-destruction signals vs supply-risk premiums.

Sources: Investing.com (Tier 2); Trading Economics (Tier 2); Argus Media (Tier 2); IISS online analysis (Tier 2); IRU IRU diesel tracker (Tier 2)

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Red Sea / Gulf of Aden: Norderney struck by 2 Houthis missiles (both hit), MSC Tavvishi struck by 1 (second intercepted) on 9 Jun (Wilson Center/CENTCOM confirmed Tier 1/2). Jun 10: partner forces downed 1 Houthi UAS. Jun 11 Wilson Center entry in progress — no confirmed new vessel strike at 06:00 UTC.
  • Houthi Israel-linked ship ban active since 8 Jun (citing Iran–Israel exchange). Suez Canal throughput ~26 containerships/week vs 80 baseline (gCaptain Tier 3). UNSC Res. 2812 extends Houthi reporting mandate to 15 Jul (13–0–2 vote). Red Sea as Hormuz fallback: structurally inviable.
  • Cape of Good Hope: operational with no new incidents. IRU (Tier 2) data: Cape routing adds ~10–14 days and $1M+ fuel per voyage. EU E6 freight contract rates up ~3%, spot rates up ~6% since war began. Durban/Algeciras/Rotterdam saturation watch ongoing.

Why it matters: Both primary chokepoints are simultaneously closed to commercial shipping — a historically unprecedented supply-chain condition. The Cape route is absorbing all redirected traffic but faces saturation risk in South African ports and at European gateway hubs. No third major routing option exists at commercial scale.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only through Q3 minimum. Raise Red Sea 72h attack base rate to 0.65 following Hormuz closure escalation (Houthi doctrine: escalate with Iran-axis tempo). Factor 10–14 day transit extension and $1M+ fuel cost into all June–August voyage P&L calculations. Monitor Durban and Algeciras port congestion as leading indicator of routing saturation.

Sources: Wilson Center (Tier 2); CENTCOM (Tier 1); UNSC Res. 2812 (Tier 1); gCaptain (Tier 3); IRU (Tier 2)

Outlook & Scenarios

  • Scenario B (Frozen attrition) at 30%: requires Iran to rescind formal closure declaration and US to pause strikes — both politically difficult within 72h given current sovereignty framing and Trump "pay the price" language.
  • Counter-signal for C: Brent did not break $100; Iran has not struck a GCC energy terminal (Day 104 06:00 UTC); Trump maintains talks continue; BBC report of Hormuz agreement unverified but non-zero signal. US intercepted all Iranian missiles on 10 Jun with no damage.

Why it matters: The modal scenario has shifted from B to C for the first time in this tracker's history. The defining observable for Scenario C — direct US–Iran military exchange at Hormuz defence nodes while a draft MOU is simultaneously pending — was activated on Day 103 and confirmed/escalated on Day 104. This is not a false alarm: two separate CENTCOM confirmations, IRGC Telegram closure declaration, and AIS vessel-strike data provide Hard-tier corroboration.

Supply-chain implication: Scenario C means alt-route saturation begins in 2–4 weeks if no diplomatic break occurs; price rationing in refined products likely by late June; long-tenor contracts with Gulf loading provisions now exposed without FM clauses; insurance war-risk premiums re-price across both chokepoints within 48h of formal IRGC declaration.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
