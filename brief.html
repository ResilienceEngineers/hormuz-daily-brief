Threat level
5 / 5 · Crisis
CENTCOM completed its third consecutive night of strikes against Iran on 11 July, hitting 140 targets — bringing the three-day total to over 300 — after IRGC forces attacked the Cyprus-flagged MV GFS Galaxy in the Strait of Hormuz, leaving one crew member missing. Iran's IRGC formally declared the Strait of Hormuz closed and launched retaliatory missile and drone strikes against Bahrain, Qatar, UAE, Kuwait, and Jordan's Prince Hassan Air Base overnight. The Oman track remains the sole active diplomatic channel: Araghchi and Busaidi agreed on 11 July to hold further technical and political talks, with Oman drafting a two-corridor transit proposal, but no ceasefire was achieved and Iran IRGC struck again hours later. Threat level is upgraded to 5/Crisis: active simultaneous kinetic exchanges, formal Hormuz closure declaration, and multi-state Gulf retaliatory wave now constitute the hardest structural escalation since Day 1 of the war.

Six categories

01

Maritime Security

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • IRGC attacked MV GFS Galaxy (Cyprus-flag) in Strait of Hormuz 11 Jul; CENTCOM confirmed: engine fire, significant damage, 1 crew member missing (CENTCOM.mil, Tier 1).
  • UKMTO separately reported crew forced to abandon ship on a container vessel east of Oman near Hormuz (UKMTO / CNN live blog, Tier 1); may be same vessel.
  • Lloyd's List Intelligence: zero vessels above 10,000 DWT have transited Southern Highway with AIS on since 7 Jul; straits.live reports ~34 transits/day vs 88 baseline (Tier 2/3).

Why it matters: The IRGC's 11 Jul attack on GFS Galaxy — which CENTCOM called a "blatant" MOU violation — triggered the third US strike wave. IRGC simultaneously issued a formal Hormuz closure declaration, citing "unauthorized routes." This is no longer a functional restriction: it is an enforced closure with active boarding/attack capability. Lloyd's List zero-AIS-transit finding since 7 Jul confirms commercial shipping has self-evacuated the corridor even before the formal declaration.

Implication: All unescorted transit planning must be suspended immediately. The operative question is whether the Oman two-corridor proposal (W2) can restore a defined safe lane within 72h. Until UKMTO formally marks a corridor as open with escort parameters, Hormuz is operationally closed.

Sources: CENTCOM.mil (Tier 1) · UKMTO / CNN live blog (Tier 1) · Lloyd's List Intelligence via Al Jazeera (Tier 2) · straits.live (Tier 3)
02

Military Operations

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM completed third strike wave 11 Jul: 140 targets including missile/drone sites, naval capabilities, ammo storage, comm networks, coastal surveillance (CENTCOM.mil, Tier 1). Three-night total: 300+ targets.
  • IRGC retaliatory strikes overnight 11–12 Jul: Bahrain (5th Fleet area), Qatar (missile intercepted over Doha), UAE (missiles+drones intercepted), Kuwait (4 missiles + 10 drones; 1 injured), Jordan Prince Hassan AB (10 ballistic missiles) — Arab News, Al Jazeera live blog (Tier 1/2).
  • IRGC issued formal Hormuz closure declaration citing "unauthorized routes" by foreign vessels; US CENTCOM states Iran "again failed" to adhere to MOU (CENTCOM.mil, Tier 1).

Why it matters: The 11 Jul GFS Galaxy attack-to-strike sequence demonstrates an Iran tit-for-tat escalation cycle now operating on a same-day timescale. The IRGC is simultaneously attacking commercial vessels, striking US and Gulf-state military bases across five countries, and issuing formal closure declarations — a three-layer escalation package that is structurally distinct from the tactical probing of prior weeks. CENTCOM's statement that Iran "again failed" the MOU test signals no pause in US strike authorization.

Implication: Scenario C is now modal at 60%. Any new IRGC commercial vessel attack or Gulf-state base hit could trigger Day 4 within hours. The CENTCOM target ceiling has remained military infrastructure; Kharg Island energy infrastructure is the defined next escalatory threshold (W4, EOD 17 Jul).

Sources: CENTCOM.mil (Tier 1) · Al Jazeera live blog (Tier 1) · Arab News (Tier 2) · CNN live blog (Tier 1) · ABC News live blog (Tier 2)
03

Diplomatic Track

Severe → Confidence: Medium
  • Araghchi–Busaidi meeting Muscat 11 Jul: ONA confirmed discussion of "safety and freedom of navigation in light of recent developments"; both agreed to "further technical and political talks" (ONA / CNN, Tier 1).
  • Oman drafted two-corridor Hormuz management proposal: Southern Corridor (free nav); Northern Corridor (Iranian prior approval, no tolls). Yet to be finalized (CNN source, Tier 1).
  • Pakistan PM Sharif worked phones with Pezeshkian and Qatar Emir (MS NOW / Daily Pakistan, Tier 2/3); Qatar mediators arrived in Iran (Reuters cited in prior brief, Tier 1). Counter-signal: IRGC struck five Gulf states within hours of Araghchi–Busaidi talks.

Why it matters: The Oman channel remains the only active US–Iran intermediary mechanism. The two-corridor proposal is operationally significant — if adopted, it would de facto reopen the Southern (Omani territorial water) lane to commercial shipping without requiring Iranian command stand-down across the full strait. However, Iran's IRGC strikes on Gulf states on the same night as the talks signal that the IRGC is not under FM-level diplomatic constraint. This is the core "strike-and-talk" dynamic: Araghchi negotiates while IRGC strikes.

Implication remains a Scenario B–C binary gate. If Oman two-corridor proposal is accepted and CENTCOM pauses strikes (allowing Iran to demonstrate compliance), Scenario B lives. If IRGC attacks another vessel or US strikes resume, the diplomatic channel collapses and Scenario C locks in.

Sources: ONA / Oman Observer (Tier 2) · CNN live blog (Tier 1) · MS NOW / Daily Pakistan (Tier 2/3) · Reuters (Tier 1, cited prior brief)
04

Infrastructure & Energy

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • Brent closed ~$76 (10 Jul, Trading Economics / CNBC Tier 2); Day 3 strikes and formal Hormuz closure declaration expected to push Brent higher on opening; IEA warned prolonged conflict delays global inventory rebuild (IEA, Tier 1).
  • GL-X Iranian oil waiver revoked 7 Jul; new waiver authorisation expires 17 Jul — all Iranian oil transactions cease; Asian refiners in emergency procurement mode (Treasury statement / Al Jazeera, Tier 1).
  • Explosions reported in Bushehr province (including near nuclear plant outskirts) and Qeshm Island during CENTCOM Day 3 strikes; plant status unconfirmed; Russian technicians on site per prior pattern (CNN / Iranian state media, Tier 1/5).

Why it matters: The IEA has already characterised this as the "largest supply disruption in history." With Hormuz formally closed, GL-X expiring 17 Jul, and a third CENTCOM strike wave hitting coastal and naval infrastructure, the market is entering a new phase where insurance, procurement, and inventory depletion interact. UAE crude at record high partially offsets but cannot substitute for Iranian and Qatari volumes.

Implication: Reprice Q3 energy exposure. Base case: Brent $80–88. Tail: $95+ if Kharg struck or Ras Laffan operationally disrupted. Fertilizer supply chain gap for Q3 planting now structurally at risk if Hormuz stays closed beyond 25 Jul.

Sources: IEA (Tier 1) · Trading Economics / CNBC (Tier 2) · US Treasury (Tier 1) · Al Jazeera Economy (Tier 1) · CNN live blog (Tier 1)
05

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: Medium
  • Hormuz: ~34 transits/day vs 88/day baseline (straits.live Tier 3); "effectively closed" per Lloyd's List (Tier 2); IRGC formal closure declaration 11 Jul.
  • Red Sea / GoA: IRGC Quds Force (Qaani, Jun 9) warned of Hormuz-to-Bab-el-Mandeb "security belt"; Houthis declared "complete ban on enemy navigation" (Jun 8); Minervagracht GoA fire unresolved; no confirmed full Houthi commercial shipping resumption as of 06:01 UTC (ICG / MARAD Tier 1).
  • Cape of Good Hope: route saturating — Jebel Ali congestion building; Salalah and Jeddah dwell times elevated; EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extended to Feb 2027; Saudi Yanbu sole secure Red Sea crude export point (France24 / prior brief Tier 2).

Why it matters: With Hormuz closed and Cape route at saturation threshold, the Houthi resumption question (W5) is the single biggest structural risk to the alternative-route calculus. A full Houthi commercial campaign restores the dual-chokepoint closure that existed Feb–Apr 2026 — at which point there is no viable alternative except Cape, which is already congested, and the long Baltic/Arctic routes for European LNG, which have volume limits.

Implication: Supply-chain planners must now model dual-closure scenarios. Houthi resumption probability: elevated (see W5 watchlist, EOD 14 Jul). Stress-test against simultaneous Cape throughput cap: any sustained dual-closure event exceeds current buffer and forces price rationing.

Sources: ICG / Crisis Group (Tier 2) · MARAD advisory (Tier 1) · Lloyd's List via Al Jazeera (Tier 2) · straits.live (Tier 3) · France24 (Tier 2) · Security Council Report (Tier 2)

Outlook & Scenarios

Crisis ↑ Confidence: Medium
  • Mojtaba Khamenei: 11 Jul Qom ceremony passed without confirmed public appearance; Bloomberg 10 Jul: "statement did not specify whether Mojtaba would attend"; CNN 6 Jul: "has yet to appear publicly since his father's killing." IRGC security protocols and reported injuries cited (Bloomberg/CNN Tier 1). IRGC dominant in decision loop — structural escalation risk.
  • Scenario C now modal (60%, ↑15pp): IRGC formal Hormuz closure declaration, 300+ CENTCOM targets in three nights, five-state Gulf retaliation, MOU deemed failed by CENTCOM — meets all structural criteria for re-escalation lock-in without a new ceasefire mechanism.
  • Counter-signal (anti-confirmation-bias required): Oman two-corridor proposal remains on table; Araghchi–Busaidi agreed technical follow-on; Pakistan–Qatar mediation active; Trump said (19 Jul CNN) "truce talks will continue even as US launched strikes" — diplomacy not collapsed, merely overtaken.

Why it matters: The 11 Jul sequence — GFS Galaxy attack → CENTCOM Day 3 → IRGC formal closure → five-state retaliation — is structurally distinct from the June MOU flare-up. The MOU is now operationally suspended even if not formally repudiated. Mojtaba's continued absence means the IRGC is the effective decision-making authority on Hormuz — and the IRGC has demonstrated it will continue vessel attacks regardless of FM-level diplomatic engagement.

Implication: Scenario C at 60% is the planning base case. Scenario B (32%) requires Oman corridor acceptance AND a CENTCOM pause AND IRGC operational compliance — all three simultaneously within the next 72h. Scenario A (8%) requires all of the above plus a public US–Iran joint statement, which neither party has signalled.

Sources: Bloomberg (Tier 2) · CNN live blog (Tier 1) · CENTCOM.mil (Tier 1) · ONA / Oman Observer (Tier 2) · Al Jazeera live blog (Tier 1)

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
