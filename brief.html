Threat level
5 / 5 · Crisis
The Islamabad MOU has entered acute breach on Day 131. Iran's IRGC attacked three commercial vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz on 7 July — the Qatari LNG tanker Al-Rekayyat, the Saudi crude tanker Wedyan, and a third vessel — during the Khamenei funeral window that was expected to provide restraint. CENTCOM responded with 80+ retaliatory strikes targeting Iranian air defences, coastal radar, anti-ship missile sites, and more than 60 IRGC small boats. Iran counter-struck with missiles against US military installations in Bahrain and Kuwait, triggering air-raid sirens across both countries. The US Treasury simultaneously revoked the 60-day oil-sale waiver (effective 17 July), stripping Iran of a key MOU economic benefit. Brent surged above $76/b — reversing the pre-war-level decline — as the post-funeral Doha session that had been the primary diplomatic gate is now in severe doubt. Threat level moves to 5/5 Crisis; Scenario C (re-escalation/MOU collapse) rises 24pp to 40%.

01

Maritime Security

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • IRGC attacked three commercial vessels transiting the southern Omani corridor 7 Jul: Qatari LNG tanker Al-Rekayyat (UKMTO-confirmed, Qatar FM confirmed), Saudi crude tanker Wedyan (Saudi Arabia condemned), and a third unidentified vessel — UKMTO JMIC Advisory 068 issued (UKMTO Tier 1; CNBC/CBS Tier 1; Reuters Tier 1, 7 Jul).
  • CENTCOM struck 80+ targets inside Iran in response: air defences, command and control networks, coastal radar sites, anti-ship missile capabilities, and more than 60 IRGC small boats in and around Hormuz (CENTCOM.mil Tier 1, 7 Jul).
  • IMF PortWatch 5 Jul: 34 commercial transits vs 83/day baseline (41%); war-risk insurance 8× pre-crisis; 6 P&I clubs withdrawn; Al-Rekayyat carried LNG and risked explosion from engine-room fire (Reuters, Tier 1).

Why it matters: Three vessels struck in a single day on the US-endorsed corridor is the most severe Hormuz incident since the MOU signing on 17 June. The IRGC targeted the southern route — which the US, Oman, and JMIC had explicitly promoted as safe — destroying the corridor's operational premise. Both the northern IRGC route and the southern Omani route are now effectively closed. The 60-day MOU (Day 21/60) is in acute breach on its core shipping commitment.

Supply-chain implication: All Hormuz transit planning suspended pending CENTCOM and IRGC de-escalation. No certified safe passage in either route. Cargo owners with time-sensitive Gulf loading must declare force majeure or divert entirely. LNG deliveries from Qatar at direct risk; Al-Rekayyat strike on a QatarEnergy-linked vessel is the most diplomatically explosive single incident of the war.

Sources: UKMTO JMIC Advisory 068 (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · CENTCOM.mil (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · CBS News (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · Reuters (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · IMF PortWatch (Tier 1, 5 Jul)
02

Military

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM strikes 7 Jul hit Sirik, Qeshm Island, Bandar Abbas, and Kharg Island — 13+ explosions reported by IRIB; Iranian state media claimed civilian casualties at Sirik commercial pier; Iranian Khatam-al-Anbiya HQ warned of "crushing response" (Al Jazeera live blog, Tier 1, 8 Jul; CENTCOM.mil, Tier 1).
  • Iran launched retaliatory ballistic missiles targeting "85 US military installations" in Bahrain and Kuwait; air-raid sirens blared in both countries; Kuwait confirmed hostile drone and missile attacks; US CENTCOM said strikes remain "postured and prepared" (Al Jazeera, Tier 1, 8 Jul; CBS News, Tier 1, 8 Jul).
  • Mojtaba Khamenei still absent publicly — Day 8 of 9-day funeral; three other sons appeared Days 1–6; Mashhad burial 9 Jul is the final gate; Iranian military advisor Mohsen Rezaei: "Trump intends to attack again and we are fully prepared" (Fox News / Jerusalem Post, Tier 2, 7 Jul).

Why it matters: The bilateral strike exchange on 7–8 July is the most significant kinetic escalation since the MOU was signed. CENTCOM targeted the IRGC's naval enforcement capacity (60+ small boats, coastal radar, anti-ship missiles) — degrading but not eliminating Iran's ability to contest Hormuz. Iran's counter-strike against Bahrain and Kuwait signals a willingness to regionalise the exchange. Trump authorised the strikes personally from NATO Ankara, indicating presidential-level commitment to consequences for MOU violations.

Supply-chain implication: Gulf-state port operations in Bahrain at direct risk; Kuwait Ali al-Salem airbase — a CENTCOM staging hub — may affect regional logistics if damaged. Manama (Fifth Fleet) hardening underway. Regional carriers and logistics providers should review contingency plans for eastern Gulf port disruption.

Sources: CENTCOM.mil (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · Al Jazeera live blog (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · CBS News (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · The War Zone/TWZ (Tier 2, 7 Jul) · Fox News (Tier 2, 7 Jul)

Diplomatic

Severe ↑ Confidence: Medium
  • US Treasury revoked 60-day waiver authorising Iranian oil sales (eff. 17 July) — a core MOU economic concession stripped in response to 7 Jul vessel attacks; "MOU is entirely performance-based" (US official to CNBC, Tier 1, 7 Jul).
  • Qatar FM had confirmed next session "at earliest possible time after funeral processions" (Qatar FM/X, Tier 1, 2 Jul) — now in severe doubt post-strike; Qatar FM condemning Al-Rekayyat attack may complicate Qatari mediation neutrality.
  • Trump at NATO Ankara: "very disappointed with NATO" on Iran conflict support; called it a "denuclearization operation" not a war; NATO summit Hormuz declaration text status unclear post-strike (CBS News, Tier 1, 7 Jul; Fox News, Tier 2).

Why it matters: The MOU's 60-day clock (Day 21/60, with mutual-consent extension available) is now under acute stress from three directions simultaneously: US waiver revocation strips Iran's primary economic incentive; bilateral military exchanges undermine ceasefire premise; Qatar's neutrality as mediator is complicated by the attack on a Qatari state vessel. Iran's Ghalibaf called MOU implementation "difficult but achievable" on 6 Jul — before the strikes — making his post-strike position the critical signal to watch.

Supply-chain implication: Post-funeral Doha session (P=0.25, ↓30pp) is now the sole near-term diplomatic circuit-breaker. If convened, it buys time for MOU survival. If not convened within 7 days, MOU suspension becomes probable (P=0.35 ↑20pp). Long-tenor contracts with Iranian oil or Hormuz corridor assumptions must be reviewed against MOU suspension risk.

Sources: US Treasury statement (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · CNBC (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · Qatar FM/X (Tier 1, 2 Jul) · CBS News (Tier 1, 7–8 Jul) · Al Jazeera (Tier 1, 1–7 Jul)

Infrastructure & Energy

Elevated ↑ Confidence: High
  • Brent crude surged to $76.07/b (+5.6% overnight 7–8 Jul), reversing the slide to pre-war levels; WTI +5.4% to $72.25; Al Jazeera: "oil prices surge as US strikes Iran, reversing slide to pre-war levels" (Al Jazeera, Tier 1, 8 Jul; CNBC, Tier 1, 7 Jul).
  • EIA July 2026 STEO (released 7 Jul, Tier 1): Brent Q3 2026 forecast $74/b (↓$27 from June STEO of $101/b); June actual averaged $85/b; daily prices fell below $70 on 1 Jul before IRGC attacks reversed the trend (EIA.gov, Tier 1).
  • CENTCOM strikes targeted Sirik and Bandar Abbas port infrastructure and Kharg Island oil terminal — potential damage to Iranian oil loading infrastructure, adding supply-side risk premium on top of corridor closure (CENTCOM.mil, Tier 1; Al Jazeera, Tier 1, 8 Jul).

Why it matters: The pre-war-level price floor ($69–72) that had formed since the MOU has been broken. Bilateral strikes + waiver revocation create a dual upward pressure: sanctions tighten Iranian supply side, while Hormuz corridor closure removes the reopening discount that had suppressed prices. The EIA STEO $74/b Q3 baseline is now under upward revision risk; a sustained MOU breakdown could push Brent back toward $85–95.

Supply-chain implication: Reopen Q3 hedge books immediately; prior $65–85 range needs upward shift to $72–95 given crisis-level escalation. Saudi Aramco's $11/b Asian Arab Light discount signals competitive pressure even in the new price environment — Saudi exports via pipeline and Red Sea (Yanbu) are the swing supply. Monitor OPEC+ emergency session risk if prices surge above $85.

Sources: EIA STEO July 2026 (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · Al Jazeera (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · CNBC (Tier 1, 7 Jul) · Trading Economics (Tier 2, 7 Jul)

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Magic Seas bulk carrier sunk 6 Jul, 30 nm SW Al-Hudaydah — UKMTO-confirmed; skiff with AIS-dark mother ship; no Houthi claim after 48h (unusual); Houthi formal claim P=0.68 by EOD 12 Jul (UKMTO / ABC News / CBS News, Tier 1, 5–6 Jul).
  • EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extended to February 2027; Cape routing mandatory for all major Western carriers; 10–14 day premium entrenched; Red Sea throughput below 50 vessels/day vs ~250/day pre-crisis Suez baseline.
  • IRGC strikes on the southern Omani Hormuz corridor — the only route that had partially supplemented Cape diversion — means Gulf cargo now has no available sea corridor; Oman-adjacent route directly hit by IRGC on 7 Jul.

Why it matters: For the first time since April, both the Red Sea/Bab al-Mandeb and the Hormuz southern corridor are simultaneously contested by kinetic action. The Cape route remains the only functioning global alternative but is approaching saturation. There is no near-term relief valve: even if Hormuz de-escalates rapidly (72h), P&I club and insurance conditions will not clear for weeks.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only routing mandatory — no exceptions. Shippers should pre-book Cape slots now given saturation risk. Asian buyers of Gulf crude (China, India, Japan, South Korea) face simultaneous loss of both primary corridors; inventory draw acceleration imminent if Hormuz remains closed beyond 7 days. Monitor Saudi Yanbu Red Sea loading as the sole remaining Gulf-to-Asia relief route.

Sources: UKMTO (Tier 1, 5–6 Jul) · ABC News (Tier 1, 6 Jul) · MARAD 2026-006 (Tier 1) · EUNAVFOR ASPIDES (Tier 1)

  • Scenario B (Frozen attrition / managed crisis): 42% ↓7pp — modal; both sides have economic incentives to pause; Qatar/Pakistan emergency mediation possible; IRGC may have achieved route-enforcement objective without intent to fully collapse MOU; extension clause available.

Why it matters: The 24pp swing toward Scenario C in a single day is the largest single-day probability shift in this tracker's history. The IRGC action on 7 Jul was not a probe — it was a direct missile attack on vessels already on the US-designated "safe" corridor during the funeral window, suggesting either IRGC command-and-control independence from Pezeshkian/Ghalibaf diplomatic track, or a deliberate signal that the MOU's Hormuz terms are unacceptable. Either interpretation is structurally bearish for the MOU.

Supply-chain implication — by scenario: A (18%): Hormuz partial reopening within 7 days; Brent retreats toward $70; Cape premium eases. B (42%): Frozen at current Crisis level; both routes suspended 2–4 weeks; Brent $75–85; Cape saturation. C (40%): Full MOU collapse; Hormuz hard-closed; Brent $85–100+; alt-route buffers consumed; price rationing in refined products; Asian inventory draw becomes supply emergency.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
