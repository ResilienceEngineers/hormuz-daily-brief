Day 132 opened in full bilateral kinetic exchange: after IRGC struck three commercial vessels at Hormuz on 7 July, CENTCOM launched 80+ precision strikes on Day 131 and a second wave on Day 132, targeting Iranian coastal air defences, radar sites, anti-ship missile batteries, and more than 60 IRGC small boats. Trump declared the ceasefire "over" at the NATO Ankara summit and signalled reimposition of the naval blockade on Iranian ports. Iran retaliated with drone and missile strikes against US bases in Bahrain and Kuwait — air defences intercepted most ordnance; a residential building near Bahrain airport was damaged; no confirmed structural damage to US military facilities. The June 17 MOU's General License X was revoked by US Treasury (effective 7 July), ending authorised Iranian oil sales; Iran's FM threatened a "complete halt" to negotiations if strikes continue. Brent surged 5% to ~$78/b; Hormuz commercial transits — already at 41% of baseline per IMF PortWatch (5 Jul) — face further suppression as at least four additional tankers turned back following the 7 July attacks.

01

Maritime Security — Hormuz

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • IRGC struck 3 vessels 7 Jul (Qatari LNG Al Rekayyat, Saudi crude Wedyan, third tanker) using missiles on Omani-route corridor — UKMTO confirmed tanker hit/fire off Limah (Tier 1); CENTCOM confirmed three vessels attacked (Tier 1).
  • CENTCOM 7 Jul: 80+ targets struck including 60+ IRGC small boats, coastal radar, anti-ship missile sites; 8 Jul second wave: Bandar Abbas, Qeshm, Sirik targeted — "to further degrade Iran's ability to threaten freedom of navigation" (CENTCOM.mil, Tier 1).
  • At least 4 additional tankers (including Indian-flagged VLCC with Kuwaiti crude) made U-turns at Hormuz entrance post-attack (Reuters/Arab News, Tier 1); Trump reimposed naval blockade on Iranian ports (NBC/CBS, Tier 1).

Why it matters: The Omani-route corridor — the primary partial-reopening mechanism of the MOU — has been kinetically contested twice in 72h. IRGC framing of route enforcement as "regulatory" rather than offensive (consistent with heuristic update from Day 131) means this pattern will repeat whenever non-Iranian-authorised vessels attempt transit. The blockade reimposition removes even the theoretical US escort mechanism.

Supply-chain implication: Full cessation of non-Iranian-authorised Hormuz transit. No corridor viable <72h. IMF PortWatch baseline of 34/day (5 Jul) will fall further. All Gulf-origin energy and cargo routes must be planned via Cape or Saudi East-West pipeline exclusively. War-risk physical premium $3–6/b over paper.

Sources: UKMTO (Tier 1, 6–7 Jul) · CENTCOM.mil (Tier 1, 7–8 Jul) · Reuters/AP (Tier 1, 7–8 Jul) · NBC News live blog (Tier 1, 9 Jul) · Arab News (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · IMF PortWatch (Tier 1, 5 Jul)
02

Military — Kinetic Exchange

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM Day 1 (7 Jul): "powerful strikes" on 80+ targets — air defences, radar, anti-ship missiles, 60+ IRGC small boats; described by CENTCOM as "immediate response" to "clear and dangerous violation of the ceasefire" (CENTCOM.mil, Tier 1).
  • CENTCOM Day 2 (8 Jul night): second wave ordered by Trump — "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten freedom of navigation"; CENTCOM confirmed completion; no target count published yet (CBS News/CENTCOM X, Tier 1).
  • Iran IRGC retaliation 8 Jul: drone/missile strikes on Bahrain 5th Fleet HQ (Port Salman) and Kuwait Ali Al-Salem Air Base; Bahrain residential building damaged near airport; Kuwait air defences intercepted; MQ-9 drone downed over Bushehr province; IRGC claimed "85 US military sites" targeted — overstated per damage reports (AP/PBS/Arab News, Tier 1).

Why it matters: Two consecutive nights of US offensive strikes represents the most sustained kinetic tempo since the 27–28 June exchange. The IRGC's counter-strike pattern (Bahrain + Kuwait every cycle) is consistent and intercept-heavy — actual damage to US military assets remains low but the escalation ladder is being climbed. Trump's "finish the job" rhetoric + blockade reimposition signals a potential shift from punishment strikes to attrition campaign.

Supply-chain implication: IRGC small-boat fleet partially degraded (60+ boats struck) but not eliminated — Iran retains sufficient asymmetric capacity to interdict Hormuz corridor vessels. US strike focus on coastal infrastructure (Bandar Abbas, Sirik) degrades Iran's shore-based anti-ship missile capability over time but does not resolve the mines/small-boat threat in <7 days.

Sources: CENTCOM.mil (Tier 1, 7–8 Jul) · CBS News (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · PBS NewsHour/AP (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · NPR (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · Arab News (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · The Hill (Tier 1, 8 Jul)
03

Diplomatic — MOU Collapse

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • Trump at NATO Ankara (8 Jul): declared ceasefire "over," characterised further negotiations as "waste of time," said "I don't want to deal with them anymore" and "let's just finish the job" — simultaneously signalling blockade reimposition (NBC News live blog, Tier 1).
  • NATO Ankara Declaration adopted: "Allies reiterate that Iran must never have a nuclear weapon and call on Iran to fully respect freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz" — multilateral pressure formalised, but Trump expressed disappointment with allies' response (NATO/NBC, Tier 1).
  • Iran FM Araghchi threatened "complete halt" in negotiations if strikes continue; Ghalibaf (chief negotiator): "Hormuz will remain open only through Iranian arrangements, not American threats" — both statements constitute pre-conditions for talks collapse (PBS/NPR, Tier 1).

Why it matters: The June 17 MOU is in acute breach from both sides: US revoked General License X (sanctions reversal = US breach of MOU Article 10 per Iran FM); IRGC struck 3 vessels (kinetic = Iran breach per CENTCOM). Neither side has a face-saving off-ramp in the current 24h window. Mashhad burial 9 Jul removes the last funeral-window constraint — post-burial Iran is no longer in a ceremonially-constrained posture.

Supply-chain implication: No diplomatic resolution pathway visible <14 days. Post-funeral Doha session probability collapsed to <15%. Q3 supply-chain planning must now assume MOU non-operative status as baseline. Long-tenor contracts priced on MOU reopening assumptions need urgent repricing.

Sources: NBC News (Tier 1, 9 Jul) · PBS NewsHour/AP (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · NPR (Tier 1, 8 Jul) · FDD Analysis (Tier 3, 7 Jul) · NATO Declaration text via NBC (Tier 1, 8 Jul)

Infrastructure & Energy

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • Brent surged 5% to ~$78/b on 8 Jul (Trading Economics / Yahoo Finance, Tier 2); intraday range 9 Jul: $75.47–$79.24 (Investing.com, Tier 2) — reversal of the downward trend from EIA STEO $74/b baseline; key level to watch: $80+ = market re-pricing to Scenario C.
  • US Treasury revoked General License X via General License X1 (7 Jul): no new Iranian crude purchases/loading/shipments after 7 Jul; existing deals wind down by 17 Jul; payments to blocked persons held in US accounts — Iran loses primary MOU economic benefit (FDD/AP, citing Treasury, Tier 1).
  • CENTCOM strikes hit Bandar Abbas port, Qeshm, Sirik coastal infrastructure (7–8 Jul); Trump confirmed Kharg Island strike 7 Jul and threatened seizure — primary Iranian oil export terminal at acute risk (RFERL/NBC, Tier 1).

Why it matters: Three simultaneous energy shocks: (1) price spike on strike escalation; (2) Iranian oil sanctions reimposition removes supply from market; (3) Hormuz blockade reimposition removes transit. Counter-signal: OPEC+ +188k b/d for August still in effect — Saudi/UAE output partially offsetting Iranian loss, but not Hormuz transit suppression.

Supply-chain implication: Revise Q3 Brent benchmark from EIA STEO $74/b to $78–85/b range under Scenario B/C. Hedge window active. War-risk physical premium $3–6/b on non-Hormuz supply. Kharg Island threat = potential loss of 90%+ of Iranian crude export capacity if struck again.

Sources: Trading Economics (Tier 2, 8 Jul) · Yahoo Finance (Tier 2, 8 Jul) · Investing.com (Tier 2, 9 Jul) · FDD (Tier 3, 7 Jul) · RFERL (Tier 2, 7 Jul) · NBC/CBS (Tier 1, 8 Jul)

Alternative Routes — Red Sea / Cape

Severe → Confidence: Medium
  • Houthis: Maersk Sentosa (US-flagged container) reported explosion off Nishtun 9 Jul; Houthis claimed attack via ballistic and wing missiles (Wikipedia citing UKMTO-derived; no UKMTO advisory confirmed as of 06:00 UTC) — treat as unconfirmed pending Tier 1 advisory.
  • 5 Jul UKMTO: bulk carrier attacked by skiff 30nm SW Hodeidah — armed assailants, unclaimed; vessel safe; UKMTO advised caution (UKMTO/Euronews, Tier 1). Houthi pattern: ongoing Red Sea attacks resumed, consistent with Houthi statements on Iran war solidarity.
  • EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extended to February 2027; Cape of Good Hope near-saturation; +10–14d transit premium; Saudi Yanbu terminal and East-West pipeline at capacity pressure; Salalah and Jeddah handling increased rerouting traffic.

Why it matters: Red Sea/Bab al-Mandeb remains closed to commercial traffic independent of Hormuz status. With Hormuz now in full kinetic re-escalation, both chokepoints are simultaneously hostile — a structural dual-closure that has no historical precedent in the modern energy trading era. Cape capacity is constrained and cannot absorb full Hormuz+Red Sea diversion simultaneously.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only mandatory — no exceptions. Salalah/Jeddah congestion building. Air freight at premium for high-value cargo. Saudi East-West pipeline viable for Gulf crude only. No Red Sea re-entry window foreseeable <30 days. W4 (Maersk Sentosa UKMTO confirmation) is key near-term signal.

Sources: UKMTO (Tier 1, 5 Jul) · Wikipedia/Houthi attacks article (Tier 4, 9 Jul — W4 unconfirmed) · Euronews (Tier 2, 5 Jul) · CBS News (Tier 1, 5 Jul) · EUNAVFOR ASPIDES (Tier 1)

Outlook & Scenarios

  • Scenario B (frozen attrition) survival at 35% (↓7pp) — contingent on quiet back-channel contact within 72h; Qatar FM remains the only viable channel; Trump's "let's finish the job" language reduces this probability daily without signal.
  • Counter-signal against Scenario C dominance: Trump on 8 Jul also said the exchange "would not lead to long-term military action" (Britannica/AP), suggesting a potential rhetorical ceiling; Iran's FM threat of negotiation "halt" is pre-conditional, not declarative; Ghalibaf remains Iran's negotiator in post.

Why it matters: The modal scenario has shifted from B to C for the first time since Day 90. This is not a short-term volatility signal — it reflects structural breach of both sides' MOU commitments and a US executive declaration that the agreement is terminated. The 30-day framework no longer provides a credible de-escalation anchor.

Supply-chain implication: Scenario C operative baseline = Hormuz closed >60 days; alt routes at saturation; insurance underwriting effectively impossible for Gulf routes; price rationing begins in refined products; FM exposure on long-tenor contracts requires immediate review. Scenario B survival = conditional on back-channel signal by 12 Jul.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
