Day 119: The Islamabad MOU's first operational test failed within 72 hours. IRGC drones struck the Singapore-flagged Evergreen Ever Lovely near Dahit, Oman on 25 June — the first confirmed vessel attack since the MOU signing — forcing IMO to pause its 600-ship, 11,000-seafarer evacuation (UKMTO Tier 1; IMO Tier 1; CNN/CNBC Tier 1). Iran simultaneously rejected the IMO/Oman southern corridor, ordering all vessels to use only Tehran-designated routes; at least two tankers U-turned. Secretary of State Rubio completed a GCC tour in Bahrain warning that Hormuz tolls would "spread like a contagion," but found private Gulf-ally disappointment over perceived MOU concessions to Iran. IAEA inspections remain blocked: Iran's deputy FM Gharibabadi explicitly stated access resumes only after a final deal — contradicting IAEA DG Grossi's assurance that inspections are inevitable under the MOU. Lebanon Round 5 talks occurred 23–25 June but Israeli strikes killed two people in southern Lebanon during the session, leaving the de-confliction cell unverified.

Six categories

01

Maritime Security

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • Ever Lovely attack (UKMTO/IMO/CNN Tier 1): Singapore-flagged Evergreen container ship struck by projectile (likely drone, per security source) 14 km SE of Dahit, Oman on 25 Jun. Starboard bridge damage; no casualties. US officials told Reuters Iran was responsible. Not operating under IMO evacuation framework.
  • IMO evacuation paused (IMO Tier 1, 25 Jun): Secretary-General Dominguez halted the 600-ship, 11,000-seafarer operation "to reconfirm safety guarantees." Several vessels had already successfully evacuated. Resumption conditional on IRGC corridor clarity.
  • IRGC two-tier corridor (Lloyd's List / Al Jazeera Tier 1): IRGC declared southern Oman/IMO corridor "unacceptable and dangerous"; at least two tankers U-turned near Khasab; PGSA stated transit outside Tehran-approved routes will not be guaranteed safe passage. MarineTraffic recorded 70 crossings Wed (wartime high), most via southern Oman coast.

Why it matters: The IRGC has now demonstrated willingness to enforce corridor control kinetically against non-compliant vessels — including a major flag-state Evergreen ship — inside the MOU window. This raises the risk premium on all non-Tehran-coordinated transits and directly undermines the commercial reopening trajectory. The attack is the Hard-tier observable confirming that IRGC corridor authority is not merely declaratory.

Supply-chain implication: Hold all non-IMO-sequenced transits. War-risk insurance re-tightens: Ever Lovely attack will trigger P&I club reassessment. Goldman July recovery forecast shifts right contingent on corridor resolution. 11,000 stranded seafarers and ~500 ships remain in limbo.

Military / Kinetic

Severe → Confidence: Medium
  • Ever Lovely attribution (Reuters/US officials Tier 1): Two US officials told Reuters Iran fired on the ship. No formal US government statement as of 06:00 UTC 26 Jun. IRGC also ordered two Panama-flagged vessels to alter course on 25 Jun (Ambrey, Tier 3, requires corroboration).
  • Lebanon / IDF (Al Jazeera / NBC Tier 1): Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon killed 2 people on 25 Jun during Lebanon Round 5 talks. US intelligence assesses Israel will likely continue Lebanon operations. Hezbollah abiding by 19 Jun ceasefire conditionally but IDF retains "full operational freedom" per IDF spokesperson.
  • No new US–Iran direct kinetic exchange: No CENTCOM-confirmed strikes or intercepts in Hormuz region 22–25 Jun. The Ever Lovely incident is the only confirmed kinetic event in this window, and US response posture has not been publicly declared as of 06:00 UTC.

Why it matters: Iran has now kinetically enforced corridor control inside the MOU period. Trump previously warned renewed bombing if Iran violated the MOU. The absence of a declared US response within 24 hours will be interpreted by IRGC as tolerance of corridor enforcement. Lebanon compliance failure is simultaneously blocking the Araghchi rescission sequencing.

Supply-chain implication: Model 10% probability of US kinetic response to Ever Lovely within 72 hours — if triggered, re-escalation to Threat 5 within hours. If no US response: IRGC corridor control hardens as a structural feature of any interim arrangement, adding permanent transit-cost premium.

Diplomatic

Elevated ↓ Confidence: High
  • Rubio GCC tour complete (Reuters/Dawn/Al Jazeera Tier 1): Rubio visited UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain 23–25 Jun. Warned Hormuz tolls would "spread like a contagion." Stated zero support among Gulf states for Iranian tolls. Did not ask GCC to contribute to Iran reconstruction fund. Gulf allies privately "disappointed" — fear MOU too favourable to Iran (Reuters Tier 1).
  • Nuclear inspections dispute hardens (NPR/CBS News Tier 1): IAEA DG Grossi (24 Jun): inspections "will happen" under MOU, "whether in one week or 10 days." Iran deputy FM Gharibabadi same day: no IAEA access until final deal and sanctions lifted. Trump claimed Iran agreed to inspections "into infinity" — Tehran denied. MOU text does not set inspection timeframe (CBS News Tier 1).
  • Pakistan: Round 2 US–Iran talks next week (CBS News Tier 1): Pakistan mediator confirmed 60-day negotiation window will resume next week. First round ended in Switzerland 23 Jun. US and Iran offered conflicting accounts on financial incentives, Hormuz control, and Lebanon.

Why it matters: The Rubio GCC tour reveals a structural credibility gap: the Trump administration is simultaneously trying to reassure Iran (MOU is a positive deal) and GCC allies (Iran will not get too much). The nuclear inspections contradiction is a substantive disagreement, not a messaging gap — it will determine whether the 60-day window produces a real framework or collapses into MOU implementation drag.

Supply-chain implication: Round 2 talks next week are the next diplomatic inflection point. Watch for: joint statement language on IAEA access, corridor routing agreement, and Lebanon de-escalation mechanism. Absence of all three = implementation drag scenario (23% and rising).

Infrastructure & Energy

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Brent $74.43, 26 Jun (TradingEconomics Tier 3, corroborated by Investing.com range $72.64–73.72): Down 19% over 30 days; third consecutive weekly decline. Rose 2% on Ever Lovely attack Thu, partially reversing prior session losses. Goldman Sachs cut Q4 forecast to $80 (from $90); expects Persian Gulf exports at pre-war levels by end July.
  • Gulf production restart signals (TradingEconomics/Reuters Tier 1–3): Saudi tankers routing to Ras Tanura for first Persian Gulf exports since March. Qatar issued first post-war crude tender. Despite growing flows, tanker shortage is an emerging constraint — Middle Eastern producers ramping output but struggling to secure enough vessels (TradingEconomics).
  • Bypass infrastructure (East–West / ADNOC — straits.live Tier 3): East–West Pipeline Yanbu at ~2.5–3.5 Mbbl/day effective; ADNOC pipeline to Fujairah active but terminal intermittent. Combined bypass ~7 Mbbl/day vs ~20 Mbbl/day Hormuz normal. WTO Hormuz Trade Tracker: crude −95%, LNG −99%, fertilizer −94% vs pre-crisis.

Why it matters: Market has priced-in MOU peace dividend (Brent down from >$100 at war peak to ~$74). The Ever Lovely attack introduces upside risk to this dovish pricing — but Goldman's revised July recovery timeline and Saudi/Qatar restart signals suggest the market is treating the attack as a setback, not a reversal. Key risk: if IRGC corridor control becomes a permanent structural feature, RBC Capital's Croft is correct that "appreciably lower flows" become the structural norm.

Supply-chain implication: Brent hedge window $72–82 for remainder of Q2–Q3. Tanker shortage now a secondary supply constraint distinct from Hormuz closure. Agricultural buffer exhaustion remains a downstream tail risk for Gulf-region buyers. LNG −99% creates ongoing exposure for long-tenor contract holders.

Denmark joining the maritime mission is meaningful for escort capacity but does not resolve IRGC route control.

Outlook & Scenarios

Elevated/Pivoting ↓ Confidence: Medium
  • Scenario A downward revision: Ever Lovely attack, IRGC corridor enforcement, Lebanon non-compliance, and IAEA decoupling collectively reduce near-term negotiated framework probability. Path to full commercial reopening now requires at minimum 4 sequential gates: Lebanon compliance → IRGC rescission → mine-clearing → IAEA date.
  • Scenario B rising: IRGC corridor control operating as a de facto sovereignty claim; Iran's PGSA framing of Hormuz as an Iranian-managed waterway mirrors a structural change. RBC Capital's "appreciably lower flows" thesis gains analytical weight.
  • Counter-signal (anti-bias check): 70 MarineTraffic crossings on Wed 25 Jun is a wartime high. Saudi Arabia and Qatar are actively restarting exports. US–Iran round 2 talks are scheduled next week. The Ever Lovely attack targeted a non-IMO vessel — IRGC may be enforcing compliance, not seeking escalation per se. Marisks assessed on 25 Jun that risk of outright closure has "substantially" decreased.

Why it matters: The scenario distribution is now bifurcating between a protracted implementation-drag track (IRGC controls corridor, IAEA blocked, Lebanon unresolved) and a delayed-but-real negotiated framework track (round 2 produces IAEA date + corridor agreement). Re-escalation risk has ticked up but remains low — the kinetic threshold for US military re-engagement requires a clearer MOU violation pattern than a single vessel strike.

Supply-chain implication: Scenario B (implementation drag) now warrants primary planning weight for Q3 supply-chain decisions. Do not plan around Goldman's July recovery timeline until W1/W2 watchlist items resolve. Scenario C (re-escalation) is the tail risk requiring contingency inventory and contract force-majeure review.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
