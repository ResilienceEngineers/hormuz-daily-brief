Day 130: Khamenei funeral ceremonies pass through their fourth day without a kinetic incident, with the body transferred to Najaf and Karbala (Iraq) on schedule; burial in Mashhad remains set for 9 July, after which US–Iran diplomatic talks are expected to resume. The Red Sea situation has escalated materially: Houthis sank the Liberia-flagged bulk carrier Magic Seas on 6 July — UKMTO-confirmed — the second attack within 48 hours of the 5 July attack on an unnamed cargo vessel, and the most severe maritime incident since May. NATO's Ankara summit (7–8 Jul) opened with a draft declaration calling on Iran to "fully respect freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz," adding multilateral diplomatic weight to the MOU framework. Hormuz transits continue slow normalisation via the Iranian corridor, with ~27/day AIS-visible vs. 84/day baseline; Brent closed at $71.99 on OPEC+ output increases and Saudi price discounting.

Maritime — Hormuz & Gulf

Severe → Conf: High
  • Japan-linked 8-vessel convoy (5 VLCCs, 2 chemical tankers, 1 car carrier) completed Hormuz transit Monday via Iranian corridor; some had previously aborted via Oman route (Japan Times, Tier 1, 7 Jul).
  • AIS-visible transits ~27/day vs 84/day pre-war baseline; IRGC permission-based system operational; dark transits unquantified (straits.live / IMF PortWatch, 7 Jul).
  • Sunday: series of tankers abandoned Oman-route crossing and switched to Iranian corridor — IRGC enforcement of route control confirmed (Trading Economics/Reuters, Tier 1–2, 6 Jul).

Why it matters: Iranian corridor is functioning but is IRGC-controlled and permission-dependent — not commercially normalised. The corridor fee dispute remains unresolved (Oman "voluntary service fee" proposal still in play). Each transit that occurs via the Iranian route validates IRGC leverage but also incrementally reduces the ~100+ stranded VLCC backlog from March.

Supply-chain implication: No non-IRGC-cleared transits justified. Stranded VLCC backlog now "down to just a handful" per Japan Times — the overhang of trapped crude is clearing, which is part of why Brent is below $72. War-risk 8× pre-crisis unchanged; 6 P&I clubs withdrawn; physical/paper spread persists.

Military & Kinetic

Severe → Conf: Medium-High

Red Sea escalation (Magic Seas sinking) is operationally significant but structurally separate from Hormuz track — it reflects Houthi autonomous action, not Iran-directed escalation at this stage.

Supply-chain implication: Maintain kinetic-pause assumption for Hormuz through 9 Jul funeral conclusion. Red Sea threat has shifted from "probe" (5 Jul) to "full resumption pattern" (6 Jul sinking) — treat as active Houthi maritime campaign until evidence of pause or claim clarification.

Alternative Routes

Severe ↑ Conf: High
  • Magic Seas (Liberia-flagged bulk carrier) sunk 6 Jul by Houthis — UKMTO confirmed; operated by Allseas Marine; skiffs engaged vessel, projectile hit, fire, limpet mine detonations, vessel sank; crew rescued by passing commercial vessel (Washington Institute / UKMTO, Tier 1–2, 6 Jul).
  • 5 Jul attack (unnamed cargo vessel, 30nm SW Hudaydah) — UKMTO confirmed "unknown armed assailants"; skiff approached bulk carrier, opened fire, security guards returned fire, skiff withdrew to larger vessel with AIS off (UKMTO/CNBC/ABC, Tier 1, 5 Jul). Both attacks unclaimed.
  • Washington Institute assessment: absence of international naval rapid-response force in southern Red Sea "likely encouraged" escalation; EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extended to Feb 2027 but not deployed in south Red Sea (Washington Institute, Tier 2). Houthis threatened Saudi airports/assets 5 Jul after Iran plane dispute (CBS/AFP, Tier 1).

Why it matters: Two UKMTO-confirmed attacks in 48 hours — with one vessel sunk — represents the sharpest Red Sea re-escalation since the May 2025-era campaign. The Magic Seas sinking (crew abandoned ship; vessel sank following limpet mine explosions) is operationally qualitatively different from a skiff probe. Cape of Good Hope as the only safe alternative route is now confirmed necessary for all unescorted vessels regardless of flag state. The absence of claim complicates deterrence calculus.

Supply-chain implication: Red Sea routing suspension extended indefinitely pending claim attribution. Cape-of-Good-Hope mandatory. 10–14 day premium remains minimum. Houthi attribution of Magic Seas would trigger Red Sea threat sub-level to 5; reassess all Q3/Q4 routing and freight rate assumptions immediately upon claim confirmation.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
