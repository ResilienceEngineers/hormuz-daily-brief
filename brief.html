On Day 78, Trump transmitted a new nuclear proposal to Iran on 16 May with a warning that "swift progress was necessary to avoid serious consequences," while Khamenei's advisor Shamkhani publicly said Iran was ready to sign in exchange for rapid sanctions removal — yet Iranian officials simultaneously denied receiving any new US proposal. This diplomatic incoherence, compounded by the BRICS foreign ministers' meeting ending 14–15 May without a joint statement on Iran, leaves the negotiating track structurally frozen even as back-channel activity persists. No strike on named Gulf-state energy infrastructure occurred in the 72h window; threat held at 4/5 Severe.

Six categories

01

Maritime security / Hormuz

Severe ↑ Conf: High
  • IRGC's Persian Gulf Strait Authority (PGSA) formally operational: vessels must submit ownership, crew, cargo, and insurance data before paying toll (Lloyd's List via The Week, 11 May). Fees reported up to $2m, payable in CNY. Structural institutionalisation of Iranian sovereignty claim.
  • Iran claims ~30 Chinese vessels admitted under selective-access corridor since 14 May (OilPrice / Iranian state media). PGSA selective-access model: flag-state based, not cargo-neutral. US/Western flagged vessels remain excluded.
  • UKMTO JMIC (5 May update): Hormuz threat level CRITICAL; mines in TSS not fully surveyed; GNSS jamming sporadic; verification hails and suspicious approaches ongoing. No new UKMTO Warning issued after 058/26 (14 May — Haji Ali sinking confirmed).

Why this matters: The PGSA represents a structural shift from kinetic blockade to institutionalised toll-sovereignty. Even in a Frozen Attrition scenario, PGSA embeds Iranian control of Hormuz commercially and legally, making any "reopening" contingent on IRGC approval and creating a permanent compliance layer for global shipping.

Supply chain implication: Any vessel considering Hormuz transit must now factor PGSA compliance cost, IRGC vetting time, and war-risk premium (5%+) into routing economics. For non-Chinese, non-allied flag vessels, this is effectively prohibitive. PGSA normalisation accelerates permanent rerouting decisions for Asian importers.

Sources: Lloyd's List via The Week (11 May); OilPrice (15 May); UKMTO JMIC Advisory Update 041, 5 May 2026; Kpler/CNN (April transit data); Windward AI (11 May).
02

Military / IRGC posture

Severe → Conf: High
  • US blockade: CENTCOM confirmed 75 commercial ships redirected (Fox News / CENTCOM, 15 May); 4 disabled; blockade active since 13 April. US struck 2 Iranian oil tankers attempting to breach blockade (PBS/AP, ~9 May).
  • IRGC fast-craft density surge: 668 high-speed craft tracked across all five Hormuz monitoring zones on 12 May — 2.9× the prior-week peak of 230 on 4 May (UANI, via House of Saud analysis, 16 May). Swarm-style formations and escort-like behavior near commercial traffic confirmed (Windward AI, 11 May).
  • Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei (not publicly seen since war began) issued "new and decisive directives for continuation of operations" via state TV (11 May). IRGC deputy FM warned French-UK post-hostilities escort mission would face "decisive and immediate response" (PBS, 11 May).

Why this matters: The IRGC fast-craft surge on 12 May — occurring the day before the Haji Ali sinking — confirms IRGC operational posture is not tracking diplomatic tempo. 668 craft vs 230 prior-week peak is a hard signal of offensive readiness independent of back-channel talks.

Supply chain implication: Even if Trump holds kinetics past EOD 18 May, IRGC posture alone sustains the effective closure. Logistics planners should not treat diplomatic activity as a leading indicator of maritime normalisation; observable IRGC fast-craft drawdown is the only meaningful early-warning of genuine deescalation.

Sources: CENTCOM via Fox News (15 May); UANI via House of Saud analysis (16 May); Windward AI (11 May); PBS/AP (11 May); PBS/AP IRGC Gharibabadi statement (11 May).
03

Diplomatic track

Concerning ↑ Conf: Medium
  • Trump transmitted new nuclear proposal to Iran on 16 May with warning that "swift progress was necessary to avoid serious consequences" (Wikipedia 2025–2026 Iran–US negotiations, updated 16 May — Soft tier; corroborated by pattern established in prior PBS/AP/Axios reporting on US proposal cycle).
  • Khamenei advisor Shamkhani: Iran ready to sign nuclear deal in exchange for rapid removal of all financial sanctions; criticised Trump rhetoric ("He speaks of an olive branch, but we see only barbed wire") — but Iranian officials simultaneously denied receiving any new US proposal (Wikipedia negotiations page; pattern confirmed by CNBC/PBS prior reporting). Classic dual-track incoherence signal.
  • BRICS FM meeting (New Delhi, 14–15 May) ended without joint statement — Iran vs UAE divisions prevented consensus (Reuters/US News, 15 May). China sent Ambassador Xu Feihong instead of FM Wang Yi. Araghchi: Iran "doubts US seriousness"; nuclear issue to be "postponed" to later stages (AJ, 15 May). German Chancellor Merz aligned with Trump post-Beijing call: "Iran must come to the table now" (Fox News, 15 May).

Why this matters: The Shamkhani–FM contradiction is analytically significant: it suggests Iranian leadership is either genuinely split (hardliners vs pragmatists) or is deploying deliberate ambiguity to create diplomatic space without committing. Either interpretation increases miscalculation risk — US may read Shamkhani signal as a genuine opening and escalate when Tehran's formal position doesn't follow.

Supply chain implication: Hold long-tenor Gulf-origin decisions through EOD 18 May. If Trump interprets Shamkhani signal as Iranian acceptance and Tehran's FM subsequently issues a denial, the probability of a frustrated kinetic response by Trump rises sharply — this is the highest-probability path to Threat 4→5 transition this week.

Sources: Wikipedia 2025–2026 Iran–US Negotiations (updated 16 May); AJ live blog (15 May); Reuters/US News (15 May — BRICS no joint statement); Fox News live (15 May); CNBC (11 May); PBS (11 May).
04

Infrastructure damage

Elevated → Conf: Medium
  • Kharg Island: no confirmed tanker departures after 7 May (Windward AI, 11 May). Dark tanker staging clusters expanding near Larak, Qeshm, Chabahar, southeastern Hormuz — export backlog building. Iran crude export by sea: zero for 28+ days (TankerTrackers via Iran International).
  • IEA: Hormuz crude and fuel flows fell ~4 million bpd in March–April; global oil market materially undersupplied until October even if conflict resolves next month (Trading Economics / IEA May 2026 report). Brent settled ~$108–109/bbl week of 15 May, up ~8% week-on-week.
  • Kharg oil slick (single-event, ~45 sq km, May 6–8): No active continuation confirmed in May 8 satellite images (Copernicus/Conflict and Environment Observatory/The National, 13 May). W3 slick-to-Qatar-EEZ scored as MISS. Environmental acute-crisis risk reduced; but deliberate release from storage overflow remains worst-case monitoring item.

Why this matters: Kharg saturation is now a weeks-not-days timeline. If Iran cannot export crude, the pressure to either accept a deal (to release Kharg) or conduct a deliberate release as a coercive signal increases with each passing week. Both pathways have supply-chain implications — the deliberate-release scenario would represent a new environmental crisis layer on top of the commercial one.

Supply chain implication: Continued Iranian export disruption sustains IEA undersupply projection to October. Gulf-origin crude sourcing alternatives (US shale uplift, West African spot) remain structurally insufficient to replace 4m bpd gap. Price plateau at $105–115 range is the working assumption barring kinetic escalation (breakout above $115) or deal (rapid drop toward $90).

Sources: Windward AI (11 May); TankerTrackers via Iran International (12 May); IEA May 2026 report via Trading Economics (15 May); Copernicus/The National (13 May); Trading Economics Brent (15 May).
05

Alternative routes

Elevated → Conf: Medium
  • Houthi pause holding: no large-scale attacks since October 2025. MARAD 2026-006 remains active; GNSS interference residual in southern Red Sea (UKMTO JMIC, 5 May). Red Sea volumes ~40% below 2023 baseline; major carriers structurally rerouted to Cape of Good Hope.
  • Saudi East–West pipeline (Abqaiq–Yanbu) and UAE Habshan–Fujairah pipeline both near or at capacity. Combined pipeline capacity ~2.6m bpd available (IEA/CRS) vs ~20m bpd pre-war Hormuz baseline — covers ~13% of the gap. Fujairah OPL zone now confirmed IRGC interdiction area (post-UKMTO 057/26).
  • Iran deputy FM Gharibabadi warned that French-UK post-hostilities escort mission in Hormuz would meet "decisive and immediate response" (PBS, 11 May). Macron responded: "not a military deployment, an international mission once conditions allow" — signals Western intent to prepare post-conflict architecture but no operational timeline.

Why this matters: The Houthi pause is the single most valuable buffer in the current supply chain posture. It is conditional, not structural — the stated Houthi trigger for resumption is any US kinetic resumption against Iran. The EOD 18 May Trump decision node therefore also governs Red Sea corridor viability. A single Trump escalation decision simultaneously threatens both remaining maritime corridors.

Supply chain implication: Maintain Cape of Good Hope as structural baseline. Do not plan commercial flows through Red Sea on the assumption of Houthi pause continuity beyond EOD 18 May until Trump decision node clears. Saudi East–West and Habshan–Fujairah pipelines cannot absorb further volume — treat as fully committed, not as swing capacity.

Sources: UKMTO JMIC Advisory Update 041 (5 May); MARAD 2026-006 (active); IEA/CRS R45281 (pipeline capacity); PBS/Gharibabadi (11 May); The National (Fujairah zone confirmation, 13 May).

Scenario outlook

Re-escalation modal ↑ Conf: Medium
  • Scenario C (Re-escalation) now at 53% — 16pp lead over Scenario B. Primary drivers: EOD 18 May Trump decision node (Project Freedom resumption / new military options briefing); "annihilation" warning rhetoric (15 May); Iranian leadership incoherence raising miscalculation risk.
  • Scenario A (Negotiated framework) at 10% — depressed by nuclear sequencing gap (US: nuclear first; Iran: war-end first), Iranian FM denial of receipt of new proposal, and Shamkhani's conditional signal not constituting a Hard-tier acceptance. BRICS failure to produce joint statement removes multilateral pressure lever.
  • Counter-signal to trend: Shamkhani's public readiness statement is the first senior Iranian figure to explicitly say Iran is ready to sign. If this is a genuine signal rather than a positioning exercise, it marginally reduces re-escalation probability — but insufficient without Hard-tier confirmation via Pakistan channel to move scenario weights materially.

Why this matters: The 53% re-escalation probability reflects a structural condition, not a specific event forecast. The combination of: (a) Trump EOD deadline passing without Iranian formal response, (b) IRGC fast-craft surge independent of diplomatic track, and (c) ceasefire violations accumulating without accountability creates an environment where an accidental trigger (IRGC attack on a vessel Trump frames as a casus belli) is more likely than a negotiated outcome in the next 72h.

Supply chain implication: Scenario C transition within 72h would require: immediate review of all Gulf-origin sourcing contracts; activation of contingency routing (Cape-only); reassessment of inventory holding periods for refined products with Asian exposure; and war-risk insurance re-quotation for any vessel within UKMTO VRA. Begin pre-positioning these decisions now, not at trigger point.

Sources: ISW / Euronews (IRGC posture); PBS/AP (Trump "annihilation" warning 15 May); AJ (Araghchi BRICS distrust signal 15 May); Wikipedia negotiations page (May 16 proposal transmission); CNBC (11 May — internal US camp divisions on military vs diplomatic path).

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
