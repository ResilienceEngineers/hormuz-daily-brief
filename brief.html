Day 134 marks the highest-stakes diplomatic inflection since the 17 June MOU: Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi is in Muscat today for talks the US has framed as a pass/fail test — Iran must publicly acknowledge Hormuz is open and commit to halting ship attacks, or face a "not a great day" response (Axios/US officials). The strike-and-talk cycle that has defined the past five days has paused — no confirmed CENTCOM action the night of 10–11 July — but the structural deadlock over Hormuz sovereignty (Iran insists on control and fees; US demands full open transit) remains unresolved. Hormuz commercial transits remain at roughly 39% of pre-war volume with no large AIS-on vessel using the Southern Highway since 7 July. Mojtaba Khamenei's first potential public appearance — at a Qom condolence ceremony tonight after Maghrib prayers — is the week's highest-value single observable for Iranian command coherence; his continued absence through tonight would confirm IRGC-dominant decision-making and structurally undermine Scenario B.

Maritime Security

Crisis → Confidence: High
  • IMF PortWatch (5 Jul): Hormuz commercial transits at 39% of pre-war baseline (34 vs 88/day); Lloyd's List Intelligence: "no vessels above 10,000 dwt have transited the so-called Southern Highway with their AIS switched on since July 7" — at least two crossed dark.
  • UKMTO: traffic levels reflect "cautious posture" of shipping lines amid "elevated threat environment" (Al Jazeera/Lloyd's List 10 Jul); six dark tankers at Asaluyeh petrochemical port 9 Jul (Windward EO imagery) — consistent with sanctions-evasion activity.
  • Red Sea: UKMTO JMIC confirmed cargo vessel skiff attack 5 Jul SW Hodeidah; Maersk Sentosa struck GoA 9 Jul (Houthi ballistic + wing missile; Maersk corporate + Houthi claim); Minervagracht ablaze GoA 9 Jul.

Why it matters: The Southern Highway — the Omani-coast lane that briefly revived Hormuz transit post-MOU — has gone dark since the 7 Jul resumption of strikes. LNG and fertiliser outbound flows are at near-zero (WTO PortWatch). The dual-chokepoint simultaneous crisis (Hormuz + Bab al-Mandeb) is the operative maritime reality, with commercial confidence collapsing back to pre-MOU levels within 72 hours of the July 7 strike restart.

Supply chain implication: Hormuz-dependent cargo (crude, LNG, petrochemicals, fertilisers) faces minimum 10–14 additional Cape days at ~$1M/voyage extra cost. Any positive Araghchi–Busaidi outcome today will not translate to physical transit recovery for at least 5–7 days as insurance and shipper risk assessments reset. Plan for continued disruption through at least 18 July regardless of today's diplomatic outcome.

Infrastructure & Energy

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Brent: ~$75.80–76.03 (Oilprice/Forbes 10 Jul open); Brent closed ~$76 10 Jul (+5% weekly gain); market pricing back-channel optimism with Hormuz risk premium sustained. 52-week range $58.66–$120.88; current levels reflect MOU discount from March peak.
  • GL-X sanctions reinstated 7 Jul — no new Iranian oil purchases; grace period for pre-7 Jul cargoes (proceeds to blocked account). Iranian floating crude inventory 138M bbl as of 9 Jul (up from 120.5M on 20 Jun, Vortexa/Windward) — building structural oversupply overhang if Hormuz reopens. Six OFAC-designated dark tankers at Asaluyeh 9 Jul; false-flag Taiwan registration identified.
  • Kharg Island: Trump explicitly threatened strike; not yet executed. CENTCOM strikes to date limited to military infrastructure. UAE crude production at record high (Trading Economics 10 Jul). IEA warned prolonged conflict delays global inventory rebuild.

Why it matters: The 138M bbl floating inventory overhang is a structural ceiling on Brent recovery even if Hormuz reopens — Iran has been loading to sell the moment sanctions ease. The reinstatement of GL-X on 7 July removes that safety valve and converts floating inventory into stranded supply, tightening the market paradoxically. The Kharg threat remains the asymmetric upside risk: a strike would remove ~1.5–2M bbl/day from Iran's export capacity and test the 52-week high at $120+.

Supply chain implication: Hedging band $74–80 appropriate for Q3; positive Oman outcome tests $73–74 floor; Kharg strike or Oman breakdown tests $82–88. OPEC+ paper increases (188k bpd August) are largely symbolic given actual Gulf export constraints.

UN SC report: no confirmed Houthi ship-attack campaign resumed since Oct 2025 ceasefire — but threats intensifying with Iran war escalation.
  • Suez Canal: UKMTO JMIC 067 (5 Jul) confirmed transits continuing normally; GNSS interference near Yanbu noted but no operational impact. Iran-UAE Abu Dhabi: EGA Al Taweelah alumina refinery resumed operations (Khaleej Times 10 Jul).

Why it matters: The Red Sea threat is shifting from dormant-to-emerging. The Maersk Sentosa GoA hit on 9 July — the day of peak Iran-US escalation — is consistent with Houthi opportunistic escalation tied to Iran war developments rather than a restart of the systematic Red Sea campaign. If today's Oman talks fail and Iran war escalates, expect Houthi full campaign resumption within 48–72h, closing the Suez alternative and creating a true dual-chokepoint lockout.

Supply chain implication: Cape reroute capacity is at saturation risk if Red Sea also closes fully — port congestion at Salalah, Durban, and Algeciras already measurable. Build 3-4 week buffer inventory for any Hormuz-dependent commodity before Oman outcome is known.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
