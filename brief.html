Threat level
3 / 5 · Concerning
The most significant legal step since the war began: Trump signed the 14-point US–Iran Memorandum of Understanding at the Palace of Versailles on the evening of 17 June, with Iranian President Pezeshkian co-signing and Pakistan's PM confirming the MOU enters force "with immediate effect." The text, released by NBC News and CBS via a senior US official, mandates that commercial vessel traffic restart immediately and demining be completed within 30 days — but Hormuz AIS data shows zero commercial transits as of 18 June morning, confirming the operational gap between political signature and physical gate-opening. Three structural friction points persist: (1) the 30-day demining/mine-clearance window with Pentagon assessing up to 6 months required; (2) Israel's explicit non-party status — Israeli ministers vow IDF stays in Lebanon; (3) the 60-day nuclear negotiation clock is extendable but carries Trump's explicit "we go back to bombing" reservation. The Bürgenstock ceremonial signing scheduled for 19 June is now uncertain in scope; Houthi ceasefire alignment pending; Brent ~$78.57 pricing in partial reopening.

01

Maritime Security

Severe ↓ Confidence: High
  • Hormuz: zero commercial AIS transits as of 18 Jun morning; five consecutive days of zero outbound flow (hormuztracking.com). MOU Article 5 mandates "traffic of commercial vessels will immediately start" — operational gap confirmed.
  • Demining: MOU sets 30-day deadline; Pentagon assessed up to 6 months; narrow Larak–Qeshm corridor only viable passage; war-risk insurance 8× pre-crisis; 6 P&I clubs withdrawn cover (straits.live/IMF PortWatch).
  • Red Sea: No confirmed Houthi vessel strike since MV Verbena 13 Jun (CENTCOM); Houthi leader called MOU a "great victory" but warned further action depends on developments; MARAD 2026-006 in force; UKMTO threat-level noted as downgraded per hormuztracking.com but no formal Tier-1 advisory issued.

Why it matters: The political gate (MOU signature) opened on 17 Jun but the operational gate remains shut. Demining is the binding constraint: the 30-day MOU deadline conflicts with the Pentagon's 6-month technical assessment, creating a structural gap between diplomatic signal and physical reopening. Maersk emergency surcharges and carrier suspensions remain in force, confirming commercial operators are not yet acting on the political signal alone.

Supply-chain implication: Earliest credible Hormuz trickle-flow date: ~18 July 2026 (MOU D+30). Full pre-war volumes not before Q4 2026. Cape-only routing locked through minimum 18 July; Red Sea locked through minimum 24 June pending Houthi statement + UKMTO advisory.

Military / Force Posture

Elevated ↓ Confidence: High
  • MOU Article 1: "immediate and permanent termination of military operations on all fronts including Lebanon." US naval blockade begins lifting immediately; full lift within 30 days per Article 4 (NBC News, CBS News — Tier 1 ×2).
  • Israel non-party: Israeli government was not shown MOU text prior to signing (NBC News); Israeli Defense Minister vows IDF remains in southern Lebanon and will "hit Iran hard if needed"; majority of IDF and Mossad opposed to MOU (Jerusalem Post/Times of Israel — Tier 2).
  • Trump: US troops remain in Persian Gulf "for some time" post-MOU; "If it doesn't get done in 60 days… we go back to bombing" (CBS News — Tier 1). IRGC: no rescission of June 11 closure declaration yet observed; MOU operational counter-signal per methodology rule requires UKMTO confirmation before maritime planning posture changes.

Why it matters: The MOU creates a legal ceasefire framework but does not eliminate the Israeli spoiler vector. Israel's explicit non-party status and continued Lebanon occupation directly contravenes MOU Article 1, which commits "allies in the current war" to ceasing operations including Lebanon. Iran FM Araghchi stated Israel's Lebanon presence "violated the MOU." This is the primary re-escalation trigger for the next 30 days.

Supply-chain implication: P(Israeli unilateral strike on Iran by EOD 22 Jun) revised to 0.06 — US–Israel tension publicly visible (Trump publicly critical of Netanyahu) but Trump restraint structural. P(MOU Lebanon clause becoming active breach point) = 0.30 in 30-day window.

03

Diplomatic

Elevated ↓ Confidence: High
  • MOU signed: Trump (physical, Versailles, 17 Jun evening CET), Pezeshkian (digital), Vance + Ghalibaf (digital, Sunday 15 Jun). Full 14-point text released to NBC News, CBS News, i24, Media Line by senior US official. Pakistan PM confirms "enters into force with immediate effect" (Tier 1 ×4 independent confirmation).
  • Bürgenstock (19 Jun): Ceremonial signing status uncertain — Iran FM Baghaei said "talks not confirmed for now" after Versailles; Vance still expected per US official; Switzerland FDFA confirmed hosting arrangements remain in place. Likely proceeds as ceremonial event.
  • Key structural tensions in 60-day window: nuclear stockpile disposition (dilution on Iranian soil under IAEA; no forced removal); $300bn reconstruction fund mechanism undefined; frozen asset release sequencing unresolved; Israel outside deal with Lebanon occupation ongoing.

Why it matters: The MOU is now legally in force — this is a hard-tier diplomatic event, not a rhetorical signal. The text resolves the toll/free-passage dispute (60-day toll-free confirmed); sanctions waivers are immediate; frozen asset access is committed. But the 60-day nuclear negotiation window is the structural fragility: Iran commits to "not procure or develop nuclear weapons" and "resolve disposition of stockpiled enriched material" — without specifying removal, leaving US hawks and Israel unsatisfied.

Supply-chain implication: P(final deal by EOD 17 Aug) = 0.45; P(60-day extension mutually agreed) = 0.40; P(collapse before day 60) = 0.15. Each scenario has distinct inventory posture implications for H2 2026 feedstock and energy contracts.

Infrastructure & Energy

Severe → Confidence: High

  • IEA: global oil supply to rise 8mn bbl/day by 2027 vs 2mn demand growth — potential supply glut. US Treasury to issue immediate Iranian oil export waivers per MOU Article 10. 100+ oil-laden vessels stranded in Gulf awaiting Hormuz gate (TradingEconomics — Tier 3).
  • Iran infrastructure: Kharg Island terminal suspended; Mahshahr/Asaluyeh offline; Bushehr damaged — 3–6 month restart independent of MOU. Goreh-Jask pipeline bypass running partial. UAE left OPEC+ during conflict; higher UAE export quotas factored into supply outlook.

Why it matters: Oil markets have priced the political signal — $18–20 deal premium already stripped out, Brent now pricing partial reopening with lag. The physical constraint is infrastructure: even with Hormuz demined by 18 July, Iranian production restart is 3–6 months of independent technical work. This creates a 60–180 day period of below-capacity Gulf supply even under full MOU compliance.

Supply-chain implication: Lock H2 feedstock forwards in $76–82 Brent window. Do not wait for physical Hormuz reopening signal before covering Q3 2026 needs — market will reprice upward on any operational delay or Israeli disruption. Fertiliser supply chain stress extends into Q3 minimum.

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Red Sea/Suez: ~26 ships/week vs ~80 baseline; Cape of Good Hope dominant. No confirmed Houthi commercial vessel strike since MV Verbena 13 Jun (CENTCOM — Tier 1). Houthi leader al-Houthi called MOU a "great victory for Iran and Axis of Resistance" while warning further action depends on developments (Wikipedia Red Sea crisis — Tier 2).
  • MARAD 2026-006 in force; JWC war-risk designation for Red Sea unchanged; no UKMTO advisory step-down for Bab al-Mandeb. Israeli Lebanon occupation continues — primary Houthi operational justification per MOU Lebanon clause.
  • G7 joint statement (France, UK, Germany, Italy): commitment to "purely defensive, independent mission" for Hormuz shipping and mine clearance (SWI swissinfo — Tier 2). This provides a multilateral mine-clearance framework that could accelerate the 30-day demining window.

Why it matters: Red Sea and Hormuz alt-route constraints are structurally independent. Hormuz reopening does not release Red Sea. The MOU Lebanon clause is contested by Israel, maintaining Houthi operational justification indefinitely. The Houthi ceasefire on commercial shipping (declared Oct 2025 on Gaza ceasefire grounds) is structurally conditional — Iran MOU "great victory" framing suggests they may stand down further, but no explicit maritime ceasefire statement received.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only routing minimum through 24 June; extend through July pending Houthi explicit maritime statement + UKMTO advisory. Insurance step-down for Red Sea requires both conditions simultaneously.

Sources: MARAD 2026-006 (Tier 1) · CENTCOM (Tier 1) · SWI swissinfo (Tier 2) · Wikipedia Red Sea crisis (Tier 4) · Al Jazeera (Tier 1)

Outlook & Scenarios

Elevated/Pivoting ↓ Confidence: Medium
  • A (Negotiated framework, 87%, +5pp): MOU legally in force, text public, both presidents signed — this is no longer a scenario to confirm but an outcome to implement. Observable now: demining progress, UKMTO advisory, AIS transit data, Bürgenstock ceremony.
  • B (Technical slip/implementation drag, 9%, −4pp): Demining 30-day MOU deadline vs Pentagon 6-month assessment; Israeli Lebanon breach of Article 1; IRGC compliance latency; nuclear stockpile disposition unresolved; 60-day clock fragile. Observable: Hormuz AIS still at zero on Day 111; Israel–Lebanon fighting reported (Euronews).
  • C (Re-escalation, 4%, −1pp): Trump "go back to bombing" reservation explicit; IDF/Mossad majority oppose MOU; Israeli non-party status structural. Observable trigger: Israeli strike on Iran assets OR Iran suspension of MOU obligations in response to Israeli Lebanon operations.

Counter-signal (anti-confirmation-bias): Zero AIS transits 18 June — the operational gap between MOU signature and physical reopening is real and unresolved. The MOU uses "best efforts" language for commercial passage (not a hard guarantee). The Pentagon's 6-month demining assessment versus the 30-day MOU deadline is the most significant structural risk in the next 90 days. If demining stalls, supply chains remain disrupted even under full diplomatic compliance.

Supply-chain implication: The physical reopening timeline is Goldman Sachs' end-of-July 2026 (pre-war Gulf crude levels). Scenario B (slip) adds 30–60 days to that timeline. Scenario C collapses the entire post-crisis pricing structure.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
