Day 94 opens with the US–Iran MOU still unsigned — neither Trump nor Mojtaba Khamenei has given principal sign-off, and Iran's Tasnim continues to deny finalisation — while the IAEA's late-May HEU report (~440 kg at 60% enrichment) has hardened the nuclear-clause dispute. The most consequential overnight development is the Houthi resumption of commercial shipping attacks on 1 June (oil tanker Abliani, cargo ship Maina, LNG carrier Al Oraiq), ending the 23-day standdown that had served as the primary Red Sea buffer; MARAD 2026-006 is re-elevated. On the diplomatic front, Lebanon Round 4 peace talks open in Washington today (2–3 June) with Israeli and Lebanese delegations; formal ceasefire in Lebanon has been extended 45 days from 15 May and has not collapsed, which is a modest positive signal for proxy-front stability. Brent rebounded to ~$89.70 on 1 June (+2.67%) after Friday's close near $91.20, reflecting market volatility around unsigned deal news; structural energy-market disruption persists with Hormuz at ~4 transits/day vs 95/day pre-crisis.

Six categories

01

Maritime Security

Severe → Conf: High
  • UKMTO Critical advisory active; Hormuz at ~4 transits/day vs 95 pre-crisis baseline; ~80% of transits are IRGC-approved shadow-fleet vessels per Lloyd's List.
  • Houthi standdown broken 1 June: Maltese-flagged oil tanker Abliani, cargo ship Maina, and Marshall Islands-flagged LNG carrier Al Oraiq targeted in Red Sea / Arabian Sea — ending Day 23 of standdown; MARAD 2026-006 re-elevated.
  • ~1,550 vessels stranded in Gulf; ~22,500 mariners trapped per IMO/CENTCOM; MOU mine-removal clause (30-day window) not yet triggered; Pentagon estimates 6-month mine-clearance horizon post-signing.

Why it matters: Both major Middle East maritime chokepoints are now simultaneously degraded — Hormuz physically closed, Red Sea actively threatened. This is the structural "nightmare scenario" analysts flagged at crisis onset. Physical flow restoration requires MOU signature + verified mine-clearance + insurer re-entry, a minimum 90-day pipeline even in the optimistic case.

Supply-chain implication: Cape of Good Hope is the only viable routing for all non-Gulf-approved cargo. Do not pre-position inventory on Red Sea re-opening. War-risk premiums will not decline until Hormuz mines are cleared and independently verified — insurers have stated Hormuz is currently "unrateable at standard rates."

Sources: UKMTO Critical advisory (active); MARAD 2026-006 (re-elevated 1 June); Wikipedia — Houthi attacks on commercial vessels (1 June 2026 entries); IMO Strait of Hormuz page (20 May 2026); USNI News (1 May 2026); Lloyd's List / USNI vessel data.

Military Activity

Severe → Conf: Medium
  • No new confirmed IRGC kinetic strike on Gulf-state energy infrastructure since Kuwait ballistic-missile strike 27/28 May — IRGC standdown pattern at Day 4+; consistent with MOU-window restraint.
  • US CENTCOM maintains 3 carrier strike groups in AOR (USS Abraham Lincoln CSG-3, Gerald R. Ford CSG-12, CSG-10 arrived 23 April); posture unchanged; Hegseth: US "more than capable" of resuming war.
  • IDF–Hezbollah exchanges of fire continue in south Lebanon despite formal ceasefire extension; IDF CoS Zamir: "no ceasefire" on ground; Hezbollah fired 100+ rockets at northern Israel since April ceasefire announcement per Alma Centre.

Why it matters: The IRGC standdown is the most significant positive military signal since the Kuwait strike, but it is fragile — Kuwait's Patriot interceptors are estimated ~75% depleted, and any Houthi escalation following the 1 June attacks could provide political cover for IRGC parallel signaling. The Lebanon front remains hot despite formal ceasefire language.

Supply-chain implication: Kuwait interceptor depletion represents the highest single near-term infrastructure risk — a successful strike on Kuwaiti energy or port infrastructure would immediately constrain the one functioning Gulf-state export alternative corridor. Monitor Ali Al-Salem AB and Kuwait port infrastructure daily.

Diplomatic Track

Elevated ↑ Conf: Medium
  • MOU unsigned as of 06:01 UTC 1 June: Trump has not given final approval; White House confirmed Trump "wants a couple of days to think about it" (Channel 12/White House, 28 May); Vance: "hard to say exactly when or if the president is going to sign."
  • Mojtaba Khamenei sign-off unconfirmed: Israeli Channel 12 / senior Israeli source: "we do not have indications that Mojtaba has said yes"; Tasnim: "text not yet finalised or confirmed." Iran FM and IRGC-aligned media continue to contest MOU framing on control of Hormuz passage.
  • Lebanon Round 4 peace talks confirmed for Washington 2–3 June: Israeli delegation led by Ambassador Leiter; Lebanese delegation led by Simon Karam; topics include IDF withdrawal, Lebanese army takeover of south Lebanon, Hezbollah disarmament. Pentagon military coordination meeting held 29 May.

Why it matters: The MOU remains in the highest deal-proximity zone since the war began but the dual-principal sign-off problem is structural. Iran's negotiators (Araghchi, Ghalibaf) appear to have agreed text but lack signing authority — Mojtaba Khamenei is the gate. The Lebanon Round 4 talks are the most important 48h observable: a productive outcome supports both MOU momentum and Houthi restraint resumption; collapse shifts both escalation probabilities upward.

Supply-chain implication: Do not act on MOU optimism until both Trump AND Khamenei provide public confirmation — the gap between negotiator-level agreement and principal sign-off has been the persistent structural failure since Day 60. Any public joint MOU announcement would be a buying signal for Hormuz reopening optionality, but mine-clearance (6-month horizon) means physical flows will not normalise for at least Q3 2026 even in the best case.

Sources: Axios (28 May 2026); CNN live blog (28 May 2026); Times of Israel / White House confirmation (29 May); Al Jazeera 60-day proposal explainer (29 May); Wikipedia — 2026 Israel–Lebanon peace talks (updated 1 June); The Hill (White House MOU denial, May 28); VP Vance / AP (Joint Base Andrews, 28 May).

Infrastructure & Markets

Severe → Conf: High
  • Brent ~$89.70 (1 June open, +2.67% on day); May closing Brent ~$91.20 — down −17% MTD, biggest monthly loss since COVID, driven by deal-proximity pricing, not physical normalisation.
  • Iran crude loadings <0.3 mb/d vs 1.5 mb/d pre-crisis April; QatarEnergy force majeure ongoing; Gulf fertilizer (urea/ammonia ~30% global) export halt continues; US Agriculture Secretary Rollins flagged fertilizer supply chain risk publicly.
  • Analysts warn recovery timeline is 6+ months minimum even post-MOU: mines need clearing, damaged infrastructure repaired, shut-in production restarted, tanker market rebalanced; Lloyd's List editor: "until at least September" for tanker/oil market normalisation if strait reopened immediately.

Why it matters: The market is pricing MOU probability, not physical reality. Brent at ~$89–93 is a deal-optimism floor; the structural disruption floor absent a deal is $100+. The 17% May decline is a false signal for supply-chain procurement — physical flow indicators (loadings, stranded vessels, transits) are unchanged. Gap between market price and physical reality is the primary procurement risk.

Supply-chain implication: Energy price floor $87–95 through Q3 2026 under frozen-attrition scenario. Fertilizer forward procurement is urgent — spring window closing, and any escalation would eliminate remaining spot availability. War-risk insurance for Hormuz-routed cargo remains unavailable at standard rates regardless of MOU status.

05

Alternative Routes

Elevated ↑ Conf: High
  • Houthi standdown broken 1 June: Abliani (oil tanker, Maltese-flagged), Maina (cargo, Maltese-flagged, targeted twice — Red Sea then Arabian Sea), Al Oraiq (LNG carrier, Marshall Islands-flagged) attacked; MARAD 2026-006 re-elevated per Wikipedia Houthi attacks article. Red Sea buffer is no longer operative.
  • Saudi East–West pipeline (Yanbu) remains saturated at ~5 mb/d capacity ceiling; no expansion possible without infrastructure investment; Kuwait Mina Al-Ahmadi refinery partially operational but interceptor depletion limits defence posture.
  • Cape of Good Hope established as structural baseline; industry consensus (Suaid Global, Maritime Strategies International) projects diversions through at least 2027 regardless of Iran deal status; Asia–Europe rates +25–40% vs pre-crisis; Panama Canal ~$4M/vessel premium absorbing spillover tonnage.

Why it matters: The loss of the Houthi standdown simultaneously with Hormuz closure eliminates the last viable secondary maritime corridor. Both major Middle East chokepoints are now degraded. The Saudi EW pipeline provides partial Hormuz bypass for crude but has no equivalent for LNG, refined products, containers, or fertilizers.

Supply-chain implication: Cape of Good Hope is the only viable routing. Do not plan any Red Sea transits until a confirmed Houthi operational standdown (not political announcement) is re-established and independently verified. Add 10–14 days to all Asia–Europe and Asia–US East Coast voyage planning horizons through Q3 2026 minimum.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
