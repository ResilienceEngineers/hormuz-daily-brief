Threat level
4 / 5 · Severe
Iran suspended its missile campaign against Israel on 8 June after a mutual exchange of strikes — the most serious since the April ceasefire — with Tehran conditioning restraint on Israel halting attacks in southern Lebanon, a condition Israel has explicitly rejected. Trump declared both sides were seeking "an immediate ceasefire" and insisted "final negotiations" were proceeding, while the US naval blockade remains in full force pending a final deal. Commercially, Hormuz remains effectively closed at roughly 10–17 % of pre-crisis transit levels; Brent touched $98 intraday on 8 June before easing to ~$93–96 as Iran's suspension statement calmed markets. On the Red Sea, Houthi ASBM strikes continued on 8–9 June, hitting the Norderney (twice) and an unidentified vessel near Djibouti, confirming the episodic cluster model and keeping Bab al-Mandeb fully active as a second threat vector.

01

Maritime Security

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Hormuz effectively closed: ~10–17% of pre-crisis transits per IMF PortWatch/Polymarket (updated 9 Jun); IRGC Persian Gulf Strait Authority controls access; war-risk insurance 8× pre-crisis with 6 P&I clubs withdrawn.
  • Tanker fire off coast of Oman, 8 Jun: crew fully evacuated; Omani and Indian authorities responded (straits.live/Reuters); cause and outage status unconfirmed as of 06:00 UTC 9 Jun.
  • Houthi ASBM cluster 8–9 Jun: Norderney (German-owned, Antigua-flagged) struck twice in Gulf of Aden; MSC Tavvishi struck 8 Jun; unidentified vessel struck near Djibouti 9 Jun — all confirmed per Wikipedia Houthi attacks page (citing CENTCOM/reporting).

Why this matters: The 8–9 Jun Houthi cluster validates the episodic attack model and confirms both chokepoints remain operationally hostile simultaneously. The tanker fire off Oman adds a third near-simultaneous incident. Carrier confidence requires a signed MOU plus mine-clearance plus 30 demonstrated safe-transit days — none of which has occurred.

Implication: Cape-only routing is structural through Q3 minimum. Any planning assumption that Red Sea is a fallback for Hormuz is invalidated. War-risk premiums unlikely to ease until MOU signed and mine-clearance certified; lock in Cape capacity now for Q3–Q4 contracts.

Sources: IMF PortWatch (Tier 1) via Polymarket/straits.live; Wikipedia Houthi attacks (Tier 3, citing CENTCOM); MARAD 2026-006 (Tier 1); straits.live tracker; Reuters (Tier 2) via investing.com 9 Jun 2026.

Military Operations

Severe ↑ Confidence: High

  • IDF struck SW Iran petrochemical plant and military targets 8 Jun (Arab News, citing IDF statement); Israel also struck Tyre (Lebanon) 8 Jun killing 5, wounding 8, hours after Iran suspended operations — Lebanon trigger immediately re-activated.
  • Netanyahu publicly vowed Lebanon operations will continue; IDF operating "as planned" in south Lebanon; Israel holds fire vs Iran per Trump request but has not acknowledged a ceasefire.

Why this matters: The 7–8 Jun exchange was the most serious Iran-Israel escalation since the April ceasefire and validates the Day 101 methodology delta: Israel striking Iran directly against US instructions activates IRGC retaliation doctrine against a broader target set. The IRGC "warning" construct is now in its active 48–96h window (from 8 Jun). Lebanon is the active tripwire: Iran's suspension is explicitly conditional on Israel stopping Lebanon ops, which Israel has refused.

Implication: The 48–96h elevated IRGC retaliation window runs through 12 Jun. Gulf energy infrastructure and US assets remain at elevated risk. Any fresh IDF strike in Beirut or north of Litani should be treated as triggering Iran missile resumption probability ≥60% within 48h. Maintain W1 and W4 watchlist items at current probability.

Diplomatic Track

Concerning → Confidence: Medium
  • Trump on 8 Jun: "Both sides are looking to do an immediate CEASEFIRE! Final negotiations on Peace are proceeding. The Blockade will remain in place until a Final Deal" (Truth Social/Arab News confirmed Tier 2). US-Israel fracture: Trump called Netanyahu "f**king crazy" (Axios, cited by multiple Tier 2 outlets) and said Netanyahu "doesn't call the shots."
  • Iran parliament foreign policy chair Azizi: Tehran does "not see a serious will" from US; Iran claims US initially agreed to release frozen assets but has not done so (CNN 8 Jun, Tier 2). Iran's deputy FM Gharibabadi: assets "neither war spoils for Washington nor a payment fund for its allies" — $24B remains zero-sum.
  • Pakistan PM Sharif called for "all sides to exercise restraint" after 8 Jun exchange (CNN Tier 2); no new bridging proposal on frozen assets. Counter-signal: Iran President Pezeshkian said Tehran "has not abandoned negotiations" (CNN/Britannica).

Why this matters: The diplomatic architecture is frozen on two axes simultaneously: Lebanon (Iran condition vs. Israeli veto) and the $24B asset question (Iran demand vs. US redirect-to-Gulf-allies policy). Trump's public fracture with Netanyahu is real but has not translated into operational restraint of Israeli Lebanon strikes, meaning the Lebanon trigger remains active. The Pakistan channel is alive but has not produced the bridging mechanism needed to unlock the $24B.

Implication: MOU probability remains capped at ≤25% per Day 100 methodology delta. No upgrade to Scenario A probability until either (a) Lebanon ceasefire holds ≥48h without Israeli strike, or (b) a public third-party bridging mechanism for the $24B is proposed. Track both as binary observables.

  • Trump fracture with Netanyahu is operational but limited: Trump told Netanyahu to stand down on Iran strikes; Netanyahu partly complied (halted Iran ops) but refused on Lebanon; US blockade remains until "Final Deal"; no sanctions relief offered (Rubio, Tier 2).
  • Counter-signal against Scenario C: Brent eased $98→$94 on Iran's suspension — market pricing partial resolution probability (~20–25%); Iran president preserved "not abandoned negotiations" framing; OPEC+ supply increase partially absorbs demand premium.

Why this matters: The system is in a high-tension equilibrium: fighting paused by mutual exhaustion and US pressure, but neither structural cause (Lebanon, $24B assets) has moved. The 8 Jun exchange raised the demonstrated escalation ceiling without breaking the framework. Scenario B (frozen attrition) remains modal at 43%, but the Lebanon trigger keeps Scenario C elevated at 37%.

Implication: Supply chains should plan for Scenario B as operational baseline (Cape routing, $90–100 Brent, FM permanent fixtures) through Q3. Position for Scenario C (rapid escalation response: buffer stocks, alt-sourcing for refined products, immediate FM exposure on long-tenor contracts) as the primary tail risk. Do not plan for Scenario A before a signed MOU is publicly announced.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
