Threat level
3 / 5 · Concerning
The US–Iran memorandum of understanding was electronically signed on 15 June by Trump, Vance, and Iranian Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf, with a formal ceremony in Geneva set for 19 June; senior US officials confirmed the Strait of Hormuz "should be fully open by Friday." Physical reopening remains contingent on three Hard triggers that have not yet fired: IRGC formal rescission of the June 11 closure declaration, mine clearance (the IEA estimated ~14 million barrels/day in disrupted supply), and UKMTO/insurer safe-water advisory. Brent crude fell to ~$83 on 16 June (−22% in 30 days), but the Iran–IRGC dual-signal of 15 June — a UN transit notification alongside an IRGC radio warning to a US demining destroyer ("this is the last warning") — confirms the implementation gap between political deal and operational reality. Israel's declaration that it will remain in Lebanon buffer zones indefinitely is the primary residual spoiler vector for the 19 June signing.

Six categories

01

Maritime Security

Severe ↓ Improving Conf: High
  • AIS-visible commercial traffic through Hormuz remained near zero across 11–14 Jun reporting period; no oil tankers passed Monday evening despite electronic MOU signing (NBC/Kpler; HSToday/Windward — Tier 2/3).
  • Iran notified UN on 15 Jun that "non-hostile ships may transit Hormuz in coordination with Iranian authorities" — simultaneously IRGC radioed US demining destroyer: "this is the last warning" (House of Saud/BIMCO cited 15 Jun — Tier 3; corroborated by Iran Intl liveblog Tier 2).
  • Kharg Island crude loading suspended; 23 tankers queuing offshore; ~106 IRGC high-speed craft surged from Khasab South staging node 14 Jun; mines in Strait not yet cleared (HSToday Tier 3; BIMCO Tier 2).

Why it matters: The IRGC dual-signal of 15 June confirms the implementation gap between political MOU and operational maritime reality. Iran has created a paper trail for UN purposes ("non-hostile ships may transit") while simultaneously maintaining physical deterrence posture in the water. BIMCO's Jakob Larsen confirmed that "mine-free routes" are required before shipowners can be reassured — no UKMTO clear-water advisory has been issued.

Supply-chain implication: Physical Hormuz commercial transit unavailable through at minimum 19 Jun; realistically 25–30 Jun minimum for first commercial transits under any plausible mine-clearance timeline. Tanker queue drawdown at Kharg will take additional weeks. Do not plan feedstock receipts from Gulf origin before Q3 2026 at earliest.

Sources: HSToday/Windward (Tier 3) · House of Saud citing BIMCO (Tier 2) · Iran Intl liveblog (Tier 2) · NBC News/Kpler (Tier 2) · 15 Jun 2026

Military

Severe ↓ Improving Conf: High
  • US military received directive to lift naval blockade of Hormuz pending 19 Jun formal signing; US force posture in region maintained during 60-day nuclear negotiation period per senior administration officials (ABC News/CNN 15 Jun — Tier 1).
  • IRGC warned US Navy destroyer conducting demining operations on 15 Jun: "this is the last warning" — institutional signal of IRGC resistance to US military presence even within MOU framework (Iran Intl/House of Saud — Tier 2/3; corroborated by NBC News Tier 1).
  • Netanyahu declared 15 Jun that Israel has "security independence," will remain in Lebanon/Syria/Gaza buffer zones "as long as necessary," and is increasing defense budget by NIS 350bn; IDF not subordinate to MOU Lebanon clause (Al Jazeera/Times of Israel — Tier 1/2).

Why it matters: Two distinct spoiler vectors remain militarily active: (1) IRGC institutional resistance to US demining — the organization that controls the physical waterway has not stood down; (2) Israeli military independence — Netanyahu's "security independence" declaration and Lebanon occupation continuity create the same trigger conditions that caused the April 2026 ceasefire collapse. The April collapse was caused by Israel striking Lebanon after a similar framework was in place.

Supply-chain implication: P(IRGC Hormuz rescission by EOD 25 Jun) = 0.50 (reduced from 0.55 given 15 Jun dual-signal). P(Israeli unilateral strike by EOD 22 Jun) = 0.09 (marginally reduced; Trump fury is a binding constraint, Netanyahu has economic incentive to allow deal). Maintain contingency stock for Scenario C.

Sources: ABC News (Tier 1) · CNN liveblog (Tier 1) · Al Jazeera (Tier 1) · Times of Israel (Tier 2) · Iran Intl liveblog (Tier 2) · 15 Jun 2026

Diplomatic

Elevated ↓ Improving Conf: High
  • MOU electronically signed 15 Jun: Trump + Vance + Iranian Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf. Senior US officials confirmed on background; VP Vance described MOU as "about a page and a half," broad framework. Text to be released post-Geneva ceremony (RFE/RL, The Hill, Washington Examiner — Tier 1/2).
  • Geneva formal ceremony 19 Jun: Vance + Kushner attending for US; Trump said "it depends" if he attends. Doha preparatory technical meetings this week. G7 (Evian) leaders — Macron, Starmer — welcomed deal 15 Jun (NBC News Tier 1; ABC News Tier 1).
  • Key text disputes persist: Iran Mehr news agency says "funds first before nuclear negotiations begin"; US official rejected this. Iran state media: toll-free guarantee "60 days only, then Iran/Oman administer strait" — US disputes (Fortune/CNN — Tier 1/2).

Why it matters: Electronic pre-signing is a Hard-tier event confirming the deal is real. Four Tier-1 sources (ABC, CNN, NBC, RFE/RL) confirm the same facts. However, Vance's framing of the MOU as "broad framework leaving details to technical negotiations" and the competing text versions confirm the 60-day window is structurally fragile. The CFR and Atlantic Council analyses (Tier 2) both note the deal does not resolve core nuclear, sanctions, or Hormuz mechanics issues.

Supply-chain implication: P(Geneva ceremony proceeds as planned 19 Jun) = 0.88 (raised from 0.78; e-sign confirmed; G7 political momentum; Trump attending G7 and wants deal). P(deal collapse before 19 Jun) = 0.09 (Israel spoiler + IRGC non-cooperation). 60-day technical negotiation window is the new structural uncertainty.

Sources: RFE/RL (Tier 1) · ABC News (Tier 1) · CNN (Tier 1) · NBC News (Tier 1) · The Hill (Tier 2) · Washington Examiner (Tier 2) · 15–16 Jun 2026

Alternative Routes

Severe → Stable Conf: High
  • Red Sea/Suez: ~26 ships/week vs 80 pre-crisis baseline. Cape of Good Hope dominant commercial routing. MOU Lebanon clause unresolved — Netanyahu 15 Jun confirmed indefinite Lebanon occupation, which was the trigger for the April 2026 ceasefire collapse (Al Jazeera 15 Jun — Tier 1).
  • Houthi attack base rate: M/V Verbena (13 Jun, CENTCOM confirmed) was last confirmed strike. No new confirmed strike 14–16 Jun as of 06:00 UTC. MOU may create brief tactical pause, but Houthi campaign structurally independent of US–Iran framework (Wilson Center Tier 2; UNSC Res. 2812 Tier 1).
  • UNSC Res. 2812 adopted 13 in favour / 2 abstentions (China, Russia): extends Houthi Red Sea reporting to 15 Jul 2026. Houthi attack on Israel-linked vessels remains policy; ban active since 8 Jun (UNSC Tier 1).

Why it matters: The Houthi campaign's core logic — solidarity with Iran and Lebanon resistance axis — is not resolved by US–Iran MOU. Lebanon clause in MOU is unconfirmed; Netanyahu explicitly rejected any obligation. The same dynamic that collapsed the April ceasefire (Israeli Lebanon strikes → Iran suspended Hormuz access) remains structurally present. MOU Lebanon provisions (per CNN: "does not require Israel's withdrawal from Lebanon") fail to address the Houthi trigger condition.

Supply-chain implication: Maintain Cape-only routing through at minimum 22 Jun. Do not release Red Sea routing exclusion until: (a) Houthi leadership makes explicit ceasefire statement, (b) UKMTO issues Red Sea advisory, (c) insurance war-risk premiums step down. All three absent as of 06:00 UTC 16 Jun.

Sources: Al Jazeera (Tier 1) · Wilson Center Houthi timeline (Tier 2) · UNSC Res. 2812 (Tier 1) · CNN liveblog (Tier 1) · 15–16 Jun 2026

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
