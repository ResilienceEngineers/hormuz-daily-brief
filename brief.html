Threat level
4 / 5 · Severe
Iran's dayslong state funeral for Ayatollah Ali Khamenei opened at Tehran's Grand Mosalla on 4 July — deliberately timed to coincide with the 250th US Independence Day — without kinetic incident as of 06:00 UTC. Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei remains absent, confirmed injured and in hiding since 28 February; his non-appearance fuels legitimacy questions but reduces the assassination-trigger risk for this window. The MOU kinetic pause (Day 18/60) holds across all fronts: no IRGC corridor strike, no Houthi UKMTO-confirmed attack since 17 June, and Brent at ~$70–71 reflects partial market normalisation. Structural blockers remain unchanged: corridor toll dispute, Lebanon ceasefire fragility (Hezbollah called Rubio framework "null and void"), IAEA nuclear talks not started, and mine clearance uncertified — all gate the next Doha session, expected ~10 July post-funeral.

Maritime Security

Elevated → Confidence: Medium
  • Hormuz transits: IMF PortWatch 27 AIS-visible/day on 28 Jun (32% of ~84/day baseline); Windward 43 total on 1 Jul; Marisks cites 335 vessels in prior week, week to 3 Jul on course for similar. Dark-vessel traffic not captured — true flow likely higher. (Tier 1–2)
  • War-risk insurance at ~2% hull value (WTW), down from ~7% at MOU signing and ~1–5% range cited in prior days. VLCC spot ~$294k/day, halved from $500k peak 23 Jun. P&I club coverage partially restored. Mine hazard: GNSS/AIS spoofing active; mine clearance timeline uncertified. (Tier 3)
  • Houthi Red Sea pause holds — no UKMTO-confirmed attack since 17 Jun MOU; Skuld (Tier 3) confirms sustained pause; full drone/missile/USV capability intact; MARAD 2026-006 active; EUNAVFOR ASPIDES to Feb 2027. Suez ~26 ships/week vs ~250/day pre-crisis. (Tier 1–3)

Why it matters: Hormuz traffic is recovering incrementally under the MOU's "best efforts" clause but remains structurally constrained by mine hazard, IRGC corridor sovereignty dispute, and insurance basis risk. The 50–68% transit deficit versus baseline means Gulf producers are still moving oil via pipelines and dark tankers — not via the commercial maritime channel the MOU was designed to restore.

Supply-chain implication: War-risk premium normalization is faster than physical corridor normalization — creating basis risk for contracts priced on Brent paper versus physical Gulf loadings. Unhedged long-tenor Hormuz-dependent contracts remain exposed to a single-incident spike of $10–20/bbl.

Military Posture

Severe → Confidence: Medium-High
  • Funeral window Day 1 (4 Jul) opens without kinetic incident as of 06:00 UTC. Iran's joint military command (Ali Abdollahi, Khatam al-Anbiya HQ) warned US and Israel on 3 Jul to "avoid any miscalculations and to think about the harsh and regret-inducing reactions." Tehran airspace closed Monday for funeral procession. (Tier 1: CNN/NBC/AP, 3–4 Jul)
  • IRGC chief Brig. Gen. Ahmad Vahidi made rare public appearance alongside Khamenei's casket Friday evening — first confirmed public sighting since before the war. IRGC warned: "any miscalculation will be met with a decisive and more crushing response than ever before." (Tier 1: CNN/AP, 3 Jul)
  • Mojtaba Khamenei confirmed absent from funeral — severe security threats; reported injuries (burns, leg wounds requiring multiple surgeries) per NBC News Tier 1 sourcing; communicates only via written statements since 28 Feb. Israel Defence Minister Katz had publicly stated Mojtaba is "marked for death." (Tier 1: NBC News/AP, 3–4 Jul)

Why it matters: The funeral window 4–9 Jul is the highest discrete kinetic risk gate of the MOU period. Vahidi's appearance signals IRGC confidence in security measures — but also raises the Iranian command's profile as an assassination target, which could itself trigger an incident. Mojtaba's absence simultaneously reduces the most extreme trigger scenario (leadership assassination) while sustaining legitimacy uncertainty inside Iran.

Supply-chain implication: Any Israeli or IRGC kinetic incident during 4–9 Jul immediately suspends post-funeral Doha session (~10 Jul) and bounces Brent $5–15. Single incident during mass funeral crowds carries catastrophic escalation risk. MOU Day 18 kinetic pause holds — this is the operative restraint, not the ceasefire words.

Sources: CNN live (Tier 1, 3–4 Jul) · NBC News (Tier 1, 3 Jul) · AP/Reuters (Tier 1, 3–4 Jul) · NPR (Tier 1, 4 Jul) · CENTCOM (Tier 1)

Diplomatic

Elevated → Confidence: Medium
  • Doha 2 Jul: comm channel agreed to flag and address MOU violations; $3 bn goods-purchase mechanism agreed in principle; "positive progress" (Qatar FM). Next session deferred to post-funeral ~10 Jul. Nuclear working group: not yet convened. (Tier 1: Al Jazeera/Qatar FM/CNN, 2 Jul)
  • Corridor dispute structurally unresolved: Iran demands maritime administrative control over Hormuz; Vance stated "no tolls"; Ghalibaf "no negotiations until MOU implemented"; Akbarzadeh "full control of Hormuz." MOU Day 18/60 — 42 days remain including 6 consumed by funeral pause. (Tier 1–2: The National/CNN, 2–3 Jul)
  • Lebanon: Rubio framework (26 Jun, Tier 1 State Dept) signed by Israel and Lebanon — excludes Hezbollah; Hezbollah called it "null and void." IDF strikes continuing in south Lebanon; US-Iran MOU paragraph 1 requires cessation "on all fronts including Lebanon" — Israel asserts Lebanon not included. (Tier 1: Axios/Al Jazeera, 3–4 Jun; UK Parliament Library Tier 2)

Why it matters: The 6-day funeral pause is consuming ~10% of the 60-day MOU window without structural progress on corridor, nuclear, or Lebanon. The post-funeral session ~10 Jul is the decisive gate: a joint corridor + nuclear agenda lifts Scenario A; pure logistics/confidence-building without those agenda items deepens Scenario B.

Supply-chain implication: Full Hormuz commercial reopening (mine clearance, IRGC withdrawal from corridor control) cannot be achieved without the corridor working group, which has not yet met. Plan for no full Hormuz restoration before mid-August even under optimistic Scenario A.

EIA STEO (9 Jun) assumed $105/bbl for Jun–Jul — actual outcome will force major downward revision in 7 Jul release. (Tier 2/3: Investing.com, Trading Economics, Forbes, 2–4 Jul)
  • Gulf supply recovering: UAE exports 3.9 mb/d (pre-war level); Saudi Arabia ~90% pre-war; total Hormuz crude flows >10 mb/d with US military support (Trading Economics, 2 Jul, Tier 2/3). UAE routing via Habshan-Fujairah pipeline + Hormuz dark transits. (Tier 2/3)
  • VLCC spot charter ~$294k/day, down from $500k peak on 23 Jun (Yahoo Finance/Reuters). War-risk at ~2% (WTW Tier 3). Physical-paper divergence narrowing as paper Brent normalizes; but physical Gulf loading premium persists for non-sanctioned cargo. (Tier 3)

Why it matters: The paper oil market has essentially fully priced in the MOU — Brent back to pre-war levels. But physical disruption persists: mine hazard, corridor dispute, and insurance basis risk mean physical Gulf cargoes still carry a structural premium. EIA STEO 7 Jul is the first official US government recalibration of supply assumptions since the MOU — a significant data point for Q3 procurement and hedging.

Supply-chain implication: Q3 hedge window $65–80 Brent. Tail risk of $15–25 gap to disruption-scenario pricing remains for unhedged positions. Do not assume paper normalization = physical normalization. Goreh-Jask pipeline partial; demining timeline unknown.

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Red Sea: Houthi pause holds since MOU 17 Jun — confirmed by Skuld (Tier 3) and no UKMTO-confirmed strike as of 4 Jul. Houthi leader Al-Houthi called MOU a "great victory for Iran and the Axis of Resistance" while warning of further attacks depending on future developments (Wikipedia/Britannica Tier 3 compiled from Tier 1 sources). Full drone/missile/USV capability intact per EUNAVFOR ASPIDES and prior Skuld assessments. MARAD 2026-006 active. (Tier 1–3)
  • Cape of Good Hope: Near-saturation; +10–14d premium; elevated freight rates. All major container lines (Maersk, MSC, Hapag-Lloyd, CMA CGM) still Cape-rerouted. Suez ~26 ships/week — far below ~250/day pre-crisis baseline. EUNAVFOR ASPIDES mandate extended to Feb 2027. (Tier 1–3)
  • Pipelines: Saudi Yanbu pipeline active at estimated 2.5–3.5 mb/d effective (nameplate 5 mb/d; partial NGL repurposing reduces crude throughput). UAE Habshan-Fujairah pipeline active but Fujairah port operations disrupted. Bypass pipelines cover ~35% of normal Hormuz flow (7 mb/d vs ~20 mb/d). (Tier 3: multiple compiled sources)

Why it matters: No alternative route combination can substitute for full Hormuz + Red Sea combined capacity. Cape-only routing is structurally mandatory for non-Gulf cargo. Houthi resumption post-funeral would trigger Cape capacity crisis — the system has no further slack to absorb a second simultaneous closure.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only mandatory EOD 9 Jul minimum; do not pre-position for Suez resumption. Post-funeral Doha session ~10 Jul is earliest Red Sea de-escalation gate. If Houthi resume, transit time extensions +21–28 days on Asia-Europe lanes at capacity-constrained rates.

Sources: Skuld (Tier 3) · MARAD 2026-006 (Tier 1) · EUNAVFOR ASPIDES (Tier 1) · UKMTO (Tier 1) · Trading Economics/Reuters (Tier 2/3) · Wikipedia 2026 Red Sea crisis (Tier 3, compiled Tier 1)

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
