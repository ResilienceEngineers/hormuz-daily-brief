Oil topped $100/bbl on 23 July after Houthis struck two Saudi tankers — Encelia and Layla — in the Red Sea, activating the dual-chokepoint scenario and confirming that Yanbu is now operationally neutralised as a Hormuz bypass. CENTCOM completed its 13th consecutive night of strikes on Iran, targeting command centres, drone storage, and coastal surveillance sites, while Iran rejected a US ceasefire proposal delivered via Iraqi Prime Minister al-Zaidi, insisting no temporary deal will hold without resolution of Hormuz sovereignty. Iran's IRGC simultaneously struck US assets at Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait and bases in Jordan, and drone attackers hit Kuwait's Al-Abdali border crossing — signalling a deliberate geographic expansion of Iran's counter-pressure campaign beyond the Strait. Pickaxe Mountain has not yet been struck; the presidential-commitment heuristic gate (0.65, EOD 29 Jul) remains live and is the single highest-consequence bifurcating event on the current horizon.

Maritime / Strait of Hormuz

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM naval blockade: 12 commercial vessels redirected, 1 disabled in 9 days (CENTCOM.mil Tier 1, 23 Jul). Strait effectively closed to commercial transit.
  • IRGC claimed tanker fire inside Hormuz on 23 Jul (Fars/state media Tier 5); UKMTO had not independently confirmed as of 06:00 UTC 24 Jul — flagged watchlist-pending.
  • Trump Truth Social post: US will strike "ONE BRIDGE OR POWER PLANT" per Iranian Hormuz ship attack, including Tehran infrastructure (CBS News Tier 1, 22 Jul).

Why it matters: The Hormuz closure is now structural, not tactical. The CENTCOM blockade combines offensive strike degradation of IRGC maritime capability with active commercial vessel redirection — a dual-track operation preventing both Iranian-directed attacks and independent commercial transits. The counter-threat on infrastructure creates a new escalation ladder that did not exist 72 hours ago.

Implication: No cargo flows through Hormuz at commercial scale. Insurance war-risk premium for any vessel attempting transit is effectively prohibitive. Cape re-routing is mandatory for all Gulf-origin and Gulf-destination cargo. FM force-majeure clauses on long-tenor Gulf contracts now triggerable.

Long-tenor supply contracts signed pre-conflict at $65–75/bbl basis are now structurally underwater. Refined product cost reset is permanent at current trajectory unless Hormuz reopens within 30 days — assessed P(reopening within 30d) = 0.05.

Alternative Routes

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • Houthis struck Saudi product tanker Encelia (confirmed ablaze, UKMTO Tier 1; Saudi Press Agency Tier 2; Seatrade Maritime Tier 2, 23 Jul) and claimed VLCC Layla (unconfirmed independently — Layla last AIS 17 Jul). Both struck with drones and ballistic missiles. First kinetic enforcement of the Saudi blockade declared 20 Jul.
  • Multiple VLCCs (including Xin Long Yang ~2M bbl) reversed course in southern Red Sea following blockade declaration (Bloomberg Tier 1, 21 Jul). Asian buyers assessing longer Cape routes. Bab al-Mandeb effectively closed to Saudi-linked and risk-averse shipping.
  • Saudi East-West pipeline / Yanbu kinetically unstruck but commercially neutralised. Gate W2 (Yanbu kinetic strike, EOD 28 Jul) remains open at P=0.45 (↓7pp per Houthi-calibration heuristic: commercial interdiction precedes infrastructure kinetics by >72h).

Why it matters: The last functional Hormuz bypass — Saudi East-West pipeline to Yanbu — is now commercially inoperable even without kinetic destruction. The Houthi enforcement pattern is consistent with Day 146 calibration: functional blockade via vessel deterrence before kinetic infrastructure escalation. Cape of Good Hope is the only remaining viable large-volume route for Gulf energy exports. Cape capacity is finite; saturation adds +14 days transit and significantly higher demurrage and bunkering cost. There is no scale alternative to Cape at current volumes.

Implication: Accelerate Cape buffer to 60-day minimum; this is now structural, not contingency. Demurrage cost modelling for Cape re-routing should assume 14-day transit extension as base case. P(Yanbu kinetic strike by EOD 28 Jul) = 0.45. P(Houthi enforcement against non-Saudi vessel in Bab al-Mandeb) = 0.30 (new: scope expansion risk following successful Saudi tanker strikes).

Outlook / Scenarios

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • Scenario C (re-escalation / dual-chokepoint lock-in) is now observably active: both Hormuz and Bab al-Mandeb are kinetically enforced, Brent has breached $100, Iran has rejected the latest ceasefire, and CENTCOM is on its 13th consecutive strike night.
  • Scenario A (negotiated framework) has no proximate catalyst. Iran's Hormuz sovereignty condition and Trump's Pickaxe commitment are structurally incompatible with a near-term deal. P(A) = 3% (unchanged).
  • Scenario B (frozen attrition / back-channel) probability reduced: the Iraqi-mediation rejection 23 Jul closes the most plausible near-term back-channel gate. P(B) = 5% (↓1pp).

Why it matters: The dual-chokepoint scenario has now transitioned from "risk" to "observable reality." The question is no longer whether Scenario C has activated but how deep it goes: Pickaxe strike (nuclear escalation ladder), IRGC expansion to GCC infrastructure, or Iranian conditional capitulation under sustained economic pressure. Of these, only the last would alter scenario probabilities; it requires IRGC autonomous authority to fracture or Mojtaba Khamenei to assert a negotiating position directly. Neither is currently evidenced.

Implication: Supply chains should model Scenario C as base case for planning horizon. Alt-route saturation (Cape) is the near-term binding constraint, not price. Scenario C resolves only via: (a) US military decisive action that breaks Iranian command coherence, or (b) Iranian leadership-level capitulation on Hormuz. Both require events not yet in evidence. 30-day planning horizon: no return to pre-crisis flow rates.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
