Maritime · Hormuz

Red ↑ Conf: High
  • Transit collapse sustained: On 12 May 2026, only 12 vessels transited the Strait of Hormuz — 6 inbound, 6 outbound , versus a pre-crisis baseline of ~100/day. Traffic through Hormuz has run at approximately 5% of its pre-war average across April, according to vessel transponder data published by Kpler.
  • IRGC fast-boat surge — Hard tier: ~668 IRGC high-speed craft were observed across all 5 covered zones on 12 May — approximately 2.9× the prior-week peak; the Eastern Polygon (Bandar-e Jask) was newly active with no prior collection in that area. Four of the 12 transiting vessels on 12 May crossed dark — 33% dark rate — including Panamax and Aframax class vessels with no AIS.
  • Vessels stranded / mariners trapped: 1,550+ vessels stranded, 22,500 mariners trapped — confirmed by the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff General Dan Caine on 6 May. 20 warships from the US military remain deployed near the Strait of Hormuz, reinforcing deterrence.

Why this matters: The IRGC speedboat surge (2.9× prior peak, newly active eastern polygon) is a Hard-tier observable consistent with pre-positioning for expanded interdiction. Windward's MIOC assessed that ballast and laden tonnage is being queued for a forced break-out — leaving without Iranian oil onboard raises the question of Iran's approval to depart. The dual blockade is structurally unchanged. Anti-bias check: a small number of ships are still transiting the strait, but experts say more vessels are leaving than entering, indicating shipping operators are mitigating risks by avoiding the area entirely.

Implication: FM clauses on all Gulf-origin contracts should be treated as permanently triggered until bilateral agreement confirmed. Cape of Good Hope routing is the structural baseline — not a temporary measure. Saudi Aramco CEO Amin Nasser warned that if the Strait of Hormuz stays blocked beyond mid-June, market normalisation will last into 2027.

Military · Security

Kuwait held Iran fully responsible for a "flagrant violation" of its sovereignty and a "grave breach" of international law. The four men — two navy colonels, a captain, and a lieutenant commander — admitted they had been tasked by Iran's ideological army with "infiltrating" Bubiyan Island.
  • GCC / regional condemnation — Hard tier: GCC Secretary General described the operation as a "systematic attempt" by Iran to destabilise the region. UAE Foreign Minister condemned the incident "in the strongest terms," and Saudi Arabia's Foreign Ministry also denounced it as a violation of Kuwait's sovereignty. Bahrain on Tuesday sentenced three people to life in prison for conspiring with and spying for the IRGC.
  • Ongoing drone campaign: The UAE Defence Ministry said two Iranian drones were intercepted. "UAE air defence systems successfully engaged two UAVs launched from Iran." US media reported that the UAE has secretly participated in the Iran conflict as an active combatant — if confirmed, this significantly elevates UAE as an Iranian strike priority.

Why this matters: Three simultaneous Hard-tier signals in 48h (IRGC Bubiyan confirmed, Bahrain espionage convictions, UAE active combatant disclosure) indicate Iran's covert operations campaign is broader than a single incident. Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, and the UAE have all intensified internal security operations over recent months, claiming to have disrupted alleged Iranian-linked sabotage cells, maritime infiltration attempts, and drone-related operations. The IRGC speedboat surge in Hormuz on the same day as the Bubiyan disclosure is a correlated signal. The IRGC Navy warned that any attack on Iranian oil tankers or commercial vessels would be met with a "heavy assault" on one of the bases in the region used by US forces and enemy ships.

Implication: Post-summit window (16 May+) remains the highest kinetic-risk period of the conflict. IRGC covert ground operations are a new operational category that may trigger Gulf-state Article 51 self-defence responses independent of the US-Iran diplomatic channel. Kuwait reserved "its full and inherent right to self-defense in accordance with Article (51) of the Charter of the United Nations."

Diplomatic track

In his opening remarks to Trump, Xi did not mention the US–Israeli war with Iran or other global conflicts. "The common interests between China and the United States outweigh our differences," Xi said. Trump also avoided mentioning the Iran war in his opening remarks.
  • China's position on Iran — pre-summit: The recent Beijing visit by Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi shows China positioning itself as having already weighed in with Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. On Iran, China will try to avoid appearing to pressure Iran in a way that would seem to support the United States, though it will encourage President Trump to reach an agreement that reopens the Strait of Hormuz.
  • US–Iran proposal gap remains unbridged: Diplomacy to end the Iran war has stalled more than a month into a fragile ceasefire. Trump had expected a response within 48 hours of a 14-point memorandum, but when Iran delivered its formal response it arrived four days late and he called it "totally unacceptable." Tehran described its reply as a serious counter-offer but the delay reinforced Washington's view that it is slowing the process.

Why this matters: Xi's deliberate omission of Iran from opening remarks (confirmed by NBC News against CCTV official broadcast) is a Soft-tier signal — regime communication, not an operational event — but it signals Beijing's preference for framing this as a trade summit rather than an Iran-mediation summit. Analysts expect Washington to press Beijing to use its influence over Tehran, particularly because China remains the largest buyer of Iranian oil — by far — purchasing more than 80% of Iran's shipped crude exports. Counter-signal: China is unlikely to reduce its oil purchases or halt arms sales to Iran simply to satisfy American strategic objectives.

Implication: Hold all long-tenor FM reviews until post-summit readout EOD 15 May. A trade-only communiqué with vague Iran language will confirm the post-summit kinetic window is fully open. Day-2 bilateral session (15 May) is the last meaningful diplomatic pause before Trump faces a military decision deadline of EOD 18 May.

04

Infrastructure damage

Amber → Conf: Medium
  • Kharg Island oil slick — cause unresolved: Satellite imagery analysts told The National that they believe the leak is from a tanker, but warned the source could not be confirmed from optical imagery alone. Available evidence points more towards a vessel-related discharge or leakage than a confirmed terminal or pipeline leak. A rupture in an undersea pipeline connecting Kharg Island with the Abuzar oilfield was also identified as a possible source; the pipeline is decades old, poorly maintained and has suffered several leaks in recent years.
  • Iran's denial vs. Windward AI assessment: Iran VP and environmental chief Shina Ansari said monitoring found "no oil leaks from pipelines or oil facilities," and blamed a "non-Iranian tanker" for the discharge. Iran's Oil Terminals Company also said inspections found no signs of leaks from storage tanks, pipelines or nearby tankers. However, the larger spill was first detected west of Kharg Island by Windward AI on 8 May; "it is believed to be crude rather than bunker fuel and unlikely to have come from a ship, possibly originating from pipeline issues or a failed ship-to-ship transfer."
  • Trajectory and environmental risk: The spill could pass through Qatar's exclusive economic zone within about four days, with possible landfall near Al Mirfa in the United Arab Emirates in roughly 13 days, according to Windward. A slick of this size near Kharg Island risks affecting fisheries, coastal communities, desalination plants and marine habitats, experts said.

Why this matters: Kharg Island terminal handles 90% of Iran's exports. As of late April it was at 74% capacity. Experts suggest that once utilization hits 80%, the system becomes functionally inoperable. Whether the slick is from a tanker discharge, aging pipeline failure, or deliberate release, all three scenarios carry significant long-term implication for Iran's export capacity. Tens of millions in the Gulf rely on desalination plants for drinking water. An oil slick of this magnitude entering intake pipes would force immediate shutdowns, turning a maritime war into a humanitarian thirst crisis.

Implication: W5 trigger (Qatar EEZ landfall ~16 May) is the next observable deadline. If desalination shutdowns are ordered, this becomes a humanitarian emergency that could accelerate diplomatic urgency on both sides — or trigger retaliatory claims about deliberate environmental warfare. Infrastructure-exposed procurement leads should be briefed on water-security contingency.

A number of the countries are expected to offer de-mining, escorting and air policing capabilities. Australia confirmed E-7A Wedgetail AEW&C contribution at the 12 May meeting (Australian MoD, confirmed). HMS Dragon, a Type-45 warship capable of destroying guided missiles, is likely to form part of the UK's offer for the defensive naval mission.
  • Pipeline capacity ceiling and Houthi pause: Bypass pipelines can only handle ~7M bbl/day versus ~20M bbl/day normal strait flow — 35% coverage. Saudi East–West (~5 mbd) and Habshan–Fujairah (~1.5 mbd) pipelines at full utilisation. Houthi pause continues — most major carriers have rerouted back to the Cape of Good Hope.

Why this matters: The 40-nation coalition represents the most significant multi-lateral military commitment of the conflict but is explicitly conditional on a ceasefire — it is a Scenario A enabler, not a current capacity addition. Its existence signals Western states' willingness to enforce freedom of navigation post-agreement; Iran will read it as a constraint on its leverage. Paris linked the deployment to a Franco-British initiative established in late April involving more than forty nations, intended to restore maritime traffic without directly integrating into US combat operations.

Implication: Alt-route capacity is fully consumed and structurally capped. No buffer remains. Any escalation that disrupts Saudi East–West or Habshan–Fujairah pipelines would be immediately felt in global energy markets with no available fallback. Cape of Good Hope routing cost equilibrium is the structural baseline through at least end-June.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
