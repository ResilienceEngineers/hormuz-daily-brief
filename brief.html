Day 113 marks the first full operational day after CENTCOM's formal blockade lift (18 Jun, USNI/Stars & Stripes) and the Lloyd's/Chubb $400 million war-risk consortium launch (19 Jun, Lloyd's press release) — two Hard-tier signals that remove the two largest institutional barriers to transit resumption. Yet AIS recorded zero commercial transits on 19 Jun (straits.live; hormuztracking.com), confirming the operational counter-signal rule: legal clearance ≠ physical passage. Israel and Hezbollah agreed a ceasefire effective 20 Jun (CBS News/Reuters, Tier 1), materially reducing the MOU Article 1 violation risk that was the primary re-escalation trigger. The structural bottleneck is now the demining gap — western sources estimate 40 days to 6 months for Maham-series mine clearance — combined with IRGC's unrescinded "last warning" broadcast of 15 Jun and JWC listed-area status unchanged, keeping war-risk premiums prohibitive for most flag-state operators.

Six categories

01

Maritime & Chokepoints

Severe → Confidence: High (multiple Tier 1–3 sources)
  • AIS: 0 commercial transits 19 Jun (straits.live Tier 3; hormuztracking.com Tier 3); 7-day avg ~2.6 vessels/day (3% of pre-war 94/day per IMF PortWatch). Effective closure persists despite MOU.
  • CENTCOM officially lifted naval blockade 18 Jun (USNI Tier 1; Stars & Stripes Tier 1; NPR Tier 1) — US naval assets remain in region to enforce MOU compliance. All restrictions on commercial vessels entering/exiting Iranian ports ceased.
  • Lloyd's/Chubb war-risk consortium launched 19 Jun (Lloyd's press release Tier 1): $200M hull/P&I + $200M cargo capacity. JWC listed-area designation unchanged — per House of Saud analysis earliest delisting mid-2027. Premiums remain 6–8× pre-crisis.

Why it matters: The two institutional barriers — US blockade and insurance unavailability — have now been partially addressed. The binding physical constraints remain: Maham-series mines in the traffic separation zone (demining 40d–6mo, Pentagon estimate up to 6 months), IRGC "last warning" broadcast unrescinded, and JWC listed-area status keeping major flag-state operators out regardless of diplomatic clearance.

Supply-chain implication: Maintain Wave 3 hold. No cargo sourcing or routing decisions should be made on the basis of blockade lift alone. Earliest physically credible reopening: 28 July (40-day demining floor from 17 Jun MOU). Primary scenario: August–September. Budget war-risk premiums at 6–8× pre-crisis through July minimum.

Sources: USNI News (Tier 1) · Stars & Stripes (Tier 1) · NPR (Tier 1) · Lloyd's press release (Tier 1) · straits.live (Tier 3) · hormuztracking.com (Tier 3) · House of Saud (Tier 3)
02

Military & Security

Elevated ↓ Confidence: High (Tier 1 CBS/Reuters corroborated)
  • Israel and Hezbollah agreed a ceasefire effective 09:00 ET 20 Jun, per three diplomats briefed on the agreement (CBS News Tier 1; Reuters Tier 1) — follows deadliest flare-up since US-Iran MOU. Israeli strikes on Hezbollah targets in Lebanon continued through 19 Jun including Baalbek area.
  • Israeli Defence Minister Israel Katz stated IDF will continue operations in Lebanon despite ceasefire announcement — counter-signal to durability. Round 5 Lebanon-Israel talks scheduled 22–23 Jun (Stimson/AJ Tier 1/2).
  • US naval assets remain in Hormuz region post-blockade-lift to ensure MOU "adhered to, obeyed and in full force and effect" (CENTCOM Tier 1). No Iranian kinetic action against US forces since MOU signing.

Why it matters: The Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire — if it holds through 22–23 Jun — materially reduces the probability of MOU Article 1 breach (which requires "permanent termination on all fronts including Lebanon"). Katz's counter-statement keeps P(Article 1 breach) elevated at ~0.25. The Lebanon track is now the primary military re-escalation variable, not direct US-Iran kinetics.

Supply-chain implication: P(Israeli unilateral strike on Iran by EOD 22 Jun) revised to 0.03 — effectively resolved. P(MOU Lebanon clause breach) revised to 0.25 (from 0.30). Monitor Lebanon ceasefire durability through Round 5. If IDF operations confirmed continuing post-Round 5, upgrade Lebanon risk to W1-equivalent.

Sources: CBS News (Tier 1) · Reuters (Tier 1) · CENTCOM via USNI (Tier 1) · Stimson Center (Tier 2) · Al Jazeera (Tier 1)
03

Diplomatic

Elevated → Confidence: Medium (MOU text public; implementation unverified)
  • MOU legally in force (signed Versailles 17 Jun by Trump; digital signature 15 Jun by Vance/Ghalibaf). 60-day nuclear negotiation clock: Day 5 of 60 (extendable by mutual consent). Bürgenstock ceremony canceled — Swiss FDFA confirmed; VP Vance expected Switzerland "as early as this weekend" (NPR Tier 1).
  • Key unresolved: nuclear stockpile disposition; frozen asset release sequence; $300bn reconstruction fund mechanism; IAEA access to bombed sites. IAEA Board adopted resolution 10 Jun urging Iran cooperation (IAEA.org Tier 1); Iran has not allowed access to 8 bombed nuclear facilities (Arms Control Association Tier 2).
  • Lebanon-Israel Round 5 talks scheduled 22–23 Jun (AJ/Stimson Tier 1/2) — first round post-MOU. Hezbollah not party to these talks and previously rejected Lebanon-Israel bilateral deal (NPR Tier 1). Iran-US nuclear negotiations venue/format not yet confirmed post-Bürgenstock cancellation.

Why it matters: The 60-day clock is running without a confirmed negotiating venue or IAEA access arrangement. The MOU's "best efforts" language on Hormuz and "60 days extendable" on nuclear negotiations both preserve maximum optionality for both sides — they are structurally weak commitments that do not automatically translate to operational change.

Supply-chain implication: P(final deal by EOD 17 Aug) = 0.40; P(60-day extension) = 0.45; P(collapse before day 60) = 0.15. The diplomatic track is directionally positive but the absence of a confirmed nuclear negotiating venue within the first 5 days is a mild negative signal against the high-confidence scenario.

Sources: NPR (Tier 1) · IAEA.org (Tier 1) · Arms Control Association (Tier 2) · Al Jazeera (Tier 1) · Stimson Center (Tier 2) · Swiss FDFA (Tier 1)

Infrastructure & Energy

Severe → Confidence: High (multiple Tier 1–3 price/production sources)
  • Brent $80.59 (19 Jun, TradingEconomics Tier 3; corroborated straits.live Tier 3); WTI $77.33; weekly decline ~8.5–10% on reopening optimism/Bürgenstock cancellation whipsaw. Dec 2026 forward ~$76–78. Goldman Sachs: Q4 2026 Brent $80; Gulf exports back to pre-war levels end-July (optimistic scenario).
  • Nearly 10 million barrels observed transiting or positioned near Hormuz on 18 Jun including first Saudi-owned tankers to move since conflict began (TradingEconomics Tier 3); shipping activity slowed 19 Jun — no outbound vessels leaving Persian Gulf morning 19 Jun.
  • Iran announced mandatory insurance requirement for Hormuz transit vessels (Tehran statement via TradingEconomics Tier 3) — not yet enforced; creates additional compliance layer for operators. ADNOC Gas adjusted LNG production; Ras Laffan LNG exports suppressed (~90% normally Asia-bound via Hormuz).

Why it matters: Physical infrastructure restart is independent of diplomatic resolution. Kharg Island, Mahshahr, Asaluyeh require 3–6 months of repair regardless of when ships begin transiting. The Saudi VLCC pre-positioning signal (18 Jun) reversed on 19 Jun — consistent with mine uncertainty and IRGC warning inhibiting first-mover risk-taking.

Supply-chain implication: Lock H2 feedstock forwards in $76–83 Brent window. Iran's mandatory insurance announcement adds a compliance layer; factor into voyage planning. Do not assume Goldman's "end-July" export recovery — it is contingent on demining completion which has a minimum 40-day floor.

Sources: TradingEconomics (Tier 3) · Goldman Sachs via Barchart (Tier 3) · straits.live (Tier 3) · Insurance Business Magazine (Tier 2) · ADNOC via Insurance Business (Tier 2)

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: Medium (MARAD Tier 1; Houthi posture Tier 2–3)
  • Red Sea/Suez: ~26 ships/week vs ~80 pre-crisis baseline; Cape of Good Hope dominant. MARAD 2026-006 in force — Houthi threat to US/UK/IL-associated vessels unchanged. UN Security Council resolution 2812 (2026) extends Houthi attack reporting to 15 July (UN press release Tier 1).
  • No confirmed Houthi vessel strike since MV Verbena 13 Jun (CENTCOM Tier 1). Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire agreed 20 Jun — reduces but does not eliminate Houthi justification for Red Sea operations. Houthi leader statement: MOU "great victory for Axis of Resistance"; further action "depends on developments." No explicit maritime ceasefire declared.
  • Cape of Good Hope: near-capacity; Durban/Algoa Bay congestion +3–5 day delays; port infrastructure approaching absorption ceiling. Counter-signal against trend: Cape continues absorbing rerouted volume without systemic failure — resilience indicator positive.

Why it matters: The Red Sea and Hormuz threats remain structurally independent. Even full Hormuz reopening does not automatically restore Red Sea/Suez — Houthi maritime posture depends on Lebanon track, not Iran-US MOU directly. Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire is the most positive Red Sea signal in weeks but is not yet operationally confirmed.

Supply-chain implication: Extend Cape-only minimum posture through at least 23 Jun (post-Lebanon Round 5 assessment point). If Lebanon ceasefire holds through Round 5 and Houthi issues explicit maritime ceasefire, Red Sea return planning can begin — but MARAD 2026-006 must be superseded first. Cape congestion cost: +$1.2–1.8M/voyage; factor into H2 logistics budgets.

Sources: MARAD 2026-006 (Tier 1) · CENTCOM (Tier 1) · UN Security Council/UNSC (Tier 1) · Al Jazeera (Tier 1) · CBS News (Tier 1)

Why it matters: The gap between diplomatic velocity (MOU signed, blockade lifted, consortium launched in 5 days) and physical reality (0 transits, mines uncleared, IRGC silent) is the defining tension of Day 113. The risk is not re-escalation — it is prolonged implementation drag that exhausts political will before the 60-day nuclear clock expires.

Supply-chain implication: P(final deal by EOD 17 Aug) = 0.40; P(60-day extension) = 0.45; P(collapse before day 60) = 0.15. Physical Hormuz reopening earliest 28 July; primary scenario August–September. Red Sea improvement contingent on Lebanon ceasefire durability and explicit Houthi maritime statement.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
