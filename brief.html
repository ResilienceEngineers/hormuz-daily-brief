Maritime & Chokepoints

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Hormuz commercial transits remain 5–10% of pre-crisis baseline (<10 vessels/day vs ~94/day); LNG and fertiliser outbound near-zero per WTO/AXSMarine since MOU signing 17 Jun (Tier 2: WTO Trade Tracker).
  • JMIC threat level "Substantial" — mines remain in the waterway; Iran's deputy FM states demining will be "solely by Iran," rejecting France-Oman proposal (Tier 1: Iran Intl/AP; Tier 1: CBS/CNN).
  • Red Sea: ~26 ships/week vs ~80 baseline. MARAD 2026-006 in force. No new UKMTO-confirmed Houthi vessel strike since last bulletin. AIS spoofing and "dark vessel" activity ongoing in both corridors (Tier 1: MARAD; Tier 1: UKMTO/JMIC; IMF PortWatch caveat).

Why it matters: Mine hazard is the principal structural blocker to corridor re-opening. Iran's insistence on sole demining authority is a deliberate leverage instrument — no certified clearance means no commercial insurance, no IMO evacuation resumption, and no WTO/EIA-projected traffic recovery to even 40 transits/day within 30 days.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only mandatory for all non-pre-cleared tonnage. Tanker spot premium persists. LNG and fertiliser buyers on spot markets face continued allocation constraints through at least EOD 7 Jul. No corridor is operationally safe for standard commercial insurance.

Mojtaba's continued absence from public view is a succession-risk signal.

Supply-chain implication: Kinetic pause is necessary but not sufficient for commercial resumption — corridor sovereignty dispute and mine hazard are the binding constraints. P(IRGC corridor vessel interdiction) revised to 0.10 for EOD 4 Jul given pause + funeral period.

Diplomatic

Elevated → Confidence: Medium
  • 30 Jun Doha: Witkoff + Kushner met Qatari PM Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman; Qatar FM confirmed "positive" lower-level US–Iran technical talks progressing. No direct US–Iran meeting — Qatar explicitly confirmed this (Tier 1: CNN/Reuters/Bloomberg/Qatar FM).
  • Iran–Oman Joint Hormuz Committee held inaugural session 29 Jun in Muscat — preliminary governance discussions; no corridor breakthrough. Oman rejects transit fees, backs UNCLOS; Iran insists on sole demining authority (Tier 1: AP/Euronews; Tier 2: IRIA News).
  • Iran–Qatar talks 1 Jul on frozen assets and MOU implementation ongoing. $6bn of $12bn frozen Qatar assets pipeline confirmed "according to advancement of negotiations" (Qatar FM, Tier 1: CNN). MOU Day 14/60; 18 Aug expiry clock running. Khamenei funeral 4–9 Jul — senior foreign delegations attending; bilateral meetings on sidelines expected (Tier 1: Euronews/AP/CNN).

Why it matters: The messaging gap between US ("Iran requested meeting") and Iran ("no technical talks confirmed") reflects a structural ambiguity about the MOU's implementation track. Doha process is the only active channel — its continuation is positive, but "positive talks via mediators" is not a joint statement on corridor administration.

Supply-chain implication: No joint corridor communiqué means IMO evacuation remains suspended. The Khamenei funeral sideline meetings (4–9 Jul) are an underrated diplomatic channel — multilateral contacts with Iran's VP and FM could produce informal corridor language outside the formal track.

Sources: CNN (Tier 1) · Reuters/Qatar FM (Tier 1) · Bloomberg/Spokesman-Review (Tier 1) · AP/Euronews (Tier 1) · IRIA News (Tier 3) · Iran International (Tier 2)

Any corridor re-escalation (IRGC action, diplomatic collapse) bounces Brent $5–10/bbl. The Q3 EIA assumption of partial resumption is already at risk — Day 14/60 MOU with zero LNG outbound.

Supply-chain implication: Brent hedge window $70–78 Q3 remains valid. LNG and fertiliser buyers should not model Q3 Hormuz restoration. Tanker spot premium persists; tanker shortage limits throughput even if corridor opens. Cushing inventories drawn down — US gasoline prices remain sensitive.

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Red Sea: ~26 ships/week vs ~80 baseline (Suez Canal containerships). MARAD 2026-006 in force. EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extended to 28 Feb 2027; Denmark joined France/UK mission. No new UKMTO-confirmed Houthi vessel strike in current window, but threat baseline unchanged (Tier 1: MARAD; Tier 1: UKMTO/JMIC; Tier 1: EUNAVFOR ASPIDES).
  • Cape reroute near-saturation — Sohar, Fujairah, Salalah congestion persists. 10–14 day transit premium Q3 2026. DHL/supply chain operators: 4–6 months to normalization (Tier 3: DHL/gCaptain).
  • Petroline (Yanbu): active ~2.5–3.5 mb/d. ADCO pipeline (Fujairah): active but port operations intermittent. Kirkuk-Ceyhan: reduced capacity intermittent operation. Bypass pipelines cover ~35% of normal Hormuz flow — structural gap (Tier 3: hormuztracker.com; Tier 1: EIA).

Why it matters: Both primary chokepoints (Hormuz and Bab al-Mandeb) remain severely degraded simultaneously. The dual-chokepoint closure is the defining feature of this crisis — it eliminates the option to reroute within the region. Cape is the only viable route for non-Hormuz-cleared tonnage.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only mandatory through at least EOD 7 Jul. Sohar/Salalah hub congestion adding 2–5 day delays on top of base Cape transit time. No IMO-sequenced Hormuz transit without certified mine clearance and Iran safety guarantee.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
