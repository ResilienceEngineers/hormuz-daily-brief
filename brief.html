On 11 June Trump cancelled planned third-wave strikes on Iran, announcing a 60-day ceasefire framework and claiming "a great settlement of the war" — but as of 06:01 UTC 12 June, Iran has not publicly confirmed the agreement, Netanyahu says Israel is not party to it, and no document has been signed. Brent fell to ~$89/bbl on deal optimism, its lowest since March, while traders flag that even a signed deal faces 15–20 days of mine-clearance and production restart before Hormuz flows normalise. The IRGC formal "closed to all vessels" declaration issued 10 June remains technically in effect; a UKMTO-confirmed vessel approach near Bab al-Mandeb and a probable Houthi incident on 12 June underscore that kinetic risk is not extinguished. The threat level is lowered from 5/5 Crisis to 4/5 Severe reflecting the breakthrough signal, but the trend is held at Same/Pivoting pending Iranian confirmation and document signing.

Six categories

01

Maritime Security

Severe → Confidence: High
  • IRGC formal "closed to all vessels" declaration issued 10 Jun; 2 oil tankers struck in kinetic enforcement (IRGC/Reuters/NBC confirmed); CENTCOM disputes closure claim but acknowledges tanker strikes.
  • Trump 11 Jun claims deal includes Hormuz reopening "the moment it's signed" and US will lift naval blockade; traders note 15–20 day mine-clearance and restart lag even post-signing (Trading Economics, Reuters).
  • UKMTO confirmed armed-boat approach near Balhaf, Yemen (Bab al-Mandeb) 10 Jun; probable new Houthi incident 12 Jun (Wikipedia Houthi attacks article, entry cut); Norderney + MSC Tavvishi struck 8–9 Jun (Wilson Center/CENTCOM confirmed).

Why it matters: The IRGC closure declaration, even if not fully enforced, has operationally eliminated commercial transit through Hormuz — the 2/day residual reflects US-escorted or covert movement, not commercial normality. The deal pivot does not change current flow reality until IRGC formally rescinds declaration AND mines are cleared AND safe-transit escort regime is established.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only routing locked through Q3 minimum. Even if MOU signed this weekend, earliest realistic Hormuz commercial normalisation is late June at absolute best. Lock freight contracts and do not pre-position Hormuz-dependent inventory before Hard-tier confirmation (signed document + IRGC rescission). Red Sea/Bab al-Mandeb is not a fallback — Houthi ban remains operational.

Sources: IRGC/IRNA (Tier 5, kinetic confirmation upgrades to Hard), CENTCOM (Tier 1), NBC News liveblog (Tier 2), UKMTO via The National (Tier 1/2), Wilson Center (Tier 2), Trading Economics (Tier 2), Reuters via Investing.com (Tier 1).

Military

Severe ↓ Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM completed strikes on Iranian air-defence, ground-control stations, surveillance radar sites near Hormuz on 10 Jun — "proportional response to unjustified Iranian aggression" (CENTCOM official public release, Tier 1).
  • IRGC struck 18 US targets (Bahrain Fifth Fleet, Ali Al-Salem/Ahmad Al-Jaber Kuwait, Al-Azraq Jordan F-35 hangars); Jordan, Kuwait, Bahrain intercepted most munitions (NBC News/NPR/CENTCOM confirmed).
  • Trump cancelled planned 3rd-wave strikes 11 Jun citing breakthrough (AP/PBS/Washington Times confirmed); CENTCOM: "remains vigilant, postured to defend." No new US strikes reported as of 06:01 UTC 12 Jun.

Why it matters: The 48h kinetic pause is the first genuine cessation of US offensive action since 9 Jun. However, it is Trump-directed — reversible on social media post if deal collapses. IRGC still has un-rescinded closure declaration and kinetic enforcement capability active. Both sides retain escalation options; the pause is politically contingent, not structurally constrained.

Supply-chain implication: GCC energy terminal strike probability drops from 0.55 floor to ~0.20 during active ceasefire talks, but does not go to zero — IRGC has not stood down. Methodology update: when US offensive pause is in effect AND Trump cites diplomatic breakthrough, lower GCC terminal strike probability floor to 0.20 for 72h. Revert to 0.50 floor if talks collapse or Iran rejects deal publicly.

Sources: CENTCOM official release (Tier 1), AP/PBS (Tier 1), NBC News liveblog (Tier 2), NPR (Tier 2), Washington Times (Tier 3), Times of Israel liveblog (Tier 2).

Diplomatic

Concerning ↑ Confidence: Medium
  • Trump 11 Jun (Truth Social): "discussions and final points have been, in both concept and great detail, approved" by US, Israel, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Turkey, Pakistan, Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, Egypt and others. Signing ceremony "maybe in Europe" this weekend; Vance/Witkoff/Kushner to attend (AP/ABC News confirmed).
  • Counter-signal — Iran: Senior Iranian official told MS NOW "Iran has not yet agreed to any MOU or framework." Iran FM has not publicly confirmed. Israeli PM Netanyahu: "Israel is not party to the MOU." A senior Israeli official says Jerusalem "has not been made aware of a finalized deal" (Times of Israel liveblog, Tier 2).
  • 60-day ceasefire framework terms per Wikipedia/AP: immediate halt to hostilities, Hormuz reopening, 15–20 day negotiation period on nuclear; no enrichment; US lifts naval blockade. Iran's semi-official Fars reported Tehran "likely to accept deal" (Soft tier, treat as Iranian signalling, not confirmation).

Why it matters: Trump has claimed "breakthrough" multiple times since April without it materialising (April 7, May 24, May 27). The 11 Jun announcement is the strongest signal yet — cancelled strikes + named party approvals + signing ceremony plan — but Iran has not confirmed and Netanyahu's office is distancing. Treat as Soft tier until Iran FM or Khamenei publicly confirms OR signing ceremony occurs.

Supply-chain implication: Do not adjust routing or inventory posture based on Trump announcement alone. Trigger for posture change: (1) Iran FM public confirmation OR (2) signing ceremony with both US and Iranian officials present. Lebanon remains outside deal — Hezbollah/IDF operations continue regardless, keeping Red Sea threat structurally elevated.

Sources: AP (Tier 1) via PBS/Yahoo, ABC News liveblog (Tier 1/2), MS NOW liveblog (Tier 2), Times of Israel liveblog (Tier 2), Washington Times (Tier 3), Wikipedia 2026 Iran war ceasefire (Tier 3/4, cross-referenced).

Infrastructure & Markets

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Brent $89.25/bbl on 12 Jun (–1.25% day-on-day, –4% on 11 Jun post-Trump announcement, –15.5% past month); WTI $85.91 (–2.05%). 52-week range $58.72–$126.41 (Investing.com). Asian stocks rallied on deal optimism (Reuters, 12 Jun).
  • EIA STEO (released 9 Jun, Tier 1): assumes Hormuz remains closed near-term; forecasts Brent avg $105/bbl in June–July; Middle East crude production down >11 mb/d vs pre-conflict. Assumption now in question if deal signed.
  • GCC energy terminals: no new confirmed strikes in 11–12 Jun window. Mahshahr and Asaluyeh petrochemical outage ongoing (IDF/Argus confirmed 8 Jun). QatarEnergy Ras Laffan force majeure active — no restart date. Kharg Island export terminal central to deal terms.

Why it matters: Market is pricing a 60–70% probability of deal materialising based on Brent dropping to $89 (vs $105 EIA baseline). However, traders are correct to be cautious: even a signed deal requires mine clearance, production restart, and insurance premium normalisation before flows recover — minimum 15–20 days per Reuters analysis. Price downside surprise risk is now the dominant tail, not upside spike.

Supply-chain implication: Lock LNG and petrochemical forwards at current spot before any confirmed deal triggers further price falls. SW Iran feedstock outage locked through Q3 regardless of deal outcome — damage to Mahshahr/Asaluyeh infrastructure not repaired in days. US crude inventories declining seventh consecutive week (EIA); SPR releases have dampened price but not eliminated structural tightness.

Sources: Trading Economics (Tier 2), EIA STEO June 2026 (Tier 1), Investing.com/Reuters (Tier 1/2), Argus Media (Tier 2).

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • UKMTO confirmed armed-boat approach on cargo vessel SW of Balhaf, Yemen 10 Jun; exchange of fire; assailants broke off (The National, UKMTO Tier 1). Separate UKMTO incident: tanker engine-room fire 20 nm NE of Sohar, Oman 10 Jun; cause unclear.
  • Probable Houthi attack on Liberian-flagged Greek-owned vessel 12 Jun (Wikipedia Houthi attacks on commercial vessels, entry truncated; unconfirmed at 06:01 UTC — treat as watchlist pending UKMTO/CENTCOM corroboration).
  • Houthi "complete ban on enemy navigation" in Red Sea active; Norderney + MSC Tavvishi struck 8–9 Jun (Wilson Center/CENTCOM confirmed). BIMCO: US/Israel-linked vessels "likely struggle to obtain cover at any price." UNSC Res. 2812 reporting mandate extended to 15 Jul (13-0-2).

Why it matters: Even if US–Iran deal is signed, Lebanon is explicitly excluded — Houthi operations are tied to "Unity of Fronts" doctrine linking maritime action to Lebanese and Palestinian conflict trajectory, not just Iran ceasefire. Bab al-Mandeb threat is structurally independent of Hormuz deal. Saudi Yanbu (7 mb/d via EWP) remains exposed: 70–75% of that crude transits Bab al-Mandeb.

Supply-chain implication: Cape of Good Hope routing is structural through Q3 regardless of Hormuz deal. Red Sea is not a Hormuz fallback — it's a separate risk domain. Yanbu crude exposure is the underappreciated second-order risk: if Houthis escalate against Saudi vessels, the alternative to Hormuz loses capacity. Lock Cape freight capacity through September now.

Sources: UKMTO via The National (Tier 1), Wilson Center (Tier 2), CENTCOM (Tier 1), UNSC press release (Tier 1), BIMCO via HouseofSaud analysis (Tier 3), Wikipedia Houthi attacks (Tier 3/4, unconfirmed 12 Jun entry).

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
