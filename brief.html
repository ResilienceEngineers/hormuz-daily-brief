On Day 100, the US-Iran-Israel war entered a dangerous new sub-cycle: CENTCOM shot down four Iranian attack drones threatening Strait of Hormuz traffic on 5–6 June, then struck coastal surveillance radar sites at Goruk and Qeshm Island — the fourth publicly acknowledged US "self-defence" strike since the April ceasefire. Iran responded by firing seven ballistic missiles at Kuwait and Bahrain (six intercepted, one failed). MOU negotiations are now structurally deadlocked: Iran demands immediate release of $24 billion in frozen assets as a precondition, while the Trump administration is pivoting to redirect those same assets to Gulf allies for war-damage reconstruction. The Lebanon precondition remains unresolved — Hezbollah continues kinetic operations after rejecting the 3 June ceasefire framework, and talks are not confirmed to resume until 22 June. Strait of Hormuz commercial transits remain at approximately 10 per day vs. a 95/day pre-war baseline, with Brent crude near $93 reflecting diplomatic uncertainty rather than a meaningful de-escalation signal.

Maritime Security

Severe → Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM (Tier 1): shot down 4 Iranian one-way attack drones threatening Strait of Hormuz maritime traffic 5 Jun; struck coastal surveillance radar at Goruk + Qeshm Island (4th self-defence strike since Apr ceasefire). Two more drones downed 6 Jun.
  • straits.live (Tier 2): ~10 commercial transits/day vs ~95/day baseline as of 6 Jun; AIS interference persists; dark-ship activity unquantifiable. 53 vessels currently in transit in broader area (small craft + coastal included).
  • Houthis claimed Elbella + AAL Genoa strikes Red Sea 7 Jun; CENTCOM confirmed 4 ASBMs fired into Red Sea (Wilson Center / CENTCOM 7 Jun). No UKMTO vessel-damage confirmation yet. MARAD 2026-006 active.

Why it matters: The CENTCOM "self-defence strike" cycle at Hormuz is now institutionalised at 4+ confirmed incidents since April — each exchange degrades Iranian sensor/radar infrastructure but also hardens Iran's justification for renewed access-control doctrine. With ~10 transits/day, Hormuz is functionally closed; carrier confidence requires MOU + mine-clearance + 30 days demonstrated safety before service restoration.

Implication: Cape-only routing is structural through Q3 minimum. Any supply-chain plan relying on Hormuz opening before EOD August should be treated as optimistic tail. Bab al-Mandeb remains a secondary risk with Houthi cluster activity on 7 Jun — dual-chokepoint closure is the operational baseline, not a tail risk.

Military

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM (Tier 1): Iran fired 7 ballistic missiles toward Kuwait + Bahrain overnight 5–6 Jun; 6 intercepted, 1 failed to reach target. US forces subsequently struck Iranian coastal radar at Goruk + Qeshm. Iran condemned strikes as "clear violation of April 8 ceasefire" (Iranian FM via ABC News).
  • Trump (NBC Meet the Press, 5 Jun): Iran retains ~21–22% of pre-war missile arsenal. DroneXL/CENTCOM: IRGC drone activity down ~95%, missile volume down ~90% since war began — but whether this reflects depletion or deliberate pacing is unresolved.
  • Lebanon: IDF killed Lebanese army soldiers including senior officer (Israeli strike 6–7 Jun); Hezbollah drone injured 4 IDF reservists. Two Israeli soldiers killed in separate incidents. Lebanese health ministry: 3,593 killed in Lebanon since 2 March.

Why it matters: The tit-for-tat pattern is now at its most compressed tempo — drones at Hormuz, missile salvo at Gulf states, radar strikes, Iranian FM condemnation, repeat. This is the "frozen attrition at elevated kinetic intensity" version of Scenario B, not the benign version. Interceptor depletion (Patriot PAC-3 ~$4M per intercept vs. IRGC drone ~$2,500) is the critical asymmetric variable.

Implication: Any further IRGC strike reaching a Gulf energy terminal or causing ≥1-day outage would move threat level to 5/5. The missile-and-drone exchange cycle is not de-escalating; it is stabilising at a higher floor with each exchange. Gulf-state air-defence interceptor inventory should be treated as a binding constraint on this equilibrium's duration.

Diplomatic

Concerning ↓ Confidence: High
  • Bloomberg/Reuters (Tier 2, 6 Jun): MOU talks deadlocked over $24B in frozen Iranian assets. Iran demands release as precondition; US redirecting same assets to Gulf allies for reconstruction. Treasury Bessent directed cost-assessment team. Iran advisor Rezaei told CNN deal hinges on $24B release.
  • Pakistan back-channel (Tier 3, 6–7 Jun): Interior Minister Naqvi in Tehran carrying "special letter" from Pakistan army chief + PM to Khamenei. Lebanese army commander in Islamabad. Pakistan track is the only active diplomatic mechanism identified.
  • Lebanon precondition: Hezbollah kinetics ongoing through 7 Jun (drone vs IDF; Israeli strikes on Lebanese army). Next Israel-Lebanon round: 22 Jun. HoC Library: "no further [US-Iran] talks confirmed." OFAC issued fresh Iran LNG shadow-fleet sanctions 6 Jun.

Why it matters: The $24B frozen-asset question was always the financial keystone of any MOU. US redirecting those assets to Gulf allies is not merely a bargaining tactic — it structurally removes the primary Iranian economic incentive for signing. If Iran perceives its leverage asset is being seized rather than returned, rational choice theory points to walkout rather than concession.

Implication: MOU probability has fallen structurally. The Pakistan back-channel is a leading observable — if Naqvi returns with a Khamenei-level signal that bridges the asset impasse, MOU probability could recover. Absent that signal by EOD 12 June, Scenario A (negotiated framework) should be revised below 20%.

Alternative Routes

Severe ↑ Confidence: High
  • Houthis claimed strikes on Elbella (Malta-flagged container) + AAL Genoa (Cyprus-flagged cargo) in Red Sea 7 Jun with ballistic/naval missiles + drones; CENTCOM confirmed 4 ASBMs launched toward Red Sea (Wilson Center). No independent vessel-damage confirmation yet — vessel operators should treat as active threat, not confirmed damage.
  • IRGC Quds Force cmdr Qaani warned 2 Jun: Bab al-Mandeb will face "same traffic restrictions as Hormuz" if Lebanon war continues (IRGC via GlobalSecurity). MARAD 2026-006 active. Red Sea daily transits significantly below 250/day pre-crisis baseline.
  • Cape routing cost structure locked in: MSC +$1.2k/TEU since March; Maersk +$1.0k/TEU; Hapag-Lloyd surcharges on top of Red Sea contingency adders. Saudi EW pipeline ~5 mb/d ceiling. Cape adds ~$40–50M/week fleet-wide.

Why it matters: Both major alternative corridors are simultaneously degraded. Bab al-Mandeb is not a Hormuz backup — it is a second active threat vector. IRGC Quds Force warning about Bab al-Mandeb closure is the highest-authority threat signal yet from Tehran on that chokepoint, corroborated by Houthi activity on 7 Jun.

Implication: Cape routing is the only reliable alternative, but it is capacity-constrained and adds 10–14 days. Destination-port buffer inventory at origin-side is the only resilience lever available. Any supply chain relying on last-minute Red Sea rerouting as a fallback to Cape routing has no remaining buffer.

Outlook & Scenarios

Concerning → Confidence: Medium
  • Counter-signal (anti-confirmation bias): Pakistan diplomatic back-channel is active — Naqvi in Tehran with Khamenei-level letter; Lebanese army commander in Islamabad; diplomatic track not dead. Brent at $93 (not $110+) signals markets still pricing partial resolution probability. Trump said "deal is stuck but Iran has to make tough decisions" — framing as progress, not collapse.
  • Escalation signal: US redirecting $24B frozen assets to Gulf reconstruction + new OFAC LNG sanctions 6 Jun simultaneously removes Iran's primary deal incentive and tightens economic pressure. CENTCOM 4th self-defence strike in ~60 days = structural kinetic exchange cycle, not aberration.
  • Scenario B (frozen attrition at elevated floor) is the modal outcome because both sides are absorbing costs without either side having sufficient incentive to fully escalate or capitulate: Iran retains 21–22% missile stock (Trump); US domestic political pressure from gas prices; Iran economic crunch from oil embargo.

Why it matters: Day 100 marks a structural inflection: the financial impasse ($24B assets) has now become a zero-sum game where one side's diplomatic inducement is the other side's war reparation. Resolution requires a bridging mechanism (third-party escrow, phased release tied to MOU milestones) that neither side has publicly proposed.

Implication: Supply chains should plan for Scenario B as 6–8 week structural floor: Hormuz closed, Red Sea degraded, Cape routing mandatory, Brent $85–$100 range. Scenario C (re-escalation) trigger to watch is IRGC strike on energy terminal causing ≥1-day outage — that moves threat to 5/5 and Brent back toward $105–$115 within 48h.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
