Day 93 opens with the US–Iran memorandum of understanding still unsigned: Trump exited his 29 May Situation Room session without announcement and laid out fresh demands on 30 May — unrestricted Hormuz, full HEU handover, and nuclear programme termination — that Iran's state media rejected within hours. The IAEA simultaneously reported on 31 May that Iran has amassed a record quantity of military-grade enriched uranium, hardening the nuclear sticking point precisely when principal sign-offs are the only remaining gate. Physical conditions at the Strait are unchanged: ~4 PortWatch transits/day vs a 95/day pre-crisis baseline, ~404 vessels anchored, UKMTO Critical advisory active, war-risk insurance at 8× pre-crisis; however Bloomberg reports shipowners are "increasingly optimistic" after CENTCOM began providing navigation advice to vessels attempting transit. The Houthi commercial-shipping standdown holds at Day 23+, but Lebanon kinetics are intensifying — IDF advancing past the Litani, Hezbollah launching drone and rocket barrages into northern Israel — keeping the MOU Lebanon clause the hardest outstanding sticking point between Washington and Tehran.

Maritime Security

Severe → Confidence: High

  • Positive signal: Bloomberg 29–30 May — shipowners "increasingly optimistic" after CENTCOM began providing navigation advice to vessels attempting transit. Two shipowners in contact with US military forces. CENTCOM: "not escorting but advising."

Counter-signal: shipowner optimism building is genuine and may translate to cautious trial transits in coming days.

Implication: Do not pre-position for Hormuz reopening ahead of signed MOU with verified mine-clearance initiation. DHL's 4–6 month normalisation estimate is the operational planning floor. Maintain Cape of Good Hope as default routing.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
