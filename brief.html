CENTCOM confirmed a fifth round of strikes against Iran at 03:56 BST 13 July — targeting air-defence systems, coastal radar, missile and drone capabilities, and small boats — after Iran fired on a commercial vessel in the Strait of Hormuz on Sunday and expanded ballistic-missile and drone barrages to six Gulf states (Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan, UAE, and Oman's Musandam exclave). Why it matters: The IRGC's willingness to continue attacking commercial vessels through five consecutive US strike waves signals that the IRGC — not the Pezeshkian government or Araghchi — is the operational decision-maker on Hormuz. Each new vessel attack triggers a CENTCOM response under the existing MOU "failed test" framework, creating a self-reinforcing kinetic loop that diplomatic channels cannot interrupt without IRGC operational stand-down.

Supply-chain implication: All Hormuz-dependent cargo flows — crude oil, LNG, fertilisers, refined products — remain effectively on Cape-only routing. WTO PortWatch confirms LNG and fertiliser AIS flows near zero; crude oil AIS flows at "isolated shipments" level. The 17 Jul GL-X waiver expiry adds a hard deadline: if non-renewed, Iranian oil sales freeze and the remaining AIS-dark transit incentive structure collapses further. Buyers exposed to Persian Gulf crude or LNG should assume 3–6 week additional lead times minimum and re-check force majeure clause coverage now.

The IRGC's targeting of Musandam — Oman's strategic Hormuz exclave — is the most significant geographic escalation of the overnight period: Oman is the primary diplomatic facilitator and a strike on its territory signals IRGC willingness to punish mediators. No US fatalities and no strike on Gulf-state civilian infrastructure keeps the ceiling below Scenario C full lock-in — but the ceiling is rising.

Supply-chain implication: Gulf state port approaches (Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar) now carry active interdiction risk; vessel operators transiting within range of IRGC missile corridors should treat Gulf approaches as active incident zones. Bahrain's 5th Fleet base operational continuity is under recurring test. No confirmed damage to Gulf port infrastructure yet — but every additional IRGC barrage raises the probability of an infrastructure-proximity strike that would force port closures.

Diplomatic Track

Severe ↓ Confidence: Medium
  • Iran–Oman: Araghchi in Muscat 11 Jul; Oman drafted two-corridor proposal for Hormuz (CNN Tier 1 source); Iran–Oman agreed "further technical and political talks" (ONA Tier 2). Proposal not yet formally accepted by either Iran or US.
  • Iran–US direct channel: CBS News (Tier 2) — "no indication of any new direct contact between US and Iranian officials"; Iran conditions talk resumption on prior US Hormuz-compliance. Pakistan–Qatar co-mediation nominally active; no firm session date or location.
  • Mojtaba Khamenei: Mashhad Imam Reza shrine 3-day memorial 10–12 Jul (Bloomberg Tier 2); statement did not specify his attendance; no Tier 1-3 confirmation of public appearance. CNN source 9 Jul: "unlikely to attend any ceremonies, including private family burial." IRGC and presidential track operating in parallel without confirmed supreme leader visible authority.

Why it matters: The back-channel's survival depends on whether Oman can convert its two-corridor draft into a mutually accepted operational framework before the kinetic cycle fully forecloses the space. Iran's precondition structure — US must demonstrate Hormuz compliance before Iran returns to direct talks — is logically circular given that IRGC is the entity controlling Hormuz and is simultaneously escalating. This is the defining structural trap: the diplomatic channel requires IRGC stand-down, but the entity that could order a stand-down (Mojtaba Khamenei) has not made a confirmed public appearance in 136 days.

Supply-chain implication: No diplomatic development in the 72h window materially reduces Hormuz transit risk for cargo planning purposes. Force majeure provisions should remain active. Any firm US–Iran direct session announcement with a date and location would be the single highest-value de-escalation signal — re-score all corridor risk assessments within 24h of such announcement.

Alternative Routes

  • Bab al-Mandeb / Red Sea: Security Council Report (Tier 1, Jul 2026) — "no new incidents reported in 2026" before this brief period; Houthi "ban" on Israeli-linked ships declared 8 Jun; UKMTO-confirmed skiff gunfire exchange near Hodeidah 5 Jul; IRGC Quds Force Qaani "security belt" Hormuz-to-Bab al-Mandeb warning (9 Jun) — unactivated but standing.   • Suez Canal: UKMTO/JMIC confirms transits continuing normally; GNSS interference near Yanbu (no operational impact). Saudi Yanbu: sole secure Red Sea crude export point; not directly targeted. EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extended to Feb 2027 (coverage GoA/Red Sea).

Why it matters: The alt-route system is absorbing the Hormuz closure but is not at saturation — Cape capacity is stressed but not breached. The critical risk in the next 14 days is a Houthi full-campaign resumption that activates the dual-chokepoint scenario: simultaneous Hormuz closure + Bab al-Mandeb interdiction would exhaust Cape routing capacity and trigger price rationing in refined products within 30 days. The IRGC "security belt" warning from Qaani remains the structural permission signal for Houthi activation.

Supply-chain implication: Maintain Cape-only routing for all non-essential cargoes. Salalah and Jeddah port calls should include 3–5 day buffer for congestion. Do not pre-position inventory in Red Sea transit expecting a rapid Suez resumption — the Houthi threat environment is elevated and the activation trigger (Iran escalation) is live. Saudi Yanbu is the one secure Red Sea crude export point; buyers sourcing Saudi Red Sea crude should confirm Yanbu-specific routing and insurance cover.

Outlook & Scenarios

Crisis ↓ Confidence: Medium
  • Scenario C (Re-escalation lock-in) now modal at 65% (↑5pp from Day 135): five consecutive CENTCOM strike waves; six-state IRGC barrage; Iran's circular precondition structure for talk resumption; GL-X expiry 17 Jul; Mojtaba Khamenei 136-day non-appearance.
  • Scenario B (Frozen attrition / back-channel survives) at 28% (↓4pp): Oman two-corridor draft is the sole remaining structural mechanism; Pakistan–Qatar mediation nominally active; Trump said willing to keep "negotiations open." Requires IRGC operational pause not yet signalled.
  • Scenario A (Negotiated framework) at 7% (↓1pp): would require public US–Iran joint statement AND AIS-on transit increase AND Mojtaba Khamenei confirmed public authority — none of three observable conditions met.

Why it matters: The structural shift from Day 135 is that the IRGC has now demonstrated willingness to strike six sovereign Gulf states in a single overnight wave — a qualitative escalation beyond the previous four-state pattern. This signals either IRGC command is operating with a wider mandate than previously assessed, or the IRGC is deliberately creating facts on the ground to foreclose diplomatic space before Mojtaba Khamenei can potentially constrain them. Either reading reinforces Scenario C.

Supply-chain implication: Scenario C posture: maintain maximum alt-route buffer; freeze spot purchases at Hormuz-exposed origins; extend force majeure declarations where contractually available; confirm 30-day+ inventory cover for critical refined products and LNG. Scenario B monitoring gate: watch for Oman two-corridor acceptance announcement — if confirmed, reassess Hormuz transit risk within 48h. Scenario A monitoring gate: confirmed Mojtaba public appearance with explicit Hormuz statement — probability of occurrence within 7 days assessed at <15%.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
