Maritime Security

Severe ↑ Conf: High
  • Hormuz ~4 transits/day (IMF PortWatch 24 May baseline) vs 95/day pre-crisis; IRGC PGSA permit enforcement active; ~1,550 vessels stranded, ~22,500 mariners trapped (CENTCOM/IMO).
  • Houthi standdown broken 1 June: Abliani (oil tanker, Red Sea), Maina (cargo, Red Sea + Arabian Sea), Al Oraiq (LNG carrier, Indian Ocean) all struck — Wikipedia Houthi attacks tracker confirmed; next attacks pattern 5 June (Roza/Vantage Dream).
  • MARAD 2026-006 active; UKMTO JMIC critical advisory; war-risk insurance 8× pre-crisis; 6 P&I clubs withdrawn cover; MSC and Maersk Cape-only routing; Suez Canal traffic near zero.

Why it matters: The simultaneous compression of both primary energy arteries — Hormuz physically closed and Red Sea standdown broken — eliminates the two main routing options that had been supporting residual commercial flow. There is no third primary alternative; Cape of Good Hope is the structural baseline, adding 10–14 days and ~$1M in fuel per voyage.

Supply-chain implication: Suspend Red Sea routing with no timeline for reinstatement. Maintain Cape-only posture. Do not pre-position cargo for Hormuz transit ahead of signed, verified MOU with mine-clearance trigger. War-risk insurance pricing will not reset meaningfully until ≥14 days of post-signing stability.

Sources: IMF PortWatch via straits.live · Wikipedia Houthi attacks on commercial vessels (updated 2 Jun) · MARAD 2026-006 · UKMTO JMIC (critical, active) · CNBC/Reuters (2 Jun)

Supply-chain implication: Fertilizer and urea forward procurement is urgent — >30% of global urea passes through Hormuz. Lock Q3–Q4 supply now. Jet fuel surcharges (+95% North America) are structural, not temporary. No recovery signal available without MOU signing and 30-day mine-clearance trigger.

Alternative Routes

Severe ↑ Conf: High
  • Houthi standdown broken 1 June: Abliani (oil tanker), Maina (cargo), Al Oraiq (LNG) all struck; Wikipedia tracker shows pattern continuing with 5 June attacks (Roza, Vantage Dream) already logged — Red Sea is not a viable alternative route.
  • Saudi East–West pipeline (Yanbu) saturated at ~5 mb/d maximum capacity; Yanbu previously struck in earlier IRGC waves; currently operational but single-point-of-failure for Saudi Red Sea exports.
  • Cape of Good Hope structural baseline: adds ~10–14 days/voyage, ~$1M fuel, fleet-wide cost ~$40–50M/week above pre-crisis; MSC $1.2k/TEU Cape surcharge; Suez Canal revenues collapsed; Cape-route congestion building.

Why it matters: The Houthi standdown break eliminates the last operating buffer that had partially offset Hormuz closure. Supply chains are now wholly dependent on Cape routing for all commodity classes — a single-route dependency with no redundancy. Yanbu pipeline saturation means Saudi Arabia cannot compensate for Hormuz closure beyond ~5 mb/d.

Supply-chain implication: Cape-only posture is mandatory. Build transit-time buffer of at least 14 additional days into all routing plans. No Red Sea transition planning until MARAD 2026-006 is formally lifted and UKMTO advisory downgrades from Critical. Watch Yanbu for secondary IRGC targeting as a scenario escalation signal.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
