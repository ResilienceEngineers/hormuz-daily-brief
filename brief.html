Six categories

01

Maritime / Hormuz

Severe → Conf: High
  • IRGC Navy reporting 25–35 vessels/day transiting under permit/coordination (PressTV/ISNA 22–24 May; corroborated by Reuters PGSA vetting documentation). Baseline: 95/day.
  • IRGC "controlled maritime zone" 500km crescent (Jask/Sirik to beyond Greater Tunb Island) formally mapped and published; PGSA 40+ question vetting form required for all vessels.
  • Trump explicitly stated 25 May: "The Blockade will remain in full force and effect until an agreement is reached, certified, and signed" — dual blockade (IRGC Hormuz + US on Iranian ports) confirmed ongoing. ~1,550+ vessels stranded; ~20,000+ seafarers.
  • ADNOC CEO (CNBC 24 May): full oil flow restoration not before Q1–Q2 2027; 4+ months to reach 80% even after deal signed.

Why this matters: The IRGC permit system has become structurally self-reinforcing — vetting infrastructure, revenue stream (~$2M/vessel reported), and sovereignty narrative are all now embedded. Even a signed MOU triggers a 30-day phase-out, not immediate normalisation. The US blockade on Iranian ports is the reciprocal constraint that Iran is demanding be lifted simultaneously — creating a sequencing deadlock that persists even if MOU is signed.

Implication: Treat Hormuz as operationally closed for planning purposes through at least end-June 2026 regardless of MOU outcome. War-risk insurance (8× pre-crisis; 6 P&I clubs withdrawn) will not normalise until IRGC permit system is verifiably dismantled and mines cleared — which cannot happen inside 30 days. Do not price in "reopening" premiums until signed MOU + IRGC mine-clearance commencement confirmed.

Sources: PressTV/ISNA 22–24 May 2026 (IRGC Navy statements); Reuters PGSA documentation; CNN/CNBC 24 May 2026; UKMTO advisory (active); MARAD 2026-006 (active).

Military / IRGC

Severe → Conf: High

  • Trump confirmed (CNBC/NPR 24 May): "held off from renewing attacks on Iran this week while serious negotiations were underway." US forces remain in region, Memorial Day recall rosters stand down pending MOU.
  • Israeli security cabinet met twice on Iran (Al Jazeera 21 May); Israeli Defence Minister Katz: "ready to bomb back to the Stone Ages" on US green-light. Israel "very unhappy with emerging deal" (NPR source); Netanyahu issued public statement demanding dismantlement standard incompatible with current MOU.
  • IRGC missile recovery: ~70% pre-war stockpile (prior CENTCOM assessment); 30 of 33 Hormuz missile sites restored. IRGC warned any return to war "will feature many more surprises."

Why this matters: The clean NOTAM expiry is the strongest Hard-tier signal yet that the diplomatic track is holding — it confirms Iran chose not to use the NOTAM window as a pre-kinetics signal. However, the military-on-standby posture (both US and IRGC) remains structurally unchanged. Israel is the primary Scenario C activation risk: Netanyahu's public dismantlement standard creates a domestic political constraint that makes Israeli acceptance of the current MOU draft structurally difficult without a US override.

Implication: The 72h window through 28 May remains the kinetics-risk window. If MOU collapses on Lebanon or frozen-asset clause, Trump's "serious negotiations" rationale for holding off evaporates. Do not treat clean NOTAM expiry as structural de-escalation — it is a process signal, not a structural one.

Sources: defconlevel.com / Iranian CAA NOTAM A1010/26 (22–25 May 2026); CNBC/NPR 24 May 2026; Al Jazeera 21 May 2026; NPR 24 May 2026 (NPR/source).

The next 72h either confirms A is becoming modal or reverts B to modal.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.

  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
