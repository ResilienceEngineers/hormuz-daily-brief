Threat level
4 / 5 · Severe
On the morning of Day 86, the US–Iran diplomatic track has produced its most concrete deliverable since the April ceasefire: Axios (confirmed by Reuters, NPR, and Times of Israel) reports both sides are close to signing a 60-day MOU under which the Strait of Hormuz would reopen toll-free, Iran would clear mines, and the US would lift its port blockade and issue sanctions waivers — with nuclear talks deferred to the 60-day window. Trump declared the deal "largely negotiated" on 23 May evening; an announcement is expected Sunday 24 May. However, Iran's Foreign Ministry simultaneously insists three issues remain unresolved: no nuclear discussion at this stage, frozen asset release as a precondition, and Iranian management of Hormuz — and Iran's semi-official Fars news agency called the Axios text "an American wish list." Netanyahu convened an emergency security meeting, calling the emerging terms a "very big problem for Israel." The MOU is unsigned, the Iranian-side confirmation is partial, and the structural gap between US "relief for performance" and Iran's "assets first" position has not been publicly bridged; any of these fault lines could collapse the deal before Sunday's close.

Six categories

01

Maritime / Hormuz

Severe → Conf: High
  • IRGC Persian Gulf Strait Authority "controlled maritime zone" remains in force; ~2–5 vessels/day transiting (2% of 95/day pre-crisis baseline). No AIS-confirmed increase as of 06:00 UTC. Source: IMF PortWatch / straits.live (Tier 2).
  • MOU draft (Axios, Tier 1; confirmed Reuters/NPR) specifies: Hormuz open toll-free during 60-day period; Iran clears mines; US lifts port blockade + issues sanctions waivers. "Relief for performance" sequencing means mine clearance must precede blockade lift. No signed text as of 06:00 UTC.
  • Iran (Fars news, Tier 5 as Iranian state position): insists Hormuz remains under Iranian management; frozen assets must be released before any Hormuz concession. This is the primary MOU fault line on maritime terms. NOTAM A1010/26 expires 25 May 08:30Z — timing consistent with MOU announcement window OR pre-kinetics clearing signal.

Why it matters: The Hormuz closure is now in Day 86 with ~1,550 vessels stranded and ~20,000 seafarers aboard (IMO). A signed MOU does not immediately restore commercial traffic — mine clearance, IRGC operational compliance, and insurance re-certification each require independent verification. The "announcement effect" (risk premium easing on news) is distinct from actual physical reopening, which analysts estimate takes 4–6 weeks minimum after a signed MOU.

Supply-chain implication: Do not stand down Hormuz-alternative logistics until: (1) MOU signed text published, (2) AIS shows ≥30 vessels/day sustained over 72 hours, (3) at least one major P&I club restores war-risk cover. All three conditions remain unmet as of today. Treat current closure as planning baseline through at least 27 May.

Military / IRGC

Severe ↑ Conf: Medium

Fifth Gulf-state deferral now implicit in MOU trajectory — mechanism not yet exhausted but approaching structural limit.
  • CBS News (22 May, Tier 2): US military and intelligence officials cancelled Memorial Day plans; recall rosters updated; strike planning still active as of 22 May. No indication of stand-down order as of 06:00 UTC 24 May. Military posture remains dual-track.

IRGC military adviser Mohsen Rezaei: "Armed forces' fingers are on the trigger." Iran missile recovery ~70% pre-war stockpile per prior CENTCOM assessment.

Why it matters: The MOU trajectory is the first genuine convergence toward Scenario A (Negotiated framework), but the military apparatus on both sides remains fully activated. The US has not issued a stand-down; Israel has not been reassured. The structural risk is a deal that collapses at the last moment — reactivating a kinetics window that is already pre-positioned. The fifth deferral application (implicit in MOU trajectory) is unprecedented; the methodology flag from Day 85 stands: P(deferral) calibrated at 0.35 for any further same-window calculations.

Supply-chain implication: Hold decisions on any logistics or procurement commitments contingent on Hormuz reopening until MOU text is confirmed signed. The "50/50" framing Trump used with Axios on 23 May — deal or bombs — is the operational baseline. A collapse of the MOU before Sunday's close is not a tail risk; it is a live scenario with meaningful probability.

Diplomatic

Concerning ↑ Conf: Medium

  • Iran FM Baghaei confirmed MOU finalization underway; told IRNA: nuclear issues "not a focus at this stage"; "3–4 more days." Separately, Iran's semi-official Fars cited "three remaining serious issues": no nuclear at this stage, frozen assets as precondition, Iranian management of Hormuz (Xinhua/Fars, 24 May). Why it matters: This is the closest the parties have come to a formal agreement since the war began on 28 February. The MOU structure (60-day ceasefire extension + Hormuz reopening + nuclear talks deferred) represents Iran's "Hormuz-first, nuclear-later" preferred sequencing, implicitly accepted by the US side. However, the US–Iran gap on the three unresolved Iranian items, combined with Israeli alarm and Republican congressional skepticism, creates multiple veto points that could collapse the deal before or after Sunday's announcement window.

Supply-chain implication: The diplomatic signal is positive but probabilistic. Treat the MOU as a 30–40% probability event for Sunday (Scenario A scenario probability raised from 10% to 30%). Procurement and logistics decisions should not be anchored to "deal done" until signed text is confirmed by both sides. The Iran FM's "3–4 more days" statement introduces a 27–28 May secondary window even if Sunday announcement fails.

Infrastructure

Severe → Conf: High
  • Barakah NPP: no new inner-perimeter attack since outer-perimeter strike 17 May. UAE investigation of Iraqi-territory attribution ongoing (UAE MoD/AP confirmed). IAEA Grossi "grave concern" standing; inner-perimeter hit remains a standalone Threat 4→5 threshold. Iraqi proxy chain: investigation ordered by PM Al Zaidi; no rapid deterrence expected.
  • Brent crude: ~$103/barrel as of 22 May (down from $114 peak on 5 May; down ~2% week-on-week on deal optimism). US SPR drawdown at record pace — 10M barrels withdrawn last week (CNBC, Tier 2). Saudi Aramco CEO Nasser (Tier 2): "If Hormuz stays blocked beyond mid-June, normalization lasts into 2027." Deal announcement would send Brent sharply lower but not to pre-war levels.
  • Against-trend signal: Even with a signed MOU, infrastructure recovery timeline is measured in months — mine clearance, port restocking, insurance reset, and stranded vessel processing are all sequential dependencies. The "announcement effect" on oil price is not the same as supply restoration.

Why it matters: The energy infrastructure disruption is now structural, not merely tactical. SPR drawdown is at historically unprecedented pace, signaling that commercial buffer is thinning. Even the best-case MOU scenario (signed Sunday) would not normalize energy markets until Q3 2026 at earliest (Aramco CEO's assessment). The Barakah inner-perimeter threat remains a standing escalation threshold regardless of diplomatic trajectory.

Supply-chain implication: Do not price in supply normalization before Q3 2026 even in deal scenario. Extend war-risk review to nuclear-facility perimeter (Barakah inner-perimeter hit = immediate Threat 5 standalone trigger regardless of MOU status). Inventory buffer depletion is the primary medium-term risk; procurement at current elevated prices is preferable to unavailability at higher prices if MOU collapses.

Scenario Outlook

Concerning ↑ Conf: Medium
  • Scenario A (Negotiated framework) raised from 10% to 30% (+20pp): MOU "largely negotiated" per Trump (Truth Social 23 May); Axios exclusive confirmed by Reuters Pakistan source and Iran FM Baghaei. This is the most concrete diplomatic deliverable of the war. However: unsigned, Iran's three unresolved items, Israeli opposition, and Republican skepticism are all active veto points.
  • Scenario B (Frozen attrition) reduced from 44% to 42% (−2pp): the MOU trajectory reduces the probability of pure frozen stalemate but does not eliminate it — if MOU collapses without kinetics, B remains the fallback. The 60-day framework, even if signed, is itself a form of extended attrition with reduced violence.
  • Scenario C (Re-escalation) reduced from 46% to 28% (−18pp): MOU trajectory significantly de-risks C for the next 48–72 hours; but any of the three Iranian unresolved items, Israeli unilateral action, or IRGC non-compliance after signing could reactivate C on short notice. C remains the tail risk with high-severity consequences. Against-trend: Netanyahu's emergency session and Israeli push for resumed strikes is the strongest remaining C-activating signal.

Why it matters: The 20pp shift in Scenario A probability is the largest single-day scenario revision since the April ceasefire announcement. It reflects genuine convergence of US–Iran diplomatic language, mediator confirmation (Pakistan, Qatar), and Iran FM public acknowledgment of MOU finalization work. The revision is calibrated conservatively — the MOU is still not signed, and every previous "close to a deal" moment in this conflict has been followed by collapse (Day 83 pattern note from methodology).

Supply-chain implication: Scenario A at 30% justifies initiating contingency planning for a partial-reopening scenario (not yet for full commitment). Scenario B at 42% remains the planning baseline. Scenario C at 28% requires maintaining full alt-route capacity and war-risk buffer. The 24–27 May window is the highest-probability near-term inflection point of the war; decision-makers should have pre-approved response playbooks for all three outcomes before Sunday's close.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
