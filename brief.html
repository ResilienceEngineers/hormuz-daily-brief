CENTCOM completed its sixth consecutive strike wave on 14 July in a five-hour mission targeting coastal defence and naval infrastructure across Bushehr, Chah Bahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa, and Bandar Abbas — including the first confirmed maritime-drone strikes on an Iranian submarine and a Bandar Abbas maintenance facility. President Trump simultaneously announced reinstatement of the full Iranian naval blockade (effective 16:00 ET 14 July) and declared a 20% toll on all non-Iranian Hormuz cargo, positioning the US as "Guardian of the Strait." Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi responded on X ("20% is too much, we will be fair"), suggesting a residual negotiating posture even as IRGC struck Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, and Oman overnight and fired warning shots at two ships transiting the strait on the morning of 14 July. Brent surged to ~$84/bbl; AIS-visible Hormuz transits collapsed to ~6 ships on 13 July; the Oman two-corridor proposal is effectively overtaken by the blockade reinstatement; Scenario C (re-escalation / full closure lock-in) is now the unambiguous modal outcome at 68%.

01

Maritime & Chokepoints

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • AIS-visible Hormuz transits: ~6 ships 13 Jul (Kpler); zero large-DWT AIS-on via Southern Highway since 7 Jul (Lloyd's List). CENTCOM Wave 6 hit Bandar Abbas naval base — first maritime-drone strike on Iranian submarine and maintenance facility confirmed (Euronews, ToI).
  • US blockade reinstated 14 Jul effective 16:00 ET: "encompasses the entirety of the Iranian coastline to include but not limited to Iranian ports and oil terminals … applies to all vessel traffic, regardless of flag" (US Navy statement via Axios). Neutral vessels warned to depart before enforcement.
  • IRGC fired warning shots at two ships attempting Hormuz transit morning 14 Jul per Iranian state TV (Euronews); overnight 13–14 Jul IRGC struck Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman in retaliation for Wave 5/6. Bab al-Mandeb: UKMTO skiff gunfire 5 Jul, 30 nm SW Hodeidah; no Houthi formal campaign claimed through 06:00 UTC 14 Jul.

Why it matters: The combination of effective Hormuz closure (AIS-dark or blockaded) plus US blockade reinstatement eliminates both the southern NCAGS corridor and the northern IRGC-approved route for compliant Western carriers. The strait is structurally non-transitable for major energy cargoes regardless of which party's authority is cited. The first maritime-drone submarine strike is a qualitative escalation in US capability demonstration.

Supply-chain implication: Hormuz crude, LNG, and fertiliser flows remain at or near zero for AIS-compliant carriers. Cape rerouting is the only viable path for Western-flagged vessels. Any dark transits now face dual risk: IRGC interdiction AND US blockade enforcement. Dual-chokepoint scenario (Hormuz + Bab al-Mandeb) is the key escalation watch.

Military

Crisis ↑ Confidence: High
  • CENTCOM Wave 6 (14 Jul, completed ~05:49 UTC per ToI): five-hour mission against Bushehr, Chah Bahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa, Bandar Abbas — coastal defence systems, missile/drone sites, maritime capabilities. First use of maritime drones against Iranian submarine and Bandar Abbas maintenance facility (CENTCOM via Euronews). Precision munitions; video released.
  • IRGC overnight 13–14 Jul: targeted US bases in Bahrain (5th Fleet Juffair), Jordan, Kuwait (claimed HIMARS launchers destroyed), Oman (FPS long-range aerial radar + vessel detection radar destroyed per IRGC). Bahrain sirens sounded; Kuwait air defences engaged. No confirmed US KIA as of 06:00 UTC 14 Jul.
  • Cumulative campaign tally: six CENTCOM strike waves; estimated 400+ Iranian military targets engaged since 7 Jul. Iran: six-state IRGC barrage across two consecutive nights. CENTCOM: "American forces remain vigilant, lethal, and ready."

Why it matters: Wave 6 marks the first US use of maritime drones offensively against an adversary submarine — a qualitative capability demonstration and precedent. The IRGC's sustained six-state barrage (Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan, UAE, Oman) shows Iran's willingness to strike mediator-state territory (Oman), which the Day 134 heuristic flags as a Scenario B downgrade trigger. The 400+ cumulative target count suggests CENTCOM is systematically degrading Iranian coastal anti-access assets rather than seeking a single coercive shock.

Supply-chain implication: IRGC coastal capability degradation is intended to reduce interdiction risk for US-escorted transits, but Iran's retained strike capability against Gulf state energy infrastructure (Qatar LNG, Saudi ULCC precedent) means the threat envelope extends beyond the strait itself. Gulf state force-protection posture remains elevated.

Diplomatic

Severe ↓ Confidence: Medium
  • Oman two-corridor proposal (CNN source, 11 Jul) has not been formally accepted by either party and is now effectively superseded by the US blockade reinstatement (14 Jul) — the southern corridor through Omani waters is now part of the US-declared blockade zone. No ONA confirmation of revised proposal.
  • Iran FM Araghchi posted on X in response to Trump's 20% toll: "20% is of course too much. We will be fair" — framing Iran as the legitimate Hormuz authority and implicitly haggling over fee terms. This is classified as a Soft-tier signal of residual negotiating posture, not a Hard diplomatic event (CBS News, NPR).
  • Pakistan PM called Saudi FM: "deep concern at escalation"; "no one's interest served." Qatar FM called Jordanian and Emirati counterparts. No firm US–Iran direct session date. Iran continues to condition talks on "prior US Hormuz compliance" — a circular precondition as the US disputes Iranian authority (CBS News).

Why it matters: The Araghchi X-post is the sole current diplomatic signal and should be read as a negotiating position rather than an offer. The circular precondition structure (Iran: comply first; US: there is nothing to comply with) has not changed since Day 133. The blockade reinstatement eliminates the practical basis for the Oman two-corridor proposal in its current form. The Tehran memorial ceremony on 15 July is the next meaningful gate — if Mojtaba appears, it signals leadership continuity and potentially reopens a back-channel; his continued absence deepens IRGC-dominance read.

Supply-chain implication: No near-term diplomatic resolution is signalled by any Hard-tier source. Supply-chain planners should not price in a negotiated reopening before EOD 18 July. The 20% toll declaration, even if not implemented, adds legal and commercial uncertainty to any Hormuz-adjacent contract repricing.

Brent at $84 is still below the $88 ceiling (no Kharg strike yet), but the trajectory is upward with the W4 catalyst (Kharg, EOD 19 Jul) unresolved.

The 20% toll, if implemented, adds a ~$1.5–2.5/bbl effective cost on top of war-risk premiums. Long-tenor contract repricing should assume no Hormuz normalisation before Q4 2026 at earliest.

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Cape of Good Hope mandatory for all non-dark major carriers; transit premium ~+14 days and 30–40% freight cost vs pre-crisis Hormuz baseline. Salalah (Oman) and Jeddah (Saudi) congestion building as transshipment demand concentrates. No new capacity constraints reported but buffers thinning.
  • Bab al-Mandeb / Red Sea: UKMTO skiff gunfire 5 Jul (30 nm SW Hodeidah, bulk carrier, crew safe, no claim; ABC News/CNBC). Houthi "ban" on Israeli-linked ships active since 8 Jun. EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extended to Feb 2027. No full Houthi commercial resumption confirmed as of 06:00 UTC 14 Jul. Threat elevated by IRGC multi-state barrage (Musandam strike on Oman precedent — mediator-state targeting) which signals Houthi activation permission environment.
  • Signal against trend: EUNAVFOR ASPIDES extension and continued Saudi Yanbu operations provide a stabilised Red Sea corridor for non-Israeli-linked vessels. Houthi campaign has not formally resumed despite 11 weeks of escalation at Hormuz — suggesting deliberate restraint or capability constraint.

Why it matters: The Bab al-Mandeb / Red Sea route remains the only alternative to Cape rerouting for time-sensitive Asian–European energy flows. A full Houthi resumption (W5, EOD 16 Jul, P=0.30) would trigger dual-chokepoint crisis, Cape route saturation, and immediate FM exposure on long-tenor LNG/container contracts. The current UKMTO posture (elevated caution, no closure) is the single largest structural buffer in the resilience picture.

Supply-chain implication: Maintain Cape routing as primary. Retain Red Sea as contingency only for non-Israeli-linked, non-US-flagged vessels with EUNAVFOR escort eligibility. Do not price in Red Sea normalisation. Monitor W5 (Houthi resumption) daily through EOD 16 Jul.

Scenario A requires reversal of all four conditions simultaneously — assessed at near-zero probability over a 30-day horizon. The 30-day structural threat read is 5/Crisis with no downgrade signal on the horizon.

Supply-chain implication: Plan for Scenario C as base case through Q3 2026. Alt-route capacity buffers (Cape, Yanbu, Salalah) are the only operational margin. Inventory build where possible above baseline. No Hormuz normalisation before structural political settlement — not signalled by any Hard-tier source within 30 days.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
