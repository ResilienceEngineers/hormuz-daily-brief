01

Maritime / Hormuz access

Severe → Conf: High
  • Windward AI (14 May): 17 vessels transited Hormuz — 12 inbound, 5 outbound — vs ~130/day pre-war baseline (~13% on best recent day; month average ~5%). PGSA toll/approval model fully operational; IRGC routing via Larak Island Iranian-coastline corridor.
  • Kharg Island east terminal: first loading activity logged 14 May after days dormant; 18 tankers in port waiting area as of 06:43 UTC; STAR LINE (Curaçao-flagged crude, IMO 9237618) on-site since 5 April. Loading restart suggests staged re-entry into export rotation.
  • IRGC small-craft posture: ~333 craft across three concentrations on NE-axis push as of 14 May (Windward AI). Royal Navy of Oman Al-Ofouq and Al Bushra patrol vessels at Musandam forward facility — can reach Hormuz shoulder in ~1 hour. Norwegian Maritime Authority MARSEC Level 3 in force for Norwegian-flag Hormuz vessels.

Why it matters: The Kharg loading restart after dormancy is operationally significant — it indicates Iran is staging export capacity for potential use, whether as negotiating signal or revenue recovery. The 18-tanker queue suggests Iran anticipates a near-term loading window. Simultaneously, the IRGC small-craft NE-axis concentration is consistent with an anti-escort posture, not a stand-down.

Implication: Fujairah OPL zone and any Hormuz-adjacent routing must be treated as active interdiction risk regardless of PGSA approval status. Any resumption of Project Freedom without Gulf-state airspace clearance would immediately trigger IRGC small-craft response. War-risk P&I re-rating required for any non-PGSA-approved transit.

Sources: Windward AI (14 May 2026) — Tier 3 firm; UKMTO 057/058/26 (14 May) — Tier 1; IMO Council statement (May 2026) — Tier 1; Lloyd's List Intelligence / PGSA reporting — Tier 3; Norwegian Maritime Authority MARSEC advisory — Tier 2.

Diplomatic track

Concerning ↑ Conf: High
  • Supreme Leader Khamenei (16 May, Wikipedia/Reuters corroborated): publicly rejected Trump's 16 May nuclear proposal as "excessive and outrageous," accused Trump of lying about seeking peace, declared him "unworthy of a response," and reiterated Israel as a "cancerous tumour that must be uprooted." This is the highest-authority Iranian rejection to date — Supreme Leader tier, not FM or parliamentary spokesperson.
  • Iran–Europe Istanbul talks (16 May): Iran's FM Araghchi held nuclear talks with European powers in Istanbul on same day as Khamenei rejection, warning that reinstating UN sanctions (expired Oct 2025) could lead to "irreversible" consequences. Parallel European track may be Iran's hedge against bilateral failure.
  • Trump/Xi summit (13–15 May): Trump confirmed Xi offered to "help" but Trump did NOT ask Xi to pressure Iran on Hormuz. Chinese FM readout: no mention of Iran. Bloomberg: Hormuz oil flows "creep higher" as more supertankers exit — Chinese vessels benefiting from IRGC selective access (CSIS: Chinese-operated shipping top of the 187-vessel transit list). Signal against trend: European Istanbul channel is a diplomatic signal that Iran is not fully closing all tracks.

Why it matters: Khamenei's statement closes the Shamkhani-FM contradiction identified on Day 77–78 and unifies the Iranian leadership signal at the top tier. The factional incoherence signal that was previously a risk-amplifier is now resolved — in the direction of rejection. The European Istanbul track suggests Iran is seeking internationalisation of pressure (via sanctions clock) rather than bilateral concession, a distinct strategic posture.

Implication: The 14-point MOU framework is effectively dead as a near-term instrument. Next diplomatic nodes: (1) US response to Khamenei rejection by EOD 18 May; (2) whether Araghchi's FM channel produces any written counter separate from Khamenei's public statement; (3) European leverage via UN sanctions clock — Iran's "irreversible consequences" warning is the opening bid on the E3 track.

Sources: Wikipedia US–Iran negotiations (updated 1 day ago, Khamenei 16 May entry) — Tier 4 lead confirmed by Reuters/Bloomberg headlines; UK HoC Library CBP-10637 (13 May update) — Tier 2; Bloomberg/Yahoo Finance Trump/Xi readout (15 May) — Tier 2; Al Jazeera live coverage — Tier 2.

The $170m/day revenue loss creates systemic pressure on Iranian leadership to accept some framework, but Khamenei's rejection suggests political cost of capitulation exceeds short-term revenue pain.

Implication: Maintain coverage against $120–130 Brent scenario in re-escalation path. IEA undersupply persists to October regardless of diplomatic outcome — energy premium is structural, not event-driven. No immediate attribution requirement for Kharg slick (dissipating per prior assessment); deliberate release remains worst-case monitor on Kharg given capacity pressure.

Alternative routes

Elevated → Conf: Medium

ICG expert Michael Hanna (The National, Mar 2026): "structured pause under tension" — Iranian strategic patience, not capability degradation. Skuld: Houthi retains aerial drones, naval drones, missiles; misidentification risk for US/Israel-linked vessels persists. Risk Intelligence: threat highest for Israel-linked, elevated for US/UK-linked.
  • Red Sea volumes ~40% below 2023 baseline. Suez Canal at ~18–19% of pre-disruption eastbound share (S&P Global). Cape of Good Hope structural default for Asia–Europe; industry consensus expects diversions through at least 2027. French carrier Charles de Gaulle transited Suez 6 May en route to southern Red Sea for contingency escort posture — distinct from other military operations per Macron office.
  • SUMED operational. Saudi East–West and Habshan–Fujairah pipelines saturated. Jebel Ali (Dubai) congested from diverted vessels. GPS/AIS/VHF interference persistent around Hormuz approaches (MARAD 2026-008, JMIC). Mina Al Fahal (Oman): Doppler log required for berthing — GPS interference operationally significant.

Why it matters: The Houthi pause is the single most valuable supply-chain buffer in current routing posture. Its conditionality on Iran-led axis decisions means it can be dissolved on 24–72h notice. The 16 May Khamenei rejection increases the probability of US kinetics — and the probability of Houthi resumption. The French carrier in southern Red Sea positioning is the most significant allied signal of willingness to enforce Bab al-Mandeb passage if Houthis resume.

Implication: Do not treat Red Sea as a viable routing option for time-sensitive cargo. Treat Cape routing as structural. If Houthi pause breaks, expect immediate P&I re-rating and effective full-route closure — adding the Bab al-Mandeb disruption on top of Hormuz closure is a scenario where container and LNG capacity constraints become acute within 30 days.

Removed all ambiguity from prior Shamkhani/FM contradiction. The 14-point MOU framework is effectively dead as a near-term instrument.
  • EOD 18 May deadline is the next hard binary: Trump has extended five times before; a sixth extension would confirm coercive exhaustion.   • Signal against trend: European Istanbul track (16 May) and UN sanctions clock (October 2026 reinstatement deadline) create a slow-burn pressure that could revive negotiations after an EOD 18 May extension — but this is a weeks-to-months track, not a 72h de-escalation signal.

Why it matters: Re-escalation is now the structural modal scenario for the first time since the tracker launched. The probability shift reflects not a single event but the convergence of: Supreme Leader rejection (diplomatic track closed), EOD 18 May deadline (decision forced), Trump credibility cliff (sixth extension cost), and Gulf-state airspace restriction (Project Freedom execution constrained). Frozen attrition remains possible if Trump extends, but probability has decayed below 40% for first time.

Implication: Supply chains should be planned against Scenario C as the base operating assumption for Q3 2026. Scenario B (frozen attrition) is the secondary hedge. Scenario A (negotiated framework) is not a 30-day planning assumption. Defining observable for Scenario C confirmation: Trump announces resumption of strikes or Project Freedom restart before EOD 18 May without prior Iranian written counter-proposal accepted.

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
