As of 06:00 UTC 14 June (Day 107), the US–Iran Islamabad Declaration stands on the cusp of formal signature. Pakistan PM Sharif and its Foreign Ministry confirmed an electronic signing ceremony was scheduled for Sunday 15 June; four US Air Force C-17s pre-positioned equipment in Europe for VP Vance's anticipated attendance in Geneva. Trump stated the deal would "immediately" reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson Baghaei said the signing "will not be on Sunday" — a date hedge, not a deal rejection — while FM Araghchi said a "coming days" signing remained possible. ISW assessed growing elite consensus in Tehran around the MOU framework, though Iranian hardliners (Kayhan editor, Qom MP) publicly challenged the Hormuz reopening concession as strategically premature. In the Red Sea, Houthi forces struck M/V Verbena with two anti-ship cruise missiles on 13 June, confirming the dual-chokepoint threat persists structurally independent of US–Iran negotiations.

01

Maritime Security

Severe → Confidence: High
  • PortWatch recorded 2 Hormuz transits on 7 Jun (latest published data) vs 94/day pre-crisis baseline; 287 vessels anchored or stopped in Persian Gulf region; 1,550+ vessels stranded since March closure.
  • Houthi forces struck M/V Verbena (Palauan-flagged, Ukrainian-owned bulk carrier) in Gulf of Aden on 13 Jun with two anti-ship cruise missiles — both hit, fires aboard, one mariner severely injured. CENTCOM subsequently destroyed one Houthi USV and two patrol boats.
  • Hormuz mine threat remains active — IRGC deployed sea mines post-28 Feb; draft MOU includes 30-day reopening clause but no verification mechanism or inspection protocol named in text.

Assessment: Dual chokepoint closure remains the structural baseline. Hormuz physical reopening is conditional on mine-clearance (weeks) + IRGC rescission (political) + MARAD/UKMTO safe-corridor advisory — not achievable on day of MOU signature alone. Red Sea Houthi campaign is structurally independent and continues at confirmed attack tempo.

Implication: Cape-only routing through at minimum Q3 2026. Do not pre-position spot capacity back through either corridor on political signal alone. War-risk insurance premium will lag physical reopening by 14–21 days minimum. Jebel Ali congestion surge is the first observable of a Wave 3 reopening crunch.

Sources: PortWatch/straits.live (Tier 2) 7 Jun; Wilson Center Houthi timeline (Tier 2) 13 Jun; CENTCOM (Tier 1) 13 Jun; NBC News tracker (Tier 2) updated 3 Jun; carraglobe.com (Tier 3) May 2026; CSIS (Tier 2) Jun 2026.

Military Situation

  • IRGC formal closure declaration for Hormuz remains in force — no rescission as of 06:00 UTC 14 Jun. ISW (13 Jun) assessed Araghchi's MOU account aligns with IRGC-affiliated media, indicating growing Iranian military establishment acceptance of framework terms.

Assessment: Military posture on both sides has shifted from active exchange to pre-agreement holding pattern. US C-17 Geneva logistics is the strongest observable of US operationalising a deal outcome rather than a military option. IRGC alignment with MOU framework text (ISW assessment) reduces probability of military spoiler from within Iran's security apparatus — but Supreme Leader public commitment remains absent.

Implication: P(3rd US wave by EOD 17 Jun) now 0.05. P(IRGC GCC terminal strike by EOD 17 Jun) now 0.08. Military tail risk shifts from active kinetics to IRGC non-compliance with Hormuz reopening after a signed MOU — the enforcement gap in the current text.

Sources: Axios (Tier 1) 12–13 Jun; ISW (Tier 1) 13 Jun via Iran Intl liveblog; AFP via CBS liveblog (Tier 1) 13 Jun; AP/PBS (Tier 1) baseline.

Alternative Routes

Severe → Confidence: High
  • Houthi forces struck M/V Verbena (Palauan-flagged, Ukrainian-owned, Polish-operated bulk carrier) on 13 Jun with two anti-ship cruise missiles in Gulf of Aden — both missiles hit, fires reported, one mariner severely injured. CENTCOM response: destroyed one Houthi USV and two Houthi patrol boats same day (Wilson Center confirmed).
  • Suez Canal throughput ~26 ships/week vs ~80 pre-crisis baseline — Red Sea/Suez route operating at approximately 33% of pre-crisis capacity; UNSC Res. 2812 extends Houthi reporting to 15 Jul 2026. Houthi ban on Israel-linked ships active since 8 Jun.
  • Jebel Ali (UAE) experiencing significant congestion from stranded Gulf tonnage; Cape of Good Hope is the only viable commercial alternative through Q3. Asia-to-Europe container freight rates elevated. 13 tankers made it through Hormuz since March 4 per CSIS — mostly dark fleet, Iranian-linked, or non-hostile flag states.

Assessment: The Verbena strike is the 5th confirmed Houthi vessel attack in the 12–14 Jun window (following ASBM launch 12 Jun and prior Norderney/MSC Tavvishi cluster 8–9 Jun). Houthi operational tempo is structurally independent of US–Iran MOU — campaign linked to Lebanon unresolved and ban on Israel-linked shipping. Even a signed MOU will not immediately restore Red Sea/Suez routing. 72h Houthi attack base rate maintained at 0.55.

Implication: Cape-only freight posture through Q3 minimum. Dual-chokepoint closure means there is no viable fallback routing if Cape capacity is disrupted. Houthi threat to Red Sea will only diminish if: (1) Lebanon ceasefire becomes durable AND (2) Houthis receive direct diplomatic engagement on Yemen — neither is near-term. Treat Suez as structurally unavailable for supply-chain planning through EOY 2026.

Sources: Wilson Center Houthi timeline (Tier 2) 13 Jun; CENTCOM (Tier 1) 13 Jun; UNSC Res. 2812 (Tier 1); CSIS analysis (Tier 2) Jun 2026; carraglobe.com (Tier 3) May 2026; MARAD advisory 2026-006 (Tier 1).

Methodology

This brief uses a tiered signal-weighting framework. Only verified physical events from primary sources can move the threat assessment; trend movement requires confirmed change versus the prior 72h. The system continuously calibrates against outcomes — when predictions miss, the underlying heuristics are sharpened. The full operating ruleset is kept private to preserve analytical edge against adversarial reading.

Sources

Inputs span six tiers, weighted by track record. Specific outlets and platforms are not disclosed publicly.

  • Tier 1 — Primary & official. Maritime authorities, defense commands, foreign ministries, treasury sanctions desks. First-party communiqués that count as Hard signal.
  • Tier 2 — Specialized commercial intelligence. Vessel-tracking platforms (AIS-based), aircraft-tracking platforms (ADS-B), tanker-flow analytics, energy-cargo intelligence, piracy-reporting bureaus. Operational data with measurable track records.
  • Tier 3 — Analytical institutions. Geopolitical and security think tanks with rigorous publication standards and demonstrable calibration history on this region.
  • Tier 4 — Commercial analysts & columnists. Independent practitioners and commodity-desk analysts whose published positions can be back-tested over multi-year horizons.
  • Tier 5 — Wire services & reputable regional press. Western wire services, top-tier business and political dailies, regional papers across the Gulf, Israel, and Iran (read with editorial-line caveats), and analytical defense-and-policy publications.
  • Tier 6 — Excluded by default. Anonymous OSINT accounts, op-eds, social media, "sources say" without attribution, AI-generated summaries, forum content. Enter the brief only after independent corroboration by Tier 1–3.
